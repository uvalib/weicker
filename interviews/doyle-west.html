Overview of Lowell Weicker’s years in Congress

J. Doyle: Thank you, Phyllis. I think it’s important as we start out to look at Lowell Weicker’s history in the Congress. He was, of course, a congressman from ’68 to ’71, and then he was elected to the United States Senate. From 1971 to 1981, he was in the minority, and that’s worth noting because minority members of either house of Congress have a whole lot less to do with what happens than the majority members. In fact, it’s my view that there was at least an even chance that had the Republicans not gained control in ’81, Weicker might not have run for re-election in ’82. He was very frustrated by being in the minority.

From ’81 to ’87, Weicker was in the majority. Even though his term ended at the end of ’88, he was back in the minority again for the last two years, so the part of his record that was the most productive, not surprisingly, was when he had the authority and the responsibility to do something. Now, it’s a credit to him that he took advantage of that, but that kind of causes us to focus on the years ’81 to ’87, I would suggest, as the richest times in terms of production for any of the things that he did. I’m going to speak to disability policy, but there were certainly many others that you’re aware of.

Weicker came to become chairman of the Subcommittee on the Handicapped quite by accident. He already had had three committee assignments in the Senate, and Bob Stafford, the then Senator from Vermont, prevailed on him to join the Labor & Human Resources Committee, the full committee, in order to give him, Stafford, and the Democrats a working majority.

If one of the more conservative members had accepted this spot Weicker ultimately took, it would’ve tilted the balance to a tie that the then chairman Orrin Hatch could’ve manipulated, so it was Bob Stafford that talked Weicker into being a member of the Labor & Human Resources Committee, his fourth committee assignment. Three were the rule, or typical; four was unusual. And then because of his seniority in the Senate, Weicker was given the Handicap Subcommittee. But both those things are a function of Bob Stafford having convinced him to do it.

Elsewhere in this dialogue I’ll talk a little bit about what that majority meant. Bob Stafford, Lowell Weicker, and the seven Democrats led by Ted Kennedy completely rewrote the rules of the Labor & Human Resources Committee, frustrated the [Ronald] Reagan administration’s attempts to gut social policy, and yielded a pretty good result for disability, too. That’s getting ahead of the game, but it’s important to know that.

Funding for the Handicapped during Reagan years

J. Doyle: Let’s start by characterizing those years 1981 to 1987. The first two coincided with the first two years of the Reagan administration, and Reagan came up with a novel concept. He used the Budget Reconciliation Process, the process of Congress adopting a budget, okay? He used that process in an attempt to do two things: one, cut spending. Big surprise. You knew that.

Leffler: Right.

J. Doyle: Two, he used it to repeal the categorical legislation in many human service programs. Of particular interest to us, let’s say, is special ed. The law began in the 1970s with the public law known as Public Law 94-142. All right. Reagan proposed repeal of that law. He called it a “block grant,” but that was just a nice way to describe repeal, and the 94-142 monies would go into this big pot, reduced 25 percent, and given to the states to spend any way they want.

So as Weicker said more than a few times, it was sort of like the animals fighting for the crumbs that fell off the table at the state capitols. Weicker frustrated that and stopped it, but it’s important to understand that this term “block grant” means repeal of the undergirding categorical legislation, as it’s called. The business of using the Budget Reconciliation Process was both novel, and in many areas largely effective, but not in the disability area.

I said those were the first two years. During that time, we had to make sure that we: one, established coalitions of disability organizations and advocates; and, two, held them together. I’ll have something to say about the latter.

It was no time to disrupt the disability movement by introducing new concepts that might offend one or the other, nor was there much chance that anything like that would pass. Nineteen eighty-one and 1982 involved, on the Senator’s part, herculean efforts, in my view, that were successful, that avoided the cuts, made some modest increases actually in funding, but preserved the undergirding legislation, the three biggest for Weicker being special ed, vocational rehab, and the Developmental Disabilities Act.

Nineteen eighty-three to ’87, but ’83 and ’84 in particular, another congressional process came along called “reauthorization.” The non-entitlement programs of the Congress go on forever unless somebody changes them. The others—let’s take one you’d be familiar with now—the Higher Education Act. Periodically, every three to four to five years, whatever, it comes up for what they called reauthorization, which means the law sunsets unless the Congress renews it, and typically that calls for a re-examination of the bill, how it can be improved, what should be done, how might the funding levels be adjusted, yadda yadda, anything that goes to the authorizing process of the Congress.

In 1983 we had the opportunity to take a look at reauthorizing, renewing, extending, the special ed law, the aforementioned 94-142, now safe from the block grants. Reagan gave up on in ’83 the Vocational and Rehabilitation Act. Reagan also gave up on the block grant, and the Developmental Disabilities Program, a much smaller initiative, but one that dates back to the [John F.] Kennedy era actually in terms of addressing at the state level the needs of people particularly with what is now called intellectual disabilities and what was then called mental retardation.

So we sort of coined a phrase that we were going to take a look at these laws not with the idea as they had been in the past of a professional being at the center of a circle, but from the client as being at the center of the circle, and we were going to array the professional services around the client as opposed to the other way around. This didn’t win us any friends. Jane, as I can remember, the Council for Exceptional Children, the state directors of special education or other folks, who were legitimate and valuable parts of that coalition I mentioned in the first two years, they all took it like good people, more or less. And there were a host of things that we did to put some flesh on the bones of that client-centered notion.

For instance, we knew there was a disparity between the educational establishment and particularly the special ed establishment and K-12 administrators and parents, the most egregious being that oftentimes school systems would cap what are called related services, PT [physical therapy]/OT [occupational therapy]/speech and the like, for disabled kids and just tell the parents there was no more. Parents had little or no recourse largely because they didn’t know the bill, the law. I could tell you an anecdote, but for the moment I won’t, about how I was made aware of that very painfully in a very well-to-do, well educated, and economically very well off community in Connecticut.
