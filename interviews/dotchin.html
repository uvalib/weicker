0:00:00

Leffler: Today is May 17, 2011. This is Phyllis Leffler. I’m in the offices of Robert Dotchin in Washington, D.C., and we’re here to do an interview for the Lowell Weicker Oral History Project. Thank you for being here and for doing this with us.

R. Dotchin: A pleasure.

Leffler: I thought I might start out by just asking you to give us a little bit of your personal background, what brought you into the halls of Congress when you decided to do so, obviously a number of years ago now for all of us.

R. Dotchin: Now, yes. I’d be more than happy to do that. Actually, my mother was a chief clerk for the legislative management committee in the State assembly in the State of Connecticut. She worked there for 30-plus years, and during the course of her tenure, she met a young, then State representative who was trying to learn his way around. She kind of showed him a little bit of the roadmap, and that was Lowell Weicker.

Leffler: And your mother’s name?

R. Dotchin: Flavia Dotchin.

Leffler: Flavia.

R. Dotchin: She was one of the gals who knew where all the bodies were buried and how the process really worked. She thought Weicker was an up-and-comer and tried to help him out. They developed a good working relationship. I always had the political bug as well. I came down and ran an intern program for the Senator during the end of my senior year at the University of Connecticut. The intern program entailed bringing high school juniors and seniors for a set program during the course of the summer. I did that and then I went back to graduate school.

0:01:57

Dotchin joins Weicker’s Senate staff

R. Dotchin: Following graduate school, there was an opening on his legislative staff, and so in January of 1973 I joined the Senator’s legislative operation. He was then a freshman Senator. As you remember, in 1970, after only one term in the House of Representatives, Weicker and Tom Meskill flipped a coin, and one ran for Governor and one ran for Senate. They both won. He was then a relatively unknown moderate-to-conservative Senator from the State of Connecticut, and I joined his team two years into that tenure.

That was, parenthetically, right before February of 1973 when Hugh Scott selected Weicker to be on the Watergate Committee. I think that was probably one of the more defining moments, and it jettisoned a freshman Senator, an obscure Senator, into the national limelight. I was close enough to that process to observe and learn, and that began a 16-year career with the Senator.

Leffler: So, you said you were always a bit of a political junkie. Did you actually study politics in college?

R. Dotchin: Yes. I was a political science major, a double major, and did graduate work in political science as well. I think part of that was probably influenced by my mother’s relationships with the State Legislature, her long tenure there. During my college years, I did an internship in the house assembly in the State of Connecticut.

Leffler: And you said you joined the staff by getting involved in running an internship program?

R. Dotchin: Right.

Leffler: So he actually asked you to do that for the summer before you had any direct experience?

R. Dotchin: Yes, exactly.

Leffler: He took a risk. [laughs]

R. Dotchin: It was more or less trying to herd cats, trying to make sure the kids didn’t run off the rails, and they learned a lot. We were using the Senator’s relationships and influence. We set up a fairly structured week program and we did this for about six weeks during the summer, where a different group would come in each week, and during the course of that week, Senators, congressmen, people from the administration would come in, sit down, talk to them and lecture them and get interaction from them. I learned from that experience as well. It further enhanced my desire to come back after I did graduate work at the University of Connecticut to try to hopefully get a more permanent position, which in fact did happen.

Leffler: And as a young staffer, can you remember anything about the initial interview process?

R. Dotchin: I remember it distinctly. The most interesting thing I remember about that interviewing process was that I was hoping to at least get $10,000 a year, and Lowell said no, $9,600 would be as far as we’re going to go.

Leffler: [laughs]

R. Dotchin: So that one I remembered. I was a known commodity to him then, so it was not so much I had to prove myself. I guess I did that during the summer before, but that’s the one that still sticks with me to this day.

It was a small, lean staff, because remember, he was a freshman. He wasn’t on any major committees where he had staff. Our legislative staff was three or four people. We had to cover the waterfront, and that was the time there was no television. You didn’t even have the squawk box. So the only way you could find out what was going on in the Senate was to go up in the gallery and observe, take notes, and then when the vote took place, you would run down the stairs, stand by the elevator, and when the Senator came out, you grabbed him and you had 20 seconds to distill what the debate was about as he went into the chamber to vote.

But it was also a great learning experience because you learned the Senate, its rules, its procedures. One of the great things I observed was the Rule 22 debate, and that was the debate that took place really between James Allen and Robert C. Byrd. They argued whether we should change the rules as they relate to the cloture and the filibuster. If you look back in the history, those types of debates have not taken place in quite a while. The post-cloture filibuster evolved through James Allen, who was a wily old Alabaman Senator who was steeped in the knowledge of the rules and traditions of the Senate, as was Mr. Byrd. As a young staffer observing that, you learned a lot about the Senate and how it operated.

Leffler: Of course. How did you get involved in the particular issues that became your focus?

R. Dotchin: Well, in 1973-74 when I first came in, Geoff Baker, Searle Field, and I were the entire operation. Your focus usually is dictated by the legislator’s committee assignments. At that point, I think he was on the Banking Committee and the Commerce Committee. That was where you spent your principal legislative efforts during the day.

There was a big housing bill in 1974 I worked on, but what happened more than anything else is that once he was on the Watergate Committee, that became an all-consuming operation, and he enhanced his staff. He brought Bill Shure and brought Bill Wickens in to add to his legal team, investigative team. Dick McGowan was his press secretary. Dick used to cover the [John F.] Kennedy-[Lyndon B.] Johnson years for the Daily News. He knew his way around and knew almost every major reporter who was covering the Watergate. As a matter of fact, Lesley Stahl was a cub reporter at the time.

In any event, that team was engaged, and it consumed most of Weicker’s time and energy because it became such a monumental challenge and a constitutional crisis. So the two people left behind but close enough to observe that were myself and Geoff Baker. We had to handle his committee assignments, tell him what was going on on the floor, and that sort of thing. We made sure that his base was covered and he was fully prepared to deal with those issues as well as the all-consuming Watergate issue.

Leffler: So that sort of pulled you into—

0:08:41

Post-Watergate reforms

R. Dotchin: And that pulled me in. In ’75, post-Watergate, I became legislative director. As such, I took over the entire legislative operation, and we were very much engaged in those last two years prior to his first election against Gloria Schaffer. We did a lot of what I called post-Watergate reform, including campaign finance reform. Political Action Committees were in fact a Watergate reform.

The other major thing we did was try to deal with one of the big abuses that came out of Watergate: how tax return information was used. Weicker said that the IRS [Internal Revenue Service] became a lending library and became a political tool used against its enemies. Well, Weicker working with then Jerry Litton of Missouri from the House side jointly put together a major initiative. That legislation became law. It tightened up who could get access to tax return information, its terms, conditions, and so forth. That’s Section 6103 of the tax code.

Leffler: My goodness.

R. Dotchin: I remember that because I was deeply engaged in that process, and it was a fascinating project.

That was probably one of the best times for Weicker, because Jerry Ford, a very close personal friend, became President during those last two years. He put together I think one of the great Cabinets of our time. Ed Levy at the Department of Justice was terrific. Carla Hills was at the Department of HUD [Housing and Urban Development] and then Dick Cheney was Chief of Staff. [Donald] Rumsfeld, I think, was Department of Defense at the time. Ford put together a staff and a group that reflected, I think, Lowell’s view to this day of what the Republican Party should be. It was a very cooperative relationship. We worked directly with the President and got some major reforms, the Watergate reforms, done in those two years since—after the Watergate Committee ran its course. Obviously, in August of ’74, [Richard] Nixon resigned.

Leffler: When you say you worked directly with the President, can you explain what that means?

R. Dotchin: Well, there was a classic photo of Weicker sitting down with President Ford and his team as he presented what he wanted to do legislatively to tighten the IRS code as it relates to the disclosure of tax information. He laid out his ideas to the President’s team. We can propose, we can dispose, but the President’s got to sign the bill. He signed onto that process and it eventually became law. A direct personal involvement meeting with the President did take place in ’75 to get this issue on his radar screen and get him fully engaged.

Leffler: Right. Now, in terms of the Small Business Administration Committee work, Lowell Weicker joins that as a minority in the Senate.

R. Dotchin: A quick story. It’s a fascinating story. In 1976, when Weicker was running for re-election, he was just on the Committee. Jack Javits was the ranking Republican.

Leffler: And I think he says in his book that Jack Javits asked him if he would—

R. Dotchin: And what happened was that because of internal machinations of the Senate, Javits had to leave the Committee. Two other people that were ahead of Lowell in seniority, J. Glenn Beall and Bill Brock, both got defeated, and so lo and behold we’re preparing—

Leffler: I’m sorry. J. who?

R. Dotchin: J. Glenn Beall. I think he since died, and also Bill Brock of Tennessee.

Leffler: Right.

R. Dotchin: Beall was from Maryland. They were both ahead of Weicker in seniority, so we were fully prepared to probably leave the Committee and move on to other committees as we looked ahead after he’d won in 1977.

0:12:36

Weicker becomes ranking Republican on Small Business Committee

R. Dotchin: Then lo and behold, we wake up, as he calls it. Sometimes it’s like being in the military. You’ve suddenly got an assignment. You train to do X and suddenly you’re doing Y. And so he became the ranking Republican on the Small Business Committee in 1977. I was legislative director at that time. He asked me to go up there, put a staff together, and be his staff director for all the Republicans on the Committee. We put together an agenda working then with the chairman of the Committee, who was Gaylord Nelson.

I decided to do that, as I thought it was a new challenge for me. So in 1977 I went up there and was fully engaged in that process until the ’80 election. Since I’d been in the Senate from ’73 to ’80, Republicans had always been in the minority. In November 1980, we woke up one day and said, “Oh, my God, we’ve got to run this place.” That was a fun challenge.

In 1981 we took over the Committee and put together the entire staff, a legislative agenda, and moved forward rather vigorously. Remember, in that 1981 timeframe Weicker was bumping up to a very tough re-election in 1982 with then Congressman Toby Moffett. He had to hit the ground running. In that time period between November of that election in 1980 until January, we fully staffed. We ramped up from a staff of four or five as a minority to a staff of 25. It was an exciting time. We put together a hell of a team. We hit the ground running and we did a number of things in that first year and a half during his chairmanship prior to his election in ’82.

Leffler: You’re suddenly in the majority, but you’re also battling, not maybe initially but after a little while, battling a President who doesn’t really—and particularly with small businesses.

R. Dotchin: Yes, with small business issues. Well, it was funny. When we first took over the committee, the first thing, and one of the most important things we did institutionally, was change the status of the Small Business Committee. It was a select committee. Gaylord Nelson never dealt with the issue head-on. They used to sometimes drop the “Select” off their letterhead, but in fact, it was still institutionally a select committee. By that, you mean it could be a temporary committee. There was no permanency to it. So one of the first things Weicker did working with then Majority Leader Howard Baker was to make the Small Business Committee a permanent, full standing committee in the United States Senate with a change in the Senate rules. That was done, and it was an appropriate statement to make to the small business constituency, saying, “Yes, this is an important constituency and it’s as important as Veterans Affairs and some of the other committees.” We got that change, and that was an important change, which obviously remains to this day.

The ranking Democrat was Sam Nunn from Georgia, and we worked well with him. For all these Senators, the Small Business Committee was their third or fourth committee assignment. They’re on Appropriations, Armed Services, Foreign Relations, and other committees and Small Business, so we always had to be cognizant of that fact and encourage people on both sides of the aisle to become involved. Small business tends to be much more of a bipartisan issue. We encouraged them to engage on issues that they cared about. We tried to foster Senators’ interest if they were legitimate issues and encourage field hearings and the like.

0:16:39

Major tax bill signed into law by Reagan

R. Dotchin: In 1981, if you may remember, when [Ronald] Reagan came in the economy was in the doldrums, much like when [Barack] Obama came in. The big issue of the day was what to do to jumpstart the economy. Reagan came up with a major tax proposal to cut taxes. Sam Nunn and Weicker responded. We held hearings on Wall Street and around the country. The hearings laid out a legislative package. We got a lot of recommendations of what should be done for the small business sector to encourage capital formation, to reduce their cost of doing business, thereby allowing them to create jobs and begin an economic recovery. That we achieved and we introduced a major bill. Nunn and Weicker did not have jurisdiction over tax matters, but they did have a broad mandate to investigate matters of interest to small business.

In 1981 the biggest issue was the economy, jobs, and taxes. Weicker and Nunn testified before the Finance Committee on their tax bill, and many of those recommendations found their way into the final bill, which Reagan signed into law in late 1981-82, so one of our first shots out of the box was that. It was a major accomplishment, one that we felt very strong and very good about, and that was achieved in part because of the staff we put together. We got somebody from Jacob Javits’s staff, an economist named Jim O’Connell, who was brilliant, who helped us put together the legislation, the hearings, and the like. It was a lot of Jim’s genius working directly with Weicker and Nunn. We were very bipartisan. That made that happen. So that was the first thing we achieved.

0:18:30

Small Business Innovation Research bill

R. Dotchin: The second major accomplishment in those first two years was that Weicker also encouraged other Senators’ involvement, partly because he was running. He got Warren Rudman engaged. Warren’s first major bill was the Small Business Innovation Research bill that was signed in June/July of 1982. It’s still going strong to this day. As a matter of fact, the Senate and the House are right now working on legislation to reauthorize the program for an additional eight years. The program requires all the agencies that have research and development budgets of over a hundred million dollars to set aside 2 percent of their budget for grants to small businesses. Small businesses would use federal funds to come up with new ideas, innovative ideas, to help move these agencies forward, consistent with their agency’s mission. It turned out to be an extremely successful program, and since 1982 billions of dollars that have been pumped out to the small business sector. I think it was really one of our stronger legacies from that particular era.

Rudman took the lead. Weicker backed him up down the line. The funny story was when we had the bill signing ceremony. You know, Weicker was not the most beloved one at the Reagan White House.

Leffler: [laughs]

R. Dotchin: But thanks to Rudman, really it was more Rudman, it was such a major accomplishment. The White House held the bill signing ceremony. The bill signing took place in the beautiful setting of the Rose Garden. The day before the Connecticut Republican convention in the state where Prescott Bush was challenging Weicker, needless to say, the visual of Weicker standing next to President Reagan at a bill signing—

Leffler: Didn’t hurt. [laughs]

R. Dotchin: Did not hurt. So it was sort of ironic. Probably they looked at it reluctantly. Since he was chairman of the committee that wrote the bill, they could not not invite him. Protocol forced them to invite Lowell, and he gladly attended.

Leffler: I think at another time for the ADA [Americans with Disabilities Act] he was consciously not invited. Is that right? Is that what I’m remembering?

R. Dotchin: Right. I think that’s correct. But that one they couldn’t get around.

Leffler: Sure.

R. Dotchin: The first couple of years were productive, and one of the other things we really wanted to do is take on the agency. The Small Business Administration had always been sort of a backwater agency.

Leffler: I was just going to—

R. Dotchin: It had always been sort of a political dumping ground for both parties, in any administration. What we wanted to do is go in and try to change that mindset. We developed an investigative capability. We hired some strong investigative reporters. They were hired to go in and dig at the agency, find out what was working and what wasn’t, and try to come up with some programmatic changes to the programs.

Leffler: You hired investigative reporters? Really?

R. Dotchin: Yes.

Leffler: So they became part of your staff, then?

R. Dotchin: They’re one of the best teams. Yes.

Leffler: Oh, interesting.

R. Dotchin: They were great talents. Marc Smolonsky, who is now one of the top people over at the Department of HHS [Health and Human Services] and Steve Snider were both people who have the blend of being great writers and investigators.

Leffler: They started that one—

R. Dotchin: They were out in the private—they were reporters, but they also knew how to do fact-finding, and once you get the data, you’ve got to assemble it, put a hearing together, and of course you want to get press attention. These capabilities were very effective. I found that to be a very good source of investigative talents.

0:22:38

Small Business set-aside program

Leffler: So I know, talking about reporting, that a number of newspapers in the earlier years of the work of the Small Business Administration did call it a backwater, just as you just did, and also there was a lot of talk about issues of fraud and corruption in the agency.

R. Dotchin: Right, and there were. There were two programs that particularly—Warren looked at that. One was the 8(a) program, which is the Small Business Set-Aside Program for businesses that are owned by socially and economically disadvantaged businesses. That’s a reference to a section of the code that created it.

Leffler: Right. 8(a).

R. Dotchin: Anyway, the Section 8(a) program. What you found is a lot of times there were front organizations, the businesses weren’t really run by a minority. They just had them up there, but they didn’t run the business, so you had those sorts of problems. The thing you wanted to do with the 8(a) program was to give people a chance to get some contracts on a no-bid basis with no competition. Then take that opportunity and use it as a business development tool, not one in which you created almost a welfare state situation.

And what we did not see enough of is a graduation out of the program. In other words, once they stopped, after X number of years, getting these contracts, they fell off the cliff. That was not the way the program should’ve worked or was conceived. That’s why in the latter end of our career working with then-Senator [Dale] Bumpers we did the last rewrite of that program. We reformed the program in ’77 and ’78, which forced graduation and had certain goal settings. We made sure that they could only have a certain percentage of their work on a declining basis in 8(a). We would wean them off nonbid status and get them into a more competitive environment.

Other reforms on more the financial side of the equation—there were problems in how we did a secondary market program where the 7(a) loan program, the largest loan program that the banks used to issue loans to small businesses that were a higher risk. Seventy to 80, maybe even 90 percent of the loans are guaranteed by the federal government. So if it goes bad, the federal government is there to back it up. The bank isn’t fully exposed. There were problems in that program that we had to clean up. From program to program we tried to peck away and make certain structural reforms. Some of those weren’t even legislative in nature.

We were making major achievements, and then they had this Jim Sanders, who I thought was one of the best administrators at SBA [Small Business Administration] developed a great working relationship, especially in 1983-84. Jim was making reforms, and we were working hand in glove to get those reforms achieved. He was also changing the mindset of the agency. There was a much better esprit de corps. There was a much better sense of worth, if you will.

0:26:04

Fighting attempts to eliminate the Small Business Administration

R. Dotchin: Then in 1985, Jim Sanders walked into a budget presentation with the President and Dave Stockman. This is a slash-and-burn budget across the board where he took on many of the social programs and whacked away at them. To show that he could do the same on the business side, he said, “Let’s eliminate the Small Business Administration outright, take a few of its programs, and put them over in the Department of Commerce and call it a day.” Sanders was fit to be tied. He had the rug cut out from under him. He didn’t have a chance to even argue his case to the President, and so the issue was joined. The issue was presented in 1985 to the Senate, which unfortunately changed our agenda radically. Instead of continuing in a productive way and trying to improve the agency, plus do some broad-based advocacy work, we were now engaged in a major fight.

One of the biggest procurers of goods and services is the federal government. How can a small business get in there and play and get the playing field so it’s not so tilted against them? We reformed the procurement process in a number of different ways. In a number of other areas we continued to advocate on behalf of small businesses. Then this came out of the blue and it became an all-consuming fight, a fight that took a good chunk of that year to play out, including some dramatic hearings when Stockman testified before Weicker at the hearings, which had national press coverage. Lowell said, “There are many reforms that can be taken, but this agency will not be eliminated on my watch. It was a quite lively hearing that day because Stockman had all his staff people behind him, he pushed them all aside, and it became almost mano-a-mano situation where Weicker and Stockman took it on, hand-to-hand.

Leffler: But you won.

R. Dotchin: We won, but it was not an easy fight. There were so many things on the table, and look what was on Weicker’s plate to protect. We totaled it up. When it was all said and done about two-and-a half to three billion dollars worth of add-ons were put back into the budget for him to get his vote. Now let’s say they didn’t need his vote. He would’ve lost the leverage and that agency may not have survived. When you look at the fights he was doing on biomedical research, on the various cuts that they were trying to take, to take on in the labor, health, his wars with the Secretary of Education Bill Bennett, all that was coming to a head in this budget fight. The Small Business Administration was part of that overall equation.

Now, with our survival, we brought to the table massive cuts to the SBA program. We did some major reforms to the programs. We did savings to the programs. We just didn’t eliminate the agency, but that became the closest time that agency ever had to extinction. The Heritage Foundation and others when a new Republican administration will come in will say, “Why don’t you get rid of the Small Business Administration?” And that’s still a consistent theme. Ironically, the major 8(a) program that is now a multi-billion dollar program, has used the Obama administration a bit as a way to boost economic recovery efforts. This agency that almost bit the dust has been relied on extremely heavily by this administration.

Leffler: Just listening to you talk about all of this, obviously, you still have incredible command of some of these details—

R. Dotchin: Yes.

Leffler: But I guess when you first got into this, would you have had any background in business yourself?

R. Dotchin: No.

Leffler: How did you learn all this? Just by doing it?

R. Dotchin: It’s almost like you got thrown into it. He threw me up there in ten feet of water to see if I survived. Part of the genius of Weicker, and I tried to emulate that, as I always tried to hire people a lot smarter than I was. I always wanted to be challenged, so the staff that we put together was what really drove the process. We had a very strong staff of people that could command—I think I had the good political instincts. I was a good generalist. I knew enough to be dangerous. But I also knew I needed to have someone who could really go a lot deeper. I hired a great procurement expert. I hired someone who—

Leffler: Who was that?

R. Dotchin: Bill Montalto was his name. He just retired after 30-plus years, but knew the intricacies of the procurement process. I also hired Kim Elliott.

Leffler: Oh, you hired Kim? Oh, interesting.

R. Dotchin: Yes. Weicker and I hired Kim, a procurement expert, and she used to work for me in 1981. One of the first things she did was the Prompt Payment Act that tried to make sure that the government paid their bills on time. Lowell, to this day, will kid her about it saying it was my first big accomplishment, but it really wasn’t, but it’s one that always stuck with him. So we always tried to bring some very strong people around us to work not only with him, but with the entire Republican delegation and the Democratic side on the committee.

The Committee was about 17 members. Senator Al D’Amato, for example, was on the Committee, and he had his set of issues. He really loved to use the Committee to go after crime issues, for example. Gaylord Nelson, when he was chairman, used the Small Business Committee to go after the drug industry. The Committee can be used as a platform to go in a variety of different directions depending upon the interest of the membership and what they wanted. That’s why I became very good at field hearings as well because there’s always a small business angle on almost anything.

0:32:37

Weicker’s strategy for building his staff

Leffler: I want to go back to Lowell Weicker’s strategies for building a staff because it seems— tell me how you would respond to this—that he was very willing to take risks in terms of hiring people who he thought were able and just throwing them into the fire, so to speak.

R. Dotchin: I think that’s true. He wanted a young, aggressive, hungry staff. The best way I can put this—one time when he became chairman of the Appropriations Committee, the old appropriations guys, they’d had these staff people that’d been there 20 years and he became chairman of the—“Here’s your staff person.” He said, “It’s not my staff person. I’m not using him.” For one year, Weicker ignored him and he brought his own people in and collectively they bore in and got to know the agency and its budgets. He worked very closely with Fritz Hollings and they did a number of things. My point is that he’s not going to be led by the nose by staff he doesn’t know. He’s going to put his own team together and collectively they’ll learn. I think that’s his general approach and philosophy. When he has an intense interest, he’ll build on that interest, as opposed to me being a generalist. He hired Bob Wicklund because of his intense interest in the oceans. When he has a strong interest, he will build on that.

Leffler: But he brought in Bob who had no understanding—

R. Dotchin: Nothing.

Leffler: —of the legislative processes. Zero.

R. Dotchin: That’s where I came in. Bob was like a bull in the china closet, he’s going to go from A to B. I know you want to get there, but it’s usually going to have to take a little bit of windy road to get there. He didn’t understand the intricacies of the legislative process, and it required sort of team effort to make sure that he didn’t self-destruct or get overly frustrated. Things didn’t get done as quickly or as cleanly as he would’ve liked. Yes, you’re right.

Leffler: Right. And I remember when I did that interview very early on with Bob Wicklund, he said that when Lowell Weicker asked him to do this work, the first thing he said to him was, “Do I need to wear shoes?” [laughter]

R. Dotchin: He was not a dresser.

Leffler: He was used to being under the water.

R. Dotchin: He used flippers. He had flippers on more than anything else, but as you know, given that subcommittee, he was able to have the leverage on NOAA [National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration] to make a difference and build quite a structure down in St. Croix.

There is a classic story where he tangled with Mac Baldrige. When he was Secretary of Commerce, Baldrige took on Weicker and NOAA. It was a war. Two Connecticut guys going head-to-head. It got to the point where when Weicker went to mark-up, Weicker zeroed out all the Secretary’s personal accounts so Mac Baldrige couldn’t even buy a pencil. That’s when the hierarchy of the Senate and cooler heads prevailed. They found an accommodation. Mac shot across his bow and Lowell returned his volley. I think Mac realized the intensity that Weicker felt toward oceans research and NOAA, and when you look at the Commerce Department, it’s sort of an odd duck.

The Commerce Department is a like a holding agency—everything from the Economic Development Agency, Minority Business Development, trade issues, export administration issues, NOAA. Most of the Commerce secretaries come in with that strong business background. They don’t know what the hell to do with this ocean program over here. Leffler: Do you have a sense of what allowed Lowell Weicker to be the maverick that he was?

R. Dotchin: Well, I think the State of Connecticut allowed him to. He didn’t get that far off the electorate. Remember, it’s a very blue state. It’s a more blue state than it was then. How many people have won statewide, Republicans, and at the federal level?

Leffler: That’s interesting.

R. Dotchin: Now, again, you had John Rowland, who was Governor for a long time, but those are different dynamics than the Senate, so I think the state allowed that sort of independence, and I think it’s like anything else, you’ve got to go back every six years, and if they think you’ve misread it, if your priorities were wrong, they’ll tell you. But I think what they like more than anything else—“Nobody’s Man but Yours” was a tagline that captured him to some extent. He was going to listen to his own drummer, but hopefully not so far off that you don’t get re-elected, and obviously that was the case.

I mean, he held every major office in the State of Connecticut, and doing that, you get a good sense of politics, of people, of the process, because from first selectman to State rep to congressman to Senator to Governor—especially going up to the Senate side, he had quite a bit of a legislative experience moving to the Senate, having been in the State house and the House of Representatives, so that’s why he understood the Senate and the legislative process pretty well.

Leffler: What were your biggest challenges in working with him and for him?

1:17:22

Challenges of working with Weicker

R. Dotchin: The biggest challenge you would have would be to get him to focus on a particular issue that you wanted to and sometimes get him not to shoot from the hip as much. Sometimes he shot from the hip. A lot of times he was right, sometimes not so right. But that was his strength, and that’s the way he operated sometimes, so that’ll be the—also, let’s say I’ve got three things I’ve got to talk to him about, but timing’s everything with him. You had to get him at the right time so that he would focus and listen. His genius was a lot of times we did some hearings and he didn’t have a chance to focus on it, and at six o’clock in the morning before the hearing at ten, I’m picking him up and having breakfast, going through the hearing, what’s it all about. He’d walk into that hearing like he’d studied it for months.

Leffler: Amazing. That’s really amazing.

R. Dotchin: So in that way, he was a very quick study, and that was his genius. But you had to be, especially as a Senator with that many balls juggling in the air. At the end, he was on the Energy Committee, he was on the Appropriations Committee, Small Business Committee, and he had three or four subcommittee assignments. All of that became quite a full plate to deal with.

Leffler: Is there anything else you want to add?

R. Dotchin: No. It just was a hell of a ride. He allowed people like myself that opportunity, and I think that’s why you see the loyalty. You’re grateful for that, and I think in a way you look back and you say, “Yes, we did make a difference.” And he was pretty cool yesterday, too.

Leffler: He was cool yesterday?

R. Dotchin: Yesterday. He said thanks to a lot of people.

Leffler: That’s wonderful. Thank you very much.

R. Dotchin: My pleasure. I hope I was somewhat instructive.

Leffler: It’s really been excellent. Thank you so much.
