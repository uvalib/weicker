0:00:00

Leffler: This is Phyllis Leffler. The date is August 12, 2010, and I’m in the offices of Kimberly Elliott for an interview in the Lowell Weicker Oral History Project. Good morning, and thank you for agreeing to meet with me about this project.

K. Elliott: It’s my pleasure.

0:00:54

Elliott’s background for work on Senate Small Business Committee

Leffler: I thought we might begin by asking you to recollect how it was that you got involved with Lowell Weicker, and perhaps as well, just tell us a little bit about your educational background that led you to seek the position that you ultimately ended up with.

K. Elliott: I originally got to know him through his chairmanship of the Committee on Small Business. In 1980, the Senate went Republican for the first time in a very long time, and Gaylord Nelson had to relinquish the chairmanship of the Small Business Committee to Lowell Weicker, the Republican.

In the job I had at that time, I had been doing a lot of work with the small business contractors, construction contractors, and knew a lot about small business labor law, small business contracting, the 8(a) Program, and so Bob Dotchin, who was the staff director of the Committee, called and asked me to interview, to come over to work for the Senate because of my background. He had to hire an entire new staff after the Nelson staff resigned. A few of them stayed in the minority, but the majority staff had to be completely hired, and so I interviewed with Bob. He offered me the job, and it wasn’t until I had been on the job for a couple of weeks that I even actually met Senator Weicker.

Leffler: How did you know Bob?

K. Elliott: I had been working with the Committee on a lot of small business issues on behalf of the organization I worked for called the Associated Builders & Contractors. We were involved with advocacy, so I was involved with both the majority and the minority staff of the Committee and had established a good relationship with both sides of the aisle, so Bob asked if I’d consider coming on as a new staff person, which I said yes to.

Leffler: Had you been trained specifically to do the kind of the work you were doing? Were you—

K. Elliott: No, not really. I had an undergraduate degree in political science and Spanish and then a master’s degree in Spanish literature, which had nothing to do with any of it.

Leffler: Well, the political science maybe.

K. Elliott: My first job in Washington was working for a group of Hispanic business owners where I was actually the only native English speaker, and so I worked helping— It’s the time the 8(a) Program had just been established under President [Jimmy] Carter, and so I became familiar with that program. The law had been written by the Small Business Committee in the Senate before it passed the full Senate, and so I just developed a lot of expertise around small business issues because that’s where I happened to be working.

Leffler: So what interested you then in switching from a private corporation, basically, or advocacy group, to work directly within the halls of government?

K. Elliott: I thought it’d be a great challenge. I’d sort of grown up being very drawn to public service, and I thought it was a great opportunity to become involved in the legislative branch of government, something I’d always been very interested in and studied, and it just seemed like a great opportunity with a brand new Congress, a brand new President, and it turned out to be one of the best decisions I ever made.

Leffler: Was there anything specific at that point about Senator Weicker that drew you in? You said you didn’t meet him for several weeks afterwards.

K. Elliott: Well, in high school I had followed the Watergate hearings, so I knew a little bit about who he was, but I was born in Arizona, raised in California, and had never been to Connecticut. I was born and raised and proud to be a Democrat, so working for a Republican was a little off-putting for me, but since he was a liberal Republican I sort of swallowed and decided to do it.

I actually went to work because the substance of the work in the Committee interested me much more than Weicker per se, except that I had admired him during Watergate. I actually worked for all of the Republican Senators on the Committee, and frankly did more work for Senator Sam Hayakawa at the time, who was from California, and Senator Orrin Hatch, because they chaired, respectively, the subcommittees where I had the most expertise, one on small business labor law and one on small business procurement law. I had very little actually to do with Senator Weicker, except when he learned that I spoke many languages he was interested in having me translate documents for him. That’s really how I interacted with him for the first year and a half, primarily looking at documents and translating either from French to English or English to French or to Italian or to Spanish.

Leffler: What sorts of documents, then? Were they documents directly related to this work?

K. Elliott: It was either directly related to the work or directly related to other interests that he had in the personal office, his Senate office as opposed to the Committee office, but he realized he had a linguist on staff, so he used my abilities.

Leffler: I bet. I’m going to come later to your engagement with the issues in Cuba, but I’ll bet your Spanish background had a lot to do with that as well.

K. Elliott: It did.

Leffler: So you joined the Small Business Committee as a staffer in 1980. Is that right?

K. Elliott: Right. December of ’80, but really started more in January of ’81 when the Congress actually converted to Republican control, or the Senate did.

Leffler: Right.

K. Elliott: But Bob hired me in December.

Leffler: What kinds of work did you do directly for that Committee?

K. Elliott: I worked a lot on legislation to make it easier for small business owners to get federal contracts, either minority or just small business in general. I ran a series of small business procurement seminars across the country, went to various states where Senators on the Committee were the representatives, and so I did several in Connecticut, California, Utah, various places. I would go and rent a room in a Hilton Hotel and have the Small Business Administration folks come in, talk to small business owners about how they might pursue government contracts, and I also worked substantively and organizationally to launch the first White House Conference on Small Business that was held by President [Ronald] Reagan in 1982.

Leffler: A whole group of people clearly must have worked on that.

K. Elliott: Right. A lot of folks worked on it, but I was the point person for the Senate Committee. Most of the work was done out of the Small Business Administration and the Commerce Department.

Leffler: Sure.

K. Elliott: But I was the liaison for the Senate Committee.

Leffler: You said it took about two weeks before you even met then Senator Weicker. Once you did meet him, and you also said you worked for several Senators, once you started working on that committee and began to get to know him, how did that relationship evolve from that initial encounter and beyond?

K. Elliott: Honestly, it really didn’t. The work that he was most interested in was not in the subjects I was working on, so really I worked primarily for other Senators. Again, my relationship with him primarily was when I was called to the personal office to look at documents or to do some translation, that sort of thing. He was very cordial. He was lovely, lovely to work for, but he really—and although he was my boss, it was in title only. I really reported more to other Senators on the Committee.

Leffler: You’ve mentioned two. Orrin Hatch, and I’m sorry I didn’t quite get the—

K. Elliott: Senator Sam Hayakawa.

Leffler: Hayakawa, right.

K. Elliott: Of California.

Leffler: Right, yes.

K. Elliott: Senator Don Nickles of Oklahoma. I really don’t remember all the others, to tell you the truth.

Leffler: Yes, sure. It’s a while ago. So from there you work as a staffer on the Committee on Small Business. When do you actually move over to Senator Weicker’s office as the—

0:09:10

Joins Weicker’s personal staff

K. Elliott: He was reelected in November 1982 and I joined the personal staff in January of 1983. So it was after the election. As most Senators do after an election, they sort of review what’s going on in their offices. His long-time legislative director, Geoffrey Baker, had decided to leave and go into a law practice with his wife, and so there was an opening there. Frankly, Bob Dotchin recommended me as someone that had developed a lot of skills in legislation and parliamentary procedure on the Committee, and so he recommended that Senator Weicker interview me for the job. He did, and he offered it to me.

Leffler: And by then you knew enough about him to know that this would be a desirable change for you?

K. Elliott: Well, watching him overcome a serious primary challenge by President [George H.W.] Bush’s brother, Prescott Bush, who later withdrew, and then winning in a very close race against Toby [Anthony John] Moffett, I really was able to watch him and got to know a lot about his policies and positions, because during a campaign that all becomes front and center, and I really felt very aligned with his position on most major issues, so I thought it would be an interesting job. I was at that time one of the very few women that had the title of legislative director, and was very unique because I wasn’t from his party or from his state, and he took a big risk in hiring me.

Leffler: So you moved immediately into that position of legislative director?

K. Elliott: I did.

Leffler: Why do you think he took that risk?

K. Elliott: I think he had a lot of confidence in Bob Dotchin’s judgment, and I think that he had seen me work on various projects over the course of two years, even if it was from a distance, and I had developed a strong relationship with his chief counsel at the time, Stan Twardy, and was able to provide Stan and then the campaign a lot of good information about small business issues. I think they liked the way I wrote, they liked the way I dug into the issues.

Leffler: You stayed on as legislative director for how many years?

K. Elliott: Until he was defeated in 1988, so literally I left the Senate in January of ’89 and actually turned off the lights in the office.

Leffler: Oh.

K. Elliott: I was the last one out the door.

Leffler: Oh. That must’ve been difficult.

K. Elliott: That was very difficult.

Leffler: I can imagine, sure. So you are really in a position between ’83 and ’89 to be watching absolutely everything—

K. Elliott: I did.

Leffler: —that goes on in that office to follow everything and try to organize the way in which issues are dealt with as best you can. Can you describe what a typical day might have looked like for you?

K. Elliott: I had breakfast with Senator Weicker almost every single morning that the Senate was in session at about seven o’clock. We would eat in the Senate cafeteria. He and I would go over the day’s likely agenda, although in the Senate the agenda is not ever determined by an individual Senator; it’s determined by the leadership. We’d be back up in the office by 7:30, quarter of 8:00, and I would go through paperwork, meet with different legislative aides that reported to me around different issues, depending on what was happening in the Senate at that time.

When I first started, Ronald Reagan was President and the far right was making big moves to influence the U.S. social agenda, particularly Jesse Helms and some others, and Senator Weicker had a very different opinion about what the Constitution meant than those folks did, and so he asked me early on to spend a couple of hours every single day over on the Senate floor just observing. I would sit back in the rows and sort of watch the parliamentary procedure, try to absorb it so that we could be better equipped if we had to raise points of order or fight them on procedure, so that I would be every bit as informed as Jesse Helms’ staff was.

Steve Moore, who had taken Stan Twardy’s place as counsel, he and I would take turns every day going over and sitting on the Senate floor in the staff area for an hour or two at a time trying to learn. And as a result of that, I developed a very close working relationship with several Senate parliamentarians and wasn’t very shy about asking what points of order were and rules, and so I would often, when there would be a break in Senate activity, go sit in their offices and try to understand what had been happening.

I’d work with the staff as well to make sure that if there was a speech that needed to be written, if there was a legislative policy paper that needed to be written, if there were talking points for something that he needed, we were always ready to go. I chaired the staff meetings for the personal office staff as well as for all the committee staff that he had, and so once a week, we would have a senior staff meeting with the staff director for the Small Business Committee, for the Appropriations subcommittees that he had staff on, for the Energy Committee, and for the Committee on Labor & Health Human Services, and then our senior staff. My job was to coordinate all that legislative activity and make sure that all the committee folks knew what the personal office was doing and vice versa.

I later described it in many different interviews that I had, that it was sort of like being the bionic octopus. I had my hands all over the legislative agenda, making sure that everyone was coordinated and that if Senator Weicker had a particular position, it was well researched, that we had documentation to back us up on anything that might be challenged, that sort of thing.

Leffler: Did you have a number of people then working for you?

K. Elliott: Oh yes.

Leffler: Aside from the staffers, but people who you could delegate things out to?

K. Elliott: Well, we had a legislative staff of about eight people that we would divide up issues so one person would handle defense and foreign policy, somebody else was doing transportation, etc.

Leffler: It just seems like keeping track of all these different things by yourself would’ve been a huge—

K. Elliott: No. People reported in to me, so I like to always say I had about a one-inch knowledge of everything, and that the depth really lay with the people that were doing the substantive work.

Leffler: Yes.

K. Elliott: So, for example, before a vote or a bill was coming up and it was a new issue, we would set up some time, and whoever the subject expert was and myself and maybe the communications person, we’d sit together with Senator Weicker and talk to him about the pros and cons of an issue based on what the research had shown and then he would make a decision. Once he did, then we would make sure that all of the supporting documentation that we needed reflected whatever decision he made.

Leffler: These people who researched the papers that are now at UVA have pointed out that there are numerous papers that indicate staff recommendations.

K. Elliott: Right.

Leffler: Which you could check yes or no.

K. Elliott: Yes or no, right.

Leffler: Was that atypical, do you think?

K. Elliott: No. That was pretty standard across most Senate offices. The idea was to try to condense the issue into a one-page paper that would do an overview, provide both sides of the argument, and then based on the Senator’s voting record, make a recommendation about how he should vote on that particular issue, but that was very standard.

Leffler: Did Senator Weicker actively seek the recommendations and explore the thinking processes of his staff members in terms of getting to those recommendations?

K. Elliott: It depended on the issue. If it was an issue that he was already working on in the Senate—I was starting with his third term, so he’d already been there for 12 years and many issues just aren’t that new. They cycle through on a pretty regular basis, so for those kinds of things, we had a well-established position. He had voted many times. He really didn’t need much assistance or input. It was more for issues that were evolving, things that were new, things that were controversial, and again, we were dealing at least in the first couple of years I was there with a far-right agenda that was quite aggressive, and so we spent a lot of time thinking as a group, briefing him as a group and as individuals about how we could successfully combat some of the far right attempts to alter the Constitution.

Leffler: Was there general unanimity on the staff on those issues, or would you say there was a range of opinion?

K. Elliott: Generally, there was unanimity. There were probably a handful of issues where there was either a range or there was disagreement.

0:19:17

Key issues for Weicker’s staff

Leffler: I have my own sense of what those issues were that were the far right issues, but it would be very helpful if you would tell me what you see as those major issues that you were working on.

K. Elliott: Certainly one of the most major was the separation of church and state.

Leffler: School prayer?

K. Elliott: School prayer was very large, and in fact, I have a photo or a drawing from Senator [John] Danforth, Senator [Howard] Baker, and Senate Weicker. That’s before they had TV in the Senate, so they had court reporters that would draw them.

Leffler: That’s terrific.

K. Elliott: When he left the Senate, he gave that to me, so it’s quite valuable, actually.

Leffler: Yes.

K. Elliott: And that was during the school prayer debate. That was 1984 and we won that one. So it was separation of church and state. It was lots of issues around busing, issues about a woman’s right to choose were very controversial. It was at the time where HIV/AIDS [human immunodeficiency virus/acquired immune deficiency syndrome] was just identified and it was portrayed as a homosexual disease and one that was fitting for those who practiced those kinds of behaviors. That was a very ugly, ugly time in the Senate, but we fought that.

You know, we fought for equity, women’s equity in sports through the Grove City case and Title X, free speech, first amendment, lots of first amendment issues, and then we took on the War Powers Act, quite a lot as well, so a pretty full social agenda.

Leffler: Right. Were you also involved in the anti-apartheid?

K. Elliott: Yes, I was very involved in the anti-apartheid. I worked on the first draft of that legislation with Senator [Edward M.] Kennedy and his staff. I missed Senator Weicker’s arrest because I was about nine months pregnant and he wouldn’t let me go out, but I was the one that stayed back so when they called to see if anybody would post bail that they would call me.

Leffler: Oh, really?

K. Elliott: It turned out they never called. They let him go on his own recognizance. The whole staff got to go watch him be arrested, but he was just worried about me. It was in the middle of winter and he was just worried that it might be not a good thing for me to be out.

Leffler: That’s very nice.

K. Elliott: I was pregnant with our first child, so—

Leffler: So this is a huge number of very heady and important issues that you—

K. Elliott: Right, but equally important—and I think this is the one thing that’s not really talked about a lot with Senator Weicker because he was such an national figure—equally important to him were the issues that mattered to Connecticut. He was a huge, huge advocate for Amtrak and kept funding going for that train system, which was vital to his state. When the Mianus Bridge collapsed near his hometown of Greenwich, we spent inordinate resources on trying to make sure that we got funds and repairs going as quickly as we could.

Even though he had a national agenda and portfolio, he always remembered that he was Connecticut’s Senator first. So we spent a fair amount of time working on issues that really would improve the quality of life for the citizens of Connecticut. We did a lot of work on defense contracts and defense legislation because at the time we had both Pratt & Whitney aircraft, we had a large submarine base down in New London, we had the Coast Guard Academy outside New London, and so I would say that it was probably more of an 80 percent/20 percent mix, 80 percent being what really impacted the citizens of Connecticut.

Leffler: Really?

K. Elliott: And 20 percent being more national—

Leffler: That’s interesting.

K. Elliott: But because what he did on the national front was so important, a lot of that got eclipsed. The other thing is, you know, I think another very important position he held was on the Appropriations Committee.

Leffler: Oh, yes.

K. Elliott: So he was able to really work on a lot of projects and provide a lot of funding to the State of Connecticut for very valuable endeavors, everything from child development programs at Yale that were cutting edge and still are cutting edge that he started, to community health centers in the state. The first one ever was under his leadership. Again, we did a lot of work with small businesses in the State of Connecticut, etc.

Leffler: So as his responsibilities proliferated, and I know they did grow substantially. How did this change the nature of your work specifically?

K. Elliott: I just spent more hours in the office.

Leffler: So it wasn’t a difference in kind, you managed things largely in the same way?

K. Elliott: Right.

Leffler: There were no new—

K. Elliott: Every Senate office receives a budget based on population, so he and Senator [Christopher] Dodd received X amount of money to run their offices, and so we had a pretty finite number of people that we could hire. That budget had to cover the offices in Connecticut as well as the office in Washington, and then the way you added staff was seniority on committees. So, for example, he had a couple of people on Appropriations because he was so senior, same thing on Small Business. He had quite a large staff because he was chairman.

All in all, you just can’t add people as the issues emerge or evolve. You sort of have to do with the group you have, and so we all, especially during the first few years, ’83, ’84, ’85, ’86, we all spent many, many nights all night working, and the Senate was often in to 11:00, 12:00, sometimes all night. The far right would do that on purpose to try to wear down those who had different points of view, and Senator Weicker was never one to not be there. He was so—I never left the Senate ever until the Senate bell rang the last time, so it might’ve been 3:00 in the morning when I went home, and I’d be back at 7:00 having breakfast with him.

Leffler: You have to be young and hardy, don’t you?

K. Elliott: Yes, young and hardy and silly. [laughter] No, it was—

Leffler: They were very important years.

K. Elliott: They were very important years, and I like to think we made—we didn’t win all, but we won enough battles to I think really keep our Constitution intact, which was our overall goal.

0:26:11

Weicker and the Constitution

Leffler: Right. I’d like to talk a little bit about the Constitution. I know Senator and Governor Weicker in many conversations he’s had with me has talked about the Constitution. Others that I’ve talked to have talked about his commitment to the Constitution, so that comes up over and over again. Can you give me your sense of what he meant by constitutionality? What specifically about the Constitution he wanted to protect? I mean, the Constitution’s clearly an incredibly complex document.

K. Elliott: Well, I think he had studied the Constitution and believed it was an incredibly elegant document and really portrayed the hopes and dreams of our forefathers as they established our country. He was particularly interested in the first amendment pieces of the Constitution, but not just that.

When I began working for him, there were many attempts to alter that Constitution, and he felt that would be wrong, so in a way, ironically, he’s probably one of the strictest constitutionalists that’s ever served because he believed in both the letter and the intent of the Constitution as protecting all Americans equally. He felt that when there was a disenfranchised group, be it people with disabilities, be it people with HIV/AIDS, be it poor and minority folks, that that was the one document that could protect their rights, and the Senate was the one institution within that document that could protect those rights, and because of the way the Senate was established where one Senator could stop certain things, could filibuster, etc., he took that extraordinarily seriously.

I believe that for him, defending the Constitution, what he interpreted that to be was defending the rights of the most disenfranchised of American citizens and upholding that this was a nation of laws and not of men. That went back to his time in Watergate, that really it was the law that should prevail and not the whims of a President run amok, or his staff, and that we really needed to always revert to what the law said. He would’ve been a magnificent Supreme Court justice because he was so clear in his thinking about what the Constitution gave us was protection of everyone under the law and it was the ultimate protection for wrongdoing at the highest reaches of our federal government in the legislative, in the judicial, or in the executive branch. It happened to be the executive branch that he became most vocal about during Watergate and then later during the war powers debates as well.

Leffler: So in terms of this commitment to the law, would you also say that he was trying to protect the separation of powers in the Constitution?

K. Elliott: Oh, absolutely. Separation of powers was hugely important to him, as were all of the very specific elements of the Constitution as they pertained to the Senate, so in terms of advice and consent on presidential nominees, insofar as treaties were concerned, those were never a pro forma vote for him. They were always taken very seriously because the Constitution itself spelled out those unique duties for Senators.

Leffler: And that’s how the War Powers Act also comes in, yes?

K. Elliott: The War Powers Act came in. That was clearly separation of powers.

Leffler: Yes. Over the Gulf War, in this case, right?

K. Elliott: Oh, gosh, no.

Leffler: No?

K. Elliott: No. Well, I mean, I’m sure that was the Gulf War, too. It was especially prominent during the invasion of Grenada.

Leffler: Oh, right. I’ve read about how he stood up.

K. Elliott: And our deployment of troops in Lebanon.

Leffler: Right.

K. Elliott: So he wasn’t in the Senate during the first Gulf War. That was the second President [George W.] Bush.

Leffler: Oh, I’m sorry. Okay. I’ve gotten my dates wrong.

K. Elliott: No. He’d already been defeated.

Leffler: Right. Right.

K. Elliott: The first Gulf War started in ’91 and he was—

Leffler: Governor then.

K. Elliott: He was—

Leffler: Governor.

K. Elliott: Was he Governor? Yes, he was Governor.

Leffler: Yes, he was Governor, so he was still in office, but not in the Senate. Right. Of course there would be those who would want to argue that the Constitution, although a document of laws and not men, needs men to interpret all those different clauses, and that there are often conflicts.

K. Elliott: And he would say that’s what the nine people across the street do.

Leffler: Yes. [laughter]

K. Elliott: Now, men and women—when he was there, men primarily—but in terms of interpreting the law, he would say that’s the position of the Court. In terms of enacting the law and creating the law, that was the position of the Senate and the House, but he was very clear that once the law was passed and signed by the President, the interpretation then went to the judicial branch and he fought hard to protect the judicial branch. He at one point was the chairman of the Subcommittee on Appropriations that funded the judiciary and was very, very intent on making sure that they had the funds and the resources to actually interpret law, not just at the Supreme Court level but at the appellate court levels as well.

Leffler: Senator Weicker is very proud of his resistance to the increasing conservatism of the Republican Party. On which issues do you think he and the staff were most successful?

0:32:37

Importance of separation of church and state

K. Elliott: I think our biggest victory during the Reagan administration was the defeat of the constitutional amendment on school prayer.

Leffler: Right.

K. Elliott: The fact that he was working with some of the moderate Republicans and the Democrats and was able to defeat that was enormously important, and really I think was a turning point in their social agenda being pushed forward, so I would always look to his leadership in defeating that constitutional amendment as something that was hugely important.

Leffler: Can we stay there for a just minute? Do you recall what some of the most effective strategies were in being able to get to that point?

K. Elliott: I think making it an issue around separation of church and state rather than allowing it to by default become the school prayer issue, really looking at the historical negative impact of the involvement of a church in a government going back to the Middle Ages, frankly, even before, and really taking it out of the sound bite of school prayer. What’s wrong with a kid saying a prayer in school? You know, most kids pray in school before a test. It’s the silent prayer, but they’re all praying for that A— [laughter]

So I think he very wisely moved that issue from just whether it was okay for the kid to say the Pledge of Allegiance and then some innocuous prayer to really looking at the historic impact of when church and state intermingle and the fact that our country was founded on the fact that religious persecution in Europe really drove many people to this country, and so reviving that historical context was incredibly useful in helping other Senators become more educated and giving them the courage, frankly, to stand up and say, “This is more than about my kid praying in school. This is far more complicated.” So I think that strategy early on was wise.

I think one of the best moments in Senate history, and unfortunately it wasn’t recorded for television because we didn’t have television in those days, but during that time he received, as you can imagine, death threats very often, sometimes a couple of times a day, certainly several times a week, and so we had alerted the FBI. [Federal Bureau of Investigation] He didn’t have Secret Service, but we all were feeling the threat and the pressure of this because folks who felt the other way were quite vocal and quite willing to take possibly negative action, so there was a lot of tension as we were mounting up towards this vote.

One day we were going through the mail and one of the correspondence folks came upstairs and said, “Well, you’re not going to believe this, but we just received a telegram from the devil,” and I went in to the Senator and I said, “Well, at least we know where the devil lives,” because Elmira, Illinois, was where the postmark on the telegram was. And so it was a very nasty and threatening telegram saying that if you persist in this, you will destroy our country, that you’re ungodly. “This is Satan and you’re coming to me soon,” and that sort of thing. It was quite something, and so because he’s Lowell Weicker, he went to the Senate floor with the telegram in his hand and asked for time and said, “I have good news and bad news.” “The bad news is that” you know, “I’ve now been portrayed as the devil by Satan himself. The good news is if we all give Elmira, Illinois, wide berth, we’re all going to be fine.”

Leffler: [laughter]

K. Elliott: So that was a moment of levity in a very tense time.

Leffler: He seems absolutely masterful in terms of being able to find the principle that can resonate with a large number of people.

K. Elliott: And you know, the good news for him is he’s consistent, because those principles are spelled out in the Constitution. It always goes back to that for him, so the separation of church and state was exactly how it’s described in the Constitution, not as whether or not there should be prayer in school, so he went back to that document he valued above everyone and found his strength in shaping the arguments there in that document and that was consistent throughout his career.

Leffler: And what were the coalitions that he was able to form to carry out these fights? Who were the most important people that he worked with to make this happen?

K. Elliott: It varied on the issue.

Leffler: Well, let’s say on this particular—

K. When we’d go out there and he’d see the labs and the rats and he wanted to—

Leffler: Well, he came from a pharmaceutical background.

K. Elliott: And he loved it. He learned about these diseases, he got involved in these diseases and finding cures, and he loved medicine, loved it, loved it, and research, loved it. So we did that, and a lot of the folks that he had advocated on their behalf they wanted him to do this because he funded NIH before it was popular to fund it, and so there were a lot of folks in town that saw this need and wanted him to run it. Then he ran for Governor successfully and I wasn’t about to move to Connecticut because I had at that point my second child and we lived here, and so we separated for a little while. Then it was about 1992 and he was in the governorship and he called. We were still—I stood up for he and Claudia at their wedding. I’m personally very close to them as well as professionally. It wasn’t like I was ever out of touch with them.

So he called me and said, “We just won the Olympics. We’re going to have the Olympics here in Connecticut,” and I said, “I think you’re dreaming. They’re going to be in Atlanta,” in 1996. He said, “No, no, it’s the Special Olympics. The Special Olympics are going to come.”

Leffler: Oh.

K. Elliott: “We’re going to do the Special Olympics World Games and I want you to be my special assistant on this and make sure—I’m going to put a $20 million line of credit, but I don’t want the games to have to use the line of credit. So I want you to come and protect my backside on this stuff,” and I said, “I’m not going to commute.” He said, “Yes, you are.”

Leffler: He’s very persuasive.

K. Elliott: To make a long story short, I went to work for the ’95 games in Connecticut. I did commute. I stayed at the Governor’s residence when I commuted, so I would fly to Hartford on a Monday afternoon or Tuesday morning. I’d stay at the residence and drive down to New Haven every day, drive back to the residence, and then I would fly home on Thursday.

Leffler: What were the ages of your children at that time?

K. Elliott: Two and six. And I have an incredibly supportive husband. When I wasn’t working in Connecticut, I used offices over at Special Olympics, and I got to know Eunice Kennedy Shriver and Sargent Shriver and that movement very well, and so when the games were over they hired me to be the chief operating officer there.

Leffler: Oh.

K. Elliott: And so because Special Olympics has always been so important to Governor Weicker/Senator Weicker, after Sonny [Weicker] was born he was very involved, and so it was a way to—I still communicated quite a lot with him about those issues, and I want to say for the record that he was heavily involved with issues with disability before Sonny was born.

Leffler: Yes, I know that. Others have said this too, and that’s very clear.

K. Elliott: And in some ways Sonny was such a gift for him to understand the laws he had worked for so hard, and he has always thought of Sonny as probably his biggest gift in life, what he’s taught all of us about life and about ability.

So I stayed in touch with that, and then after a while I worked on one of the Shriver boy’s political campaigns. It didn’t go so well for him.

Weicker asked me to staff Barkley for the two months he was going to be in the Senate because he was an Independent as Weicker had been an Independent, and he wanted somebody to hit the ground running on a parliamentary area, and then when he left the Senate, Weicker had started this with Maureen and others, Trust for America’s Health.

Leffler: Oh wow. So you did stay involved all through these years?

K. Elliott: I’ve been involved. Very little time haven’t I been actively either working for him or reporting to him. I report to him still. [laughter] Now I’m raising money for his library project.

Leffler: He has that kind of draw. He’s a very, very strong and persuasive human being. Clearly, I know there are people who did not like working for him and probably left the staff at various points, and there was one negative editorial that I know I’ve seen. I can’t even remember who wrote that, but of a staff member who said about him that he got intensely involved in an issue, worked on it hard, then sort of transferred his allegiance to something else. Clearly, the record does not demonstrate that.

K. Elliott: Well, no, and anybody who actually understood the legislative process would understand that’s par for the course, because once you resolve an issue, you go on to the next one.

Leffler: Of course, yes.

K. Elliott: So once you defeat a constitutional amendment on school prayer, there’s not another one pending—

Leffler: Right.

K. Elliott: So you do need to move on.

Leffler: But did you feel that people who worked for him needed to have a particular kind of toughness or commitment to issues that was not true across the board, or would it be your sense that most of the people who worked on his staff just really enjoyed that process? You’d be in a position to know.

K. Elliott: I don’t recall many people voluntarily leaving the staff unless they were being transferred or their spouses were moving. It was a wide mix of a lot of folks from Connecticut who understood the state, a lot of people that were experts on issues. We had very little turnover. I recall hardly any turnover, actually. I mean, there may have been disgruntled folks, but they don’t come rushing to my mind.

Leffler: Okay, so every human being has strengths and weaknesses. Are there any weaknesses in this process that you were involved in with him in terms of the time in Congress that you think made it harder for him, harder for the issues to get through?

K. Elliott: Well, I think the thing that’s his strength is also the weakness, in that he was nobody’s man but his, and so he didn’t care about being lauded by political Washington. He didn’t care about whether or not he had to own a tux and go to the White House for an event. That payoff wasn’t important to him, so what official Washington thought of him he could care less about. Those folks who want to be on the inside and going to all the right parties, we weren’t the ones that were invited. If folks wanted that kind of caché, you didn’t get it by working for Lowell Weicker.

You did get a lot of labor union guys coming and thanking you and shaking your hand, and we also had—because of Watergate, he was probably one of the most ethical members of the Senate, so none of us at any level, even though the rules allowed it, were allowed to go to lunch and have it paid for or have a gift, anything like that. I mean that was just—he went above and beyond, and I know when he was defeated, he had been the first Republican ever in the state of Connecticut to get the AFL-CIO [American Federation of Labor-Congress of Industrial Organizations] endorsement for the Senate. I had worked really hard to get that endorsement for him and was very tight with all the labor leaders in Washington, D.C. They actually asked him if they could give me a string of a pearls and he said no, it was over the gift rule. So, bless their hearts, each one of them went out and bought one pearl and had it strung together, which he said was okay.

Leffler: Oh, that’s lovely. What a great story that is.

A hundred years from now what do you think are the issues that Lowell Weicker will be most remembered for as history gets written?

K. Elliott: Again, it wasn’t during my tenure, but helping insure that this nation did not run amok under the leadership of Richard Nixon, that he contributed to reinforcing that we are a nation of laws and not of men. I think his role in Watergate as a Republican will be hugely important. I think that fighting the social agenda of the far right in the ’80s will be something.

I think on a much probably smaller level, but equally important, will be his advocacy and passion for folks with disabilities and the fact that he changed the world, literally the world, in terms of education of a population that had been denied—

Leffler: Right.

K. Elliott: —education and equal rights, and now it’s not just our country, it’s all over the world that these kids are mainstreamed.

Certainly being the fiercest defender of the Constitution of the United States during his entire tenure of the Senate. I think those are the things. Certainly what he did as Governor was a whole different issue.

Leffler: Right. One thing we haven’t talked about is his commitment to environmental issues. Were those issues that continued during your period of time?

K. Elliott: Well, I would argue that he was probably not the most environmentally committed Senator that was there. He was incredibly committed around oceans, but his record on some other kinds of environmental concerns was probably more conservative. On the Energy Committee, I think he would believe in more oil and gas drilling.

Leffler: He was involved in helping to create the Super Fund, right, after the Ixtoc spill?

K. Elliott: Yes.

Leffler: All of this business in the Gulf right now, I keep on thinking about that.

K. Elliott: Certainly his passion for the oceans and what they could bring to mankind was really unparalleled, but I’m not so sure about air and land conservation as much.

Leffler: Do you see the transportation issues, the Amtrak issues?

K. Elliott: He loved transportation issues.

Leffler: But that also could be tied, could it not, to environmental issues in terms of taking money away from building of highways and transferring it to rapid transit?

K. Elliott: Right. He was a huge advocate of mass transit, rapid transit, not just for environmental reasons, but more for quality of life reasons for his constituents, but if you asked me to name the top ten environmental Senators of all time, he certainly wouldn’t be one of them.

Leffler: He would not be one of them. I understand that.

K. Elliott: With the exception of oceans.

Leffler: No. Oceans were an absolute passion and I think they continue to be.

K. Elliott: They continue to be.

Leffler: And because he really experienced life underwater.

K. Elliott: Oh yeah, boy. I loved when he would go down to those deep dives, because he wouldn’t call me for a whole week. [laughter] It was so nice.

Leffler: And you could get something done.

K. Elliott: And not have to hear from him at 6:30 every morning or 5:00 every morning. It got to the point when the phone rang in the morning Mark would just turn over and say, “It’s him. Just answer it.”

Leffler: This has been a really wonderful interview. Is there anything else you would like for the record to talk about that we haven’t gotten to today?

K. Elliott: Well, this is a very small issue, and it’s really not that big a deal, but the other area he championed and that never gets talked about was treating the U.S. Territories as equal parts of the United States. He actually chaired the subcommittee that handled all the U.S. Territories, so whether it was Puerto Rico or the Virgin Islands or American Samoa or Guam, he really was the Senator that made sure that those Governors got invited when the NGA [National Governors Association] would come to testify about what was going on in the trust territories, so that was through the Energy Committee. It’s a very small thing, but for those scholars who are looking at the relationship between the United States government and territories, I think it’s something that shouldn’t be just glossed over because there’s a pretty rich body of information there.

Leffler: Yes. You’re the first person who’s actually raised that. So how would he do that? How would he make sure?

K. Elliott: Well, first of all, there would be hearings around different issues. He’d first of all invite the Governors of the territories to come testify before the Energy Committee and give updates. Always the most visible issue was voting rights in Puerto Rico, but he helped place marine sanctuaries in the Virgin Islands. He made sure that Guam and American Samoa had whatever appropriations they needed to do things and not just be military bases out there, but to look at the quality of life of those citizens who lived, sort of far off and far away, but were they getting services?

Leffler: Right.

K. Elliott: That sort of thing.

Leffler: And you think the motivation for that is the same as the motivation of looking at the larger equity issue?

K. Elliott: Right. We have the 50 states, but we have these other—

Leffler: Yes.

K. Elliott: And most of the reasons we have these territories is for military reasons, to help defend.

Leffler: Yes.

K. Elliott: So if they’re going to defend us, we need to defend them, and so it was sort of this whole mentality about making sure—other people weren’t thinking about that, but he would think about that, what was the quality of life for somebody on Guam.

Leffler: Right.

K. Elliott: And, again, it’s a very narrow little thing and it doesn’t get talked about much, but it was something that I always admired him for.

Leffler: Sure, absolutely. Okay. I think this will end our interview for today. Thank you so much for your time.

K. Elliott: My pleasure. If you have any other questions or you want to follow up—

Leffler: I may need to come back at some point, depending upon conversations with other people, and I appreciate this very much. Thank you.

[The conversation continues with Elliott recommending other people to be interviewed]

K. Elliott: Again, if I can just make a pitch for Senator Inouye, I mean before these people are gone from us.

Leffler: And who else?

K. Elliott: Well, Byrd would have been somebody who would have been magnificent. Kennedy’s gone, unfortunately. They were so close. Somebody should talk to Chris Dodd, for sure. It’s narrow. It’s not as broad. Inouye and Dodd have the big spectrum, and Kennedy would’ve been the best person out there and Stafford is dead and Mathias is dead. Senator Hatfield if he’s still aware. He was starting to fail, so I don’t know if he’s aware or not. But certainly those colleagues would be important. Tom Harkin, for sure. They fought so many big fights together on behalf of the disabled.

Leffler: Are there people he actively worked against who would be interesting to talk to?

K. Elliott: Who’s left? They’re all gone.

Leffler: I know. It’s really amazing.

K. Elliott: Helms is gone. Thurmond’s gone.

Leffler: So there’re really just a handful of people now that would be of value.

K. Elliott: To go back to the Senate days, somebody who’d be interesting to talk to because they had an adversarial relationship that turned into a very, very strong and positive one would be Dan Quayle. Senator [John] Tower’s gone. I’m just trying to think. [Ted] Stevens. Dan Quayle would be an interesting character to talk to.

Leffler: He would be. We can’t talk to Nixon or Reagan.

K. Elliott: No, and you wouldn’t want to. And he wasn’t there with [William J.] Clinton.

Leffler: Well, okay. Thank you again. I’m going to turn this off now.

[End of interview]
