0:00:00

Leffler: This is Phyllis Leffler. The date is April 27, 2010. I am in the Harrison Small Library with Claudia T. Weicker for an interview in the Lowell Weicker Oral History series. Good morning. Thank you for being here.

C. Weicker: Thank you, Phyllis.

0:00:31

Claudia Weicker’s early life and Catholic upbringing

Leffler: I’d like to start out by just asking you to talk a little bit about your personal background and a little bit about your educational background before you took your first job. But if you could just tell us a little bit about where you were born, anything important, you think, about your early years, and then a little bit about your educational background, that would be great.

C. Weicker: Happy to. I was born in Hartford, Connecticut, and raised in the suburbs of Hartford, in West Hartford, Connecticut. My parents lived in the same house I think for more than 40, almost 50 years, in a nice suburban area. My background is that I am second-generation Italian on all sides. All of my grandparents were immigrants to the United States. My mother’s family settled in Hartford. My father’s family ultimately in Kansas City, Missouri, and sometime during the late ’20s or early ’30s, my father, who did a summer circuit as a racecar driver, wound up in Hartford and met my mother, and all the rest was history. 

I was raised Catholic. My mother was a devout Catholic. My father was Catholic, but I think a little less devout. [laughter] I think he managed to skip a few masses every now and then, but we were sent to parochial school. It’s very interesting, because in the early years of their marriage, my parents lived, first, in Hartford, and when they decided to build a house, they built in West Hartford, which was a rapidly growing more middle class, upper middle class suburb. To the day he died my father always said that the reason why he built his house in West Hartford was because it had at the time not just the best school system in Connecticut, but the best public school system in the country. 

And he drilled into us the fact that he saw—and he voted for every education referendum that ever came up in the town—that the value of his property was directly tied to how well the town took care of its children, so that was my first introduction. 

Leffler: And that’s important in terms of later issues.

C. Weicker: Yes it is. I mean he absolutely drilled this into us, but on the other hand, they sent us to parochial school all our life [laughter], so there was a little bit of an inconsistency there, but I think my mother wanted us to have the Catholic education with the nuns. They were nuns at the time. Very few lay teachers at that time in the schools.

0:04:22

Early interest in politics

C. Weicker: I remember my first recollection of a political discussion. These took place at dinnertime. We always had dinner as a family and there were always discussions about what was going on in the world at dinner. I don’t know whether this was before or after Walter Cronkite. I sometimes remember seeing the news, but I don’t think I paid very much attention to it. I suspect we had dinner after the evening news, but I remember the first discussion, really, was of the 1952 presidential election, and it was election day. My father was furious when he found out that my mother voted for Dwight Eisenhower. He was a Democrat, although interestingly, neither of my parents actively participated, other than voting. They exercised their political, their constitutional responsibility, but they never directly participated in politics.

On the other hand, my mother’s sister, who was my godmother, was actively involved in Democratic politics, and I think through her and through friends I met in high school whose families were more directly involved, I became involved. I was interested in the subject from a young age, but I had the opportunity when I was in high school to work after school at the [John F.] Kennedy headquarters on Main Street in Hartford and I went down at least three days a week, maybe more, to stuff envelopes, sometimes work into the evening doing phoning, going over registration lists, identifying Democratic voters, doing very basic political work, the kind of thing that everybody does in a campaign and still does.

Leffler: Did the Kennedy campaign in particular inspire you, or—

C. Weicker: Oh, I think it did. I think given my background that I was Catholic, number one, that John Kennedy and Jackie Kennedy were a much more attractive couple; younger people could identify with them. The concept of this great big Irish Catholic family that seemed to have a good time when they were together was probably inspirational, but more importantly, I felt from age 14 I was a Democrat, and we had actually followed the primaries in Connecticut. I remember the primary in West Virginia clearly. I remember the results when Kennedy beat [Hubert] Humphrey. Ironically, after college, I would go to Washington and work in the Humphrey campaign in 1968, but in 1960 I was very much involved in the Kennedy campaign.

The only thing that actually irritated me was that my mother—John Kennedy was coming to Hartford, and in those days there were still great public rallies, something which we’ve just recently gotten back to, but there were great public rallies. Candidates would come to the balcony of the Hartford Times building and there’d be masses in the street to listen to them. Kennedy was coming, but my mother would not sign a permission slip to let me leave school to go and see him. I remember coming home from school that afternoon and she wasn’t home. She came back saying, “Oh, he was just wonderful.” So she had gone down there [laughter]

Leffler: But school came first.

C. Weicker: But school came first. So that really is my early introduction to politics.

Leffler: You went to Fordham University?

C. Weicker: No. I went to Marymount College—

Leffler: Marymount, I’m sorry.

C. Weicker: —which was an independent school run by the Sisters of, I guess, St. Mary, and there were several Marymounts. There was one in Arlington, which at the time was a junior college. There was Marymount in Manhattan, Marymount in Tarrytown, which was really the flagship school, and that’s where I went to school. I graduated from Marymount in Tarrytown. I actually went to a local junior college first. I went to a school called Hartford College for Women, which is now part of the University of Hartford. Hartford College was started in the ’30s. It actually was started during the Depression as a place for young women to attend college and have two years of college. 

It was sometimes called Mount Holyoke in Hartford because the original teaching staff came from Mount Holyoke. They would travel to Hartford I don’t know how many days a week for classes, and then it expanded to include professors from Mount Holyoke and Smith and Trinity and Amherst. It had basically a little Ivy League flavor and certainly wonderful, wonderful professors. Even when I was there in the ’60s it still drew its professorial staff from all of these great schools. 

I did ultimately want to go away to college, but I was sensitive to the fact that it would be a burden for my parents if I went for four years, and this seemed like the legitimate compromise here. I had access to a really first-class education in Hartford and I could then go away my last two years.

Leffler: So you lived at home? And saved the cost.

C. Weicker: I lived at home and I became even more involved actually politically during those years. I worked on Senator Tom Dodd’s re-election campaign in 1964, and that’s when I first knew Senator Chris Dodd. Chris is about a year, I think, older than I am, maybe two, and we got to know each other. He at the time was dating a very good friend of mine, and so we saw each other frequently. That’s the first time I met a President of the United States, too. 

Leffler: Who was—?

C. Weicker: Lyndon Johnson. When he came to campaign in Hartford, I was there with Senator Dodd, and I remember when he shook my hand it was the biggest hand I had ever seen. I was a very impressionable, I guess, 17-year-old at the time. 

C. Weicker: There were three or four of us who went everywhere with Senator Dodd when he made campaign appearances and we handed out buttons and we handed out flyers and bumper stickers and did all of those things. We were so-called Dodd Girls [laughter] wearing little white dresses and red, white, and blue ribbons.

And then in my years at Hartford College, I joined an organization called the Connecticut Intercollegiate Student Legislature. It was a club and had membership from all of the universities throughout Connecticut and it was primarily political. We would meet monthly with members from other schools, from Trinity, from Yale, from Wesleyan, from Bridgeport University, from Fairfield, from Connecticut State University, and we would discuss a legislative program. One weekend a year, I think in early March, we would take over the capitol in Connecticut and have a mock legislature, a house and senate. We would elect our own leaders and present legislation, and at that time, at one of those mock sessions, I ran the campaign for a young man from Fairfield University who won as speaker of the house.

Leffler: You were gaining a lot of experience before you ever entered—

C. Weicker: And then the young man who was also part of our ticket who was elected as president of the senate ironically was in the State senate when Lowell became Governor. So there’re a lot of crossovers.

Leffler: That’s very interesting. So you would’ve been at Hartford College for Women—

C. Weicker: 1964 to ’66. 

Leffler: Right, and then you went on to Marymount.

C. Weicker: And then I went on to Marymount and I graduated from Marymount in ’68, so I spent two years at Marymount in Tarrytown.

Leffler: Okay, and then in 1968, you go to work for the Humphrey campaign?

C. Weicker: That’s right. 

Leffler: And when you’re doing that, it seems, I gather that you are focused specifically on the southern states in terms of—and therefore got involved with a number of the civil rights activities that were going on in the South.

C. Weicker: Yes, I did. That was my principal responsibility.

Leffler: Did you travel to the South a lot?

C. Weicker: I didn’t go to the South. I talked with people every day in the South. We had our contacts. The way the campaign was structured with the four regions—East, West, the middle Atlantic, the middle states, and then the southern states, and then subdivided into two sections within each region, I had the Deep South—Florida, Louisiana, Mississippi, North and South Carolina, Alabama and Georgia, and there was really very little hope, with the possible exception of Florida, that Humphrey would carry any of those states. The campaign was short of cash at any rate, and wanted to spend as little as possible in those states, yet it was the first year that the Voting Rights Act was effective in a presidential election.

I was given a list of contacts in each of the states, which included the Democratic state chairman and committee people, and in a number of states I was also in contact with civil rights leaders, especially in Mississippi. In Mississippi I talked regularly with Hodding Carter, whose family had the Cleveland, Mississippi, newspaper. I can’t remember the name of it. It may have been the Delta Times. I can’t remember.

I talked daily with Hodding and I talked virtually every day with other civil rights leaders there, including Charles Evers, who was the head of the NAACP [National Association for the Advancement of Colored People] in Mississippi, and Aaron Henry, and once in a while I talked with Fannie Hamer, so it was a real education for me. But what was important was we wanted to know how voter registration drives were going in those states, whether there was any significant effort to impede the registration of black Americans in voting, and what kind of effort they were putting together to get out the vote. 

In October, I believe it was, of 1968, I was sitting at my desk one day and I received a telephone call from Charles Evers. He had accepted an invitation earlier to speak that evening on the campus of Ole Miss, to the Young Democrats at Ole Miss, and he called me and said that he had just received a telephone call from the Governor’s office informing that if he stepped foot on campus, he would be arrested immediately.

I think I had just turned 22 years old, and I was sitting at my desk and I thought, Okay, now, I think this is what you’re here for [laughter] so I immediately went and talked with my supervisor and then we went—Senator Fred Harris from Oklahoma was one of the campaign chairmen and he happened to be sitting in his DNC [Democratic National Committee] office that day. We went and talked with him and then they went and talked with Larry O’Brien. A telephone call was immediately put into the Justice Department and ultimately Charles Evers did speak that night. He was surrounded by federal marshals when he went to the campus at Ole Miss, but it was an education to me as to how different the world could be. 

I mean this was far different than where I had grown up in nice, quiet West Hartford, Connecticut, and yet it seemed like a wonderful opportunity. In Florida I had a field man, Eddie McCormack, the former attorney general of Massachusetts, who had run against Ted Kennedy in that famous primary in 1962. I talked every day with Eddie McCormack, who was always so enthusiastic about everything that was going on in Florida, always asking for materials. He called me and he said, “I finally received a box of campaign materials. There are holy card–sized photographs of Hubert Humphrey on one side with the Miami Dolphins schedule on the back. “I can use them perhaps to cover one telephone pole.” [laughter] Very soon after that, he called me and he said, “It does not make sense for me to remain in Florida. We’re not going to win here.” So he was moved out, I think, to Maryland.

In Alabama I talked every day with the chairman of the Democratic Party, whose name was Bob Vance, and obviously Alabama was totally lost to [George] Wallace from the very beginning and he was very matter of fact. He said, “You know, you really don’t need to waste a lot of time on us.” But it was this other part, “And, yes, we’re proceeding to register people,” etc. The significance of Bob Vance is that he was one of the federal judges who was killed by the Unabomber years later. It was a real shock to me when I realized who it was.

And then in Tennessee, and I don’t know why I had contact with Tennessee, but I did. I think I was covering for my partner in the region, but one of the people we talked to all the time was John Seigenthaler.

Leffler: Oh.

C. Weicker: And that is ironic, given the fact that he was from the newspaper family.

Leffler: Sure.

C. Weicker: But I suppose so was Hodding Carter. 

Leffler: It sounds to me—correct me if I’m wrong—that all of that gives you quite a bit of exposure to the challenges of winning elections, but also to the diversity of the country, and so once you get involved with Congress, you probably have a much better sense of what other Senators and members of Congress are dealing with within their own districts. Do you feel that you carried that experience with you as you went forward?

C. Weicker: I think there’s no doubt about it, because later on when I worked in the Senate, I worked primarily on committees, They’re made up of Senators from all over, and while your first responsibility may be to your subcommittee chairman and your full committee chairman, you’re really responsible to all the members of the committee. You work for everyone, and I worked with people as diverse as John Glenn from Ohio, who came from an industrial state. Obviously, Lowell Weicker and Abe Ribicoff from Connecticut. I knew Connecticut. Connecticut is very mixed, and at that time, I suppose, I don’t think we used the term as much, but liberal state, progressive maybe more so. And Jim Allen from Alabama, who came from a whole different background, he had a whole different set of beliefs and he had a very different constituency that he was representing, and so you have to respect the member for who their constituents are. Even though you may not agree with their policies, you still have to find a way to work with them. 

On the House side, years later when I was on the Appropriations Committee, and particularly Labor, Health & Human Services, the House chairman was Congressman Bill Natcher from Kentucky. He was an institution in the House. He was one of the longest-serving members of the House. He had a very strong ethic. He was courtly in a very southern way, but he was really incredibly well mannered.

He was far more conservative than either Lowell Weicker or Mark Hatfield, the chairman of the Appropriations Committee, but so well mannered that he would always let you discuss things. And yet he, for years, when he had been chairman of the District of Columbia Appropriations Committee and I lived in the District, had held up for years and years and years the D.C. Metro. He was the single person most responsible for the fact that we didn’t build the Metro 10 or 15 years earlier than we ultimately did. 

Leffler: This was Bill Natcher?

C. Weicker: Bill Natcher, and in the long run it cost so much more to build it. He obviously was representing a constituency that didn’t want to spend money on those people in the District of Columbia.

Leffler: So you’ve had all this experience as a very young person. You’re obviously very connected to the notion that it’s important to make a difference by getting engaged, but then you actually don’t get engaged right away. You go to work on Wall Street for a number of years.

C. Weicker: I did, only because getting that first job on Capitol Hill was very difficult. You either really know someone who—

Leffler: Or you work for nothing.

C. Weicker: Well, even working for nothing may be difficult, or you get a job as an intern I suppose, and work for nothing. I couldn’t afford to work for nothing. My parents had supported me for a couple of months in Washington during the summer of ’68 when I first visited and started looking for a job, and the Humphrey campaign barely paid me enough to live on, but it did. It paid me enough.

Then I was unemployed again, so I had to settle—I had interviews on Capitol Hill. I interviewed with Tom Foley. I interviewed with Brock Adams on the House side. I had a couple of interviews, but it’s a chicken and egg situation. In order to get in, they’re looking for someone with experience, and the only place I knew to get experience was to be there working. I saw this just recently with one of our sons who wanted to get a job in government, and ran into the same conundrum. Unless you want to get there and work for free for a while and hope for the best, then it’s hard to break in.

 So a friend whom I had worked with on the Humphrey campaign and came off of Capitol Hill was opening this office for Arthur Lipper Corporation, and then it became a subsidiary of Oppenheimer, and it was research based on activity in Washington. It was market-based research, but it had its twist in that we followed legislative activities, regulatory activities, executive decisions, that could impact the markets. We followed all of the statistics that come up monthly on unemployment, cost of living, etc., so it actually broadened my background. I went to legislative hearings. I got to know legislative staff on the Hill I called with questions, and so I got my experience through the back door.

Leffler: And now you’re using your economics major with your interest and previous exposure to politics and putting those two together, in a sense growing in terms of your understanding.

C. Weicker: Exactly, but I always, always wanted, in the first instance, to be there, to be on the Hill working. And I did enjoy my years working off the Hill. I had some incredible experiences there, too. For instance, in 1972, one of the young men who came on as a member of our firm, which by that time had changed its name to Bradley Woods, was Dick Cheney, and two of our partners had worked in the [Richard] Nixon administration. Obviously Dick had had experience in the Nixon administration. He had just come off of working on the Cost of Living Council in 1972 and his office was right next to mine. I sat day after day in Dick’s office and we watched the Watergate hearings together in 1973.

Leffler: Oh, really?

C. Weicker: And they were all his friends and contemporaries who were testifying. The Dwight Chapins and the John Deans were people that were not just work colleagues. They were personal friends.

Leffler: It must have been fascinating.

C. Weicker: Well, what was fascinating was when it first happened, just a couple of weeks after the Watergate break-in itself, Alan Woods, one of our partners who had come out of the White House, had to go down to the D.C. Courthouse to look up something and he came back to the office and he immediately went into Dick’s office. I was sitting there and he said, “Dick, you know, there might be something more to this Watergate stuff than we realize.” He said, “I was just down at the courthouse and Hugh Sloan got into the elevator with his lawyer and his lawyer wouldn’t let him talk to me.” Hugh Sloan was one of the early people who did the right thing, so I got to see it with Dick, and of course then later Dick went and became Jerry Ford’s Chief of Staff. 

I knew Dick Cheney when he was a moderate. I knew him not just through work. My husband at the time and I, we were social friends. We had dinner at each other’s houses. I remember Dick’s children when they were collecting worms in jars. So what happened to him between the time he left us and ultimately became Vice President is amazing. I mean the person I saw leave the Vice Presidency is nowhere near the person I knew in the early ’70s. 

Leffler: That’s very interesting. Lots of people, of course, traveled that similar kind of road. We’ve seen it over and over again.

C. Weicker: But I think fundamentally what happened to Dick is that when he became Ford’s Chief of Staff and there was the great surge to reform legislation following Watergate, he saw the executive give up a tremendous amount of authority. You had a couple of Supreme Court decisions. You had [James L.] Buckley v. [Francis R.] Valeo on the veto, the President’s line item veto basically, and you had the aftermath of Watergate, where Congress was really impacting what had been powers assumed by the executive and putting far more controls on it. I think that was the beginning of Dick’s conservatism, and then he went back to Wyoming and emerged as this conservative congressman. 

0:34:54

Hired by Lowell Weicker as legislative assistant

Leffler: In 1975 you joined the minority staff of the Subcommittee on Open Government and you’re hired by Lowell Weicker into that position as a legislative assistant.

C. Weicker: That’s right.

Leffler: But you have told me all along that you were a strong Democrat, so how is it that you were even interested in going into that job? Was it that it was an entrée into government, or was there something specific about the job or the person already at that point that you knew you wanted to do that?

C. Weicker: I think it was a combination of things. Number one, I did want to work on the Hill, and it definitely was an entrée to Capitol Hill. Number two, he never asked me if I was a Democrat. I think he assumed I was, but I don’t think Lowell ever asked any of his staff whether they were Republicans or Democrats. I know later when I was hired by Senator Ribicoff that Ribicoff never asked, and he knew I came from a Republican staff, and later on, Senator Hatfield never asked. Obviously, there’re those who do ask, but—

Leffler: But you were hired because of your skills to do the work.

C. Weicker: Yes. Someone believed that I could do that job that was offered and therefore I took it, but part of the reason I think also was it was explained to me that most of the legislation emanating from Watergate would be coming to, at that time it was called the Governmental Affairs Committee, and most of the reform legislation coming out of Watergate would be going through that Committee, and that’s what I would be working on. I would be working less on Subcommittee business, some on Subcommittee business, but more importantly on these full Committee issues that would be coming up, and the Watergate legislation included the financial disclosure legislation, the establishment of a congressional counsel, the special prosecutor legislation. 

The Congressional Budget Act had already been passed. The oversight legislation over the intelligence agencies and the re-organization of congressional oversight itself would be going through the Governmental Affairs Committee, so these were all very current issues and they were big issues and they would be controversial issues and I thought, Gosh, this is baptism by fire. I like this. We also, on the Subcommittee—the reason why the Subcommittee was called the Subcommittee on Open Government was we did the Government in the Sunshine legislation where congressional hearings and meetings and agency meetings would no longer be held behind totally closed doors. Unless there was a serious reason for closing a hearing, they had to be open to the public.

Leffler: So this is your first exposure to Senator Weicker’s office.

C. Weicker: Right.

0:38:50

Becomes minority staff director of Subcommittee on Open Government

Leffler: And how the office works, and you’re a legislative assistant for—would it be two years before you become the director?

C. Weicker: No. I think I became the director within a year. Dick McGowan, who had been Lowell’s press secretary during Watergate, moved over to become the staff director of the Subcommittee when I was first hired, and then Dick left within a year, I think. He left the Committee and I became the staff director of the Subcommittee, minority staff director.

Leffler: So can you just talk a little bit about how that work got done, who you worked with most closely, how the office itself operated?

C. Weicker: At that time, Bob Dotchin was Lowell’s chief legislative assistant, and I communicated most often and most directly with Bob Dotchin because I still needed his advice on the mechanics of things. He could fill in a little bit of the history of the Senator’s views on items and where he might be coming from so that I was prepared in anything that I wrote for the Senator or would have to present to him as a proposal for a decision that I’d be prepared for whatever view he might take. I mean, if I was suggesting something that might be contrary to his views, I wanted to know it ahead of time and be prepared to defend it, so we had a really close working relationship.

I had lunch I think most days with Bob Dotchin, who was the legislative director, Jeff Baker, who worked on the Commerce Committee staff for Senator Weicker, and a fellow by the name of Mike Scully. Mike was also a legislative assistant, but he was primarily a speechwriter. He was brilliant and very conservative, very conservative. I mean, Mike was an early disciple of [William] Kristol.

Leffler: Oh right. Of course, Bill Kristol.

C. Weicker: Bill Kristol and George Will, and so I think that’s one of the things that was actually sort of refreshing. I was definitely on the other side of the spectrum from everybody. I would put Bob and Jeff in the middle, and I was definitely on the other side from my background and my activities, and we’d always have these really lively conversations at lunch. We frequently had very lively conversations when we got together with the Senator, too, because he had all views around him.

Leffler: And he was interested in all those views and processing that.

C. Weicker: Yes. He definitely was.

Leffler: He relied on his staff in that way. It wasn’t just to do the work to—

C. Weicker: No, we weren’t told. We were rarely told. The only time we were told to do something the way he wanted it done was after a decision had been made, period. But we were there to present decisions, to present issues, and frequently there would be an open discussion. He had his senior staff, who were probably five or six people, and because I was the staff director of one of his subcommittees, I was considered part of that senior staff group. 

He had his administrative assistant at the time. He had Bob Dotchin. He had Jeff Baker, and he’d usually bring in Mike Scully for these discussions, because ultimately Mike might have to write a speech incorporating ideas, and we would frequently have an open discussion on Watergate legislation, for instance. I think Lowell at one time had in his separate views, I think he suggested that everyone should just disclose their tax returns. Of course the Senate legislation was not moving in that direction, and Bob said, “Well, you know, your previous view in your separate comments was tax returns should be disclosed. It’s not what this Committee is currently considering. How do you want to handle that?” “I’ll offer an amendment.” So we would move that way. It was a very open staff relationship. We didn’t have turf battles.

Leffler: Which is remarkable, I think. I mean, I don’t know—

C. Weicker: We never had turf battles. I think he—he being the Senator—made clear from the very beginning that he was hiring this person to do this job, and he made that clear to everyone else who worked for him, to all of his senior staff. If you have an issue that might overlap, then you two get together, and then we’ll talk about it, and that’s usually the way it worked out. In the early days I had very few overlapping issues. The Governmental Affairs Committee work really didn’t overlap with the office at all. Frequently after I got into the routine of working with the office and working with legislation and also working with the full Committee, Dotchin would say, “I think it’s your call, it’s your judgment, and you should present it to the Senator. If you want me there as a backup, I’ll be there.” 

Leffler: In terms of the functioning of these senior staff people, how atypical do you think this is in terms of the ability not to have turf battles? Would you say from what you observed of other offices at the time that this was unique, or do you think it was pretty typical in terms of the way senior staff worked with one another?

C. Weicker: I don’t necessarily think it was that unique. I think if you had a fundamentally good administrator as your sponsor Senator—

Leffler: As the chief of staff, or the Senator?

C. Weicker: The Senator. Then quite frankly he’d laid it out as to how he wanted things handled. Where it could sometimes get difficult is if you had an administrative assistant on top of the legislative assistant who had to handle things. Frequently, that relationship might get a little confused and blurred as to who was making those decisions. There might be turf there if the administrative assistant felt that he or she had more responsibility over legislation. Then there was an added layer.

Leffler: Now, did Senator Weicker have administrative assistants? 

C. Weicker: He had administrative assistants. From time to time he had administrative assistants who for the most part were just that. They made the office run.

Leffler: So that’s a distinction between chief of staff—

C. Weicker: That is different from chief of staff. During the time that I was first with him, he did have an administrative assistant who came out of the Nixon administration, Pete Kinsey.

Leffler: Could you say that name again?

C. Weicker: Pete Kinsey.

Leffler: Pete Kinsey. Thank you.

C. Weicker: Pete came out of the White House counsel’s office, and so he knew a lot of background about Watergate. He was a friend of John Dean’s, but on the other hand, Pete might be able to fill in background. He never really attempted. I would write questions for hearings at home, get in early to make sure that they would be typed up in time for the hearing, and so these were very heady times.

I am not aware of any personal relationship that Lowell had with Ronald Reagan. In fact, the only thing I could tell you, this was also around the time that the school prayer debate was going on, and if you remember, in Vanity Fair recently the Reagan diaries were released. There is that quote in Reagan’s diary that he was defeated on the constitutional amendment on school prayer in the Senate and Lowell Weicker was the ringleader as he is on so many things that we want and he is just a— I’m trying to think of the exact words—“a pompous, no-good fathead.”

Leffler: Yes, exactly.

C. Weicker: That was the quote, “a pompous, no-good fathead.” So there certainly wasn’t any love lost. At one point, during the debate or afterward, I guess, whenever it was that Reagan was shot, Lowell wrote him a personal note which said, “I’m keeping you in my thoughts and prayers,” and Reagan wrote him back thanking him for his kind note and he put a P.S. in his handwriting, and unfortunately this one has been lost, saying, “I didn’t know you prayed.” [laughter] So I think that’s probably the lightest moment in their relationship.

Lowell was horrified when Reagan time after time after time submitted budgets which would cut programs for the disabled or oppose legislation that would expand the rights of disabled people. Reagan held a tenth anniversary celebration of the passage of PL 94-142, the Education for All Handicapped Children Act, and Lowell was horrified by the hypocrisy of that and I think did not go. So unfortunately, I think Lowell always saw Reagan as a great communicator, but that the public who really loved him saw very little of this other side of him that was so callous about America being great, but not so great if you’re poor.

Leffler: He says that in his book.

C. Weicker: His programs went right to the heart of all of these programs which would provide opportunity. Head Start—how do you cut Head Start? From all of the studies and all of the experience, children in Head Start programs do so much better than their peers who have not had that experience. These were all the issues that we dealt with.

Leffler: Right, and they’re really center stage.

C. Weicker: And they are center stage.

Leffler: And the Senator is in a place to make a difference at that point in his career.

C. Weicker: They directly affect people’s lives, the quality of life. I think this is why I felt the Labor, Health & Human Services budget was so important. Everything that I was raised to believe in probably from my Catholic upbringing is that you have a responsibility to help people who do not have the same opportunities you do, and this bill represented that responsibility. Bill Natcher, who was the conservative Democratic chairman from Kentucky referred to this bill, which he loved, as “the people’s bill,” and as conservative as he was, he was very generous in his deliberations on this bill because he saw probably more than anyone else—he had a much larger constituency probably in Kentucky that benefitted from these programs.

Leffler: So which specific bill are we talking about?

C. Weicker: Labor, Health & Human Services, the people’s bill.

Leffler: The bill of what? So it would be a particular year.

C. Weicker: The Appropriations bill. Oh, both years.

Leffler: Oh, both years.

C. Weicker: Both years, because we faced the issue—

Leffler: Each and every year.

C. Weicker: These issues were constant. They came up year after year, and it was like treading water, and in the end—

Leffler: Saved a lot of programs.

2:35:06

How the U.S. budget reflects the country’s priorities

C. Weicker: Well, we saved a lot of programs, but I think we helped to at least save how we define ourselves just as a country. I mean, our country’s priorities are reflected in the budget. I became persuaded of that when I finally did go to work for the Appropriations Committee. There’s plenty of legislating that goes on in an appropriation bill, as we know now, and the best advice that I got was from the Democratic staff person I replaced on State, Justice. He turned to me one day and he said, “You know, what you have to understand coming from an authorizing committee is that one law is as good as another,” and to me, it says everything, because we always did get the last word.

Leffler: Right.

C. Weicker: They could authorize anything. I mean, you could authorize the moon, but it didn’t have to be funded, and that applied to many defense programs. They could be authorized, but they didn’t all have to be funded, and the same thing here. You could request ridiculously low numbers for AIDS research or really disabling amounts of appropriations for NIH during cuts in the NIH budgets, but then you’re changing, really, what has been a fundamental relationship in the United States in research between the NIH, the educational institutions, and the private sector, and all of this was seen as deregulatory, privatizing, limiting government control, limiting government waste.

Leffler: Exactly what Reagan was loved for, right?

C. Weicker: Sold as limiting government waste.

Leffler: Yes, sold as. Right.

C. Weicker: But the rhetoric’s a lot different than reality as to how it falls.

Leffler: So these are incredibly important and heady years. Why do you leave?

2:37:48

Marriage to Lowell Weicker

C. Weicker: Well, I was divorced by then, by the end of that time, and I guess we had worked together long enough that I began seeing Senator Weicker and he asked me to marry him.

Leffler: So how long of a period of a time was that between the time of—

C. Weicker: Not long. Months.

Leffler: Really, wow. But you knew each other very well.

C. Weicker: We knew each other very well, and I didn’t always agree with him. [laughter] I still don’t. The only funny thing about it was when he actually asked me to marry him, he was very appropriate and he said, “Is there anyone I should talk to?” and I said, “You mean, like my father?” And here I was divorced with two children and he said, “Yes, I’ll talk to your father.” But I said, “Well, I actually think you should talk to Senator Hatfield.” [laughter] And he did. He went to see Mark Hatfield and told him. So at any rate—

Leffler: So you stopped working because—

C. Weicker: I stopped working. I worked through 1984 and stopped right after because it was January.

Leffler: You had all these children to take care of.

C. Weicker: I had all these children to take care of, and blending a family to worry about, and if you asked me today, do I miss it? Absolutely.

Leffler: Sure.

C. Weicker: Even today. Those years in the Congress were really great, and I particularly in those last several years felt that we really made a difference. There are not many people who actually get the satisfaction of doing that, because the legislative process outside of Appropriations is so ponderous and it can take years to—I mean, Ribicoff worked I don’t know for how many years to try to get the Department of Education bill passed, and it was only when Jimmy Carter endorsed it in his presidential campaign that he knew it was going to happen.

Leffler: It’s really the alignment of the positions that allow you ultimately to make that difference. That’s what I’m understanding from all of this.

C. Weicker: Right.

Leffler: Having the right people on your staff, working with the other right people, but also just having the authority between serving on the Appropriations Committee and the Authorizing Committee at the same time there is a kind of juncture of possibilities.

C. Weicker: For him that certainly was, and also, to be honest with you, I think it was a very important period of time, also, for Lowell, because up until the time the Republicans took control of the Senate, Lowell was never one to really engage in leadership activities of the Republican Party. He was always a very independent operator. I’m sure he’ll go over all of this with you, but I think that what happened—people began to see something other than the so-called maverick who was willing to take the Senate floor on an issue that may not be popular, i.e., to stand up against a prayer in school amendment and to be willing to hold the floor indefinitely until people understood what it meant. That seemed like such an apple pie issue.

People had always seen him as that maverick or the gadfly, the unpredictable member. You weren’t quite sure how he was going to come out, and if he could mess you up, it just might happen. But he never abandoned certain beliefs, and on Appropriations and Small Business, as well, I suppose, on all these committees, but more so on Appropriations, he had very important responsibilities to manage and he did it really well. He was seen as an administrator, as a leader. He had to lead a committee of, first, a subcommittee of seven maybe on State, Justice, and far more than that on Labor, Health & Human Services, but get the consent of 29 people on a full committee, the majority of 29 people on a full committee, to move anything forward, and I think that was really—

Those were his finest hours, because he was working against his own President frequently, and he wasn’t making friends within the administration on many of—

Leffler: Indeed.

C. Weicker: —his proposals, and yet he was following through, I think, on something that he also had had engrained in him, which was it’s not necessarily his constituents in Greenwich who needed his help, but it was the poor people in Bridgeport, which he has said, obviously.

Leffler: Bridgeport. Yes, indeed, he has.

C. Weicker: That’s where it all came together, and in order to do it, he had to be out front and he had to take a lot of political risks, and my feeling is part of the reason why he lost in 1988 is not because people didn’t respect him, but his brand of politics became tiring because it was so in-your-face all the time.

2:44:36

Thoughts on Lowell Weicker’s legacy

Leffler: What do you think he will be most remembered for?

C. Weicker: I think the disabilities legislation.

Leffler: The Americans with Disabilities Act? Or—because he wasn’t actually in the Senate when that passes—

C. Weicker: No, but he wrote it.

Leffler: I know.

C. Weicker: He wrote it, but I think it’s the accumulation of all of it. I don’t know if he told you that the President asked him to be on that National Council of Disabilities and he turned it down.

Leffler: Our current President?

C. Weicker: Yes. He did. He turned it down, for a variety of reasons. Not because he doesn’t want to serve the President, it just was a matter of timing at this point. So he is well known for his activities on disabilities. I think he will be forever remembered for the role he played in Watergate, and at some point history might remember the role he played on preserving these important social programs.

Leffler: And the environment, right? That was an important initiative as well.

C. Weicker: And the environment.

Leffler: That was an important initiative as well, I think, in terms of—

C. Weicker: It is. Unfortunately, people still don’t pay enough attention to the oceans, and I think water is such an important factor going out. We have to deal with it. So that’s where I see his record.

Leffler: So the social programs, but specifically education programs, research for health issues, disabilities, and the early stand he took for Watergate.

C. Weicker: Watergate, and I don’t know that people will remember constitutional issues from years ago.

Leffler: Maureen Byrnes in the interview I did with her really pushed that point.

C. Weicker: The constitutional issues?

Leffler: Right. The way in which he valued the Constitution and the constitutional issues piece.

C. Weicker: I don’t know. I think if people remember, if people care, and that’s where I have, I think, less faith with the public at large. Again, it’s just great to be able to say we have prayer in school, but it’s honestly a question of what prayer. I had an experience as a child—my best friend was a Jewish girl who lived around the corner who went to the public school. We were playing one day and she said, “I know what prayer you say,” and she proceeded to recite the Protestant version of “The Lord’s Prayer,” and I said, “But that’s not what prayer I say.” And she said, “Yes, it is.” I said, “No, it’s not. But why do you know this?” And she says, “Because we have to say it every day in school.” And I, as a seven-year-old, it bothered me.

Leffler: You thought that was wrong, right?

C. Weicker: Her grandmother had been through the Holocaust and had been in a concentration camp and still had the tattoo on her arms, and I once in a while would go over there. Her grandmother was Orthodox and I would turn on the lights for her. Anyway, but that’s an aside. They are important.

I don’t disagree with Maureen. I think the thing about the constitutional issues—the Supreme Court guaranteed that women can have an abortion. The Republicans, the conservatives, took it away from a woman who was poor, took that right away because she can’t afford to pay for it.

The Supreme Court said that there are remedies for desegregating schools. The Congress tried to take away some of those remedies. And then obviously there’s the prayer issue, and so I think these were important times because all of these issues were put out there as Christian issues belonging to our Judeo-Christian heritage.

Leffler: Not busing.

C. Weicker: Not busing. Well, busing had a different kind of justification, that it was somehow immoral to take little children on long bus trips away from their homes.

Leffler: That’s true. Right. I’d forgotten that piece.

2:50:18

Jacob Javits gives public tribute to Lowell Weicker

C. Weicker: And that these judges are terrible and insensitive and Lowell did stand up for that and in 1984, I’m pretty sure it was 1984, Lowell was invited to Jacob Javits’s 80th birthday.

Leffler: Oh, right. I know this story, but please go ahead and tell it.

C. Weicker: And it wasn’t a big birthday. I went with him. It wasn’t a big birthday luncheon. It was at “21,” and people like Douglas Fairbanks and Barbara Walters were there. It was very close family and close personal friends, and after all of the comments by members of his family and his former law partner he responded, and he began his response—I mean, 90 percent of his response—but I’ll never forget the way he began it. This is a man in a wheelchair who literally is gasping after each partial sentence, who began by saying, “No one understands how cold a place the Senate can be when you choose to stand alone than Lowell Weicker.”

Leffler: That’s beautiful.

C. Weicker: And he went on to talk about Lowell’s courage and his conviction in standing up on issues that other people would run from, and it was incredibly moving.

I looked at Lowell and he had tears coming down his cheeks because it was the least expected and it was just amazingly powerful, but that’s the role he assumed, whether it was in support of poor women getting abortions or being able to distribute clean needles to drug addicts so that we wouldn’t spread AIDS or to have an AIDS education program. Most of the people in the Senate were terrified to vote for these things. The votes were frequently 92 to 2, Lowell and Daniel Patrick Moynihan the only two people who’d be willing to stand up for clean needles.

Leffler: Well, Ted Kennedy perhaps in terms of supporting—

C. Weicker: Oh, yes, the support did come later. I mean, 92 to 6 sometimes. [laughter] And then later, apartheid was amazing. Talk about something that no one expected to see in our lifetime, apartheid government of South Africa collapsing, and Lowell led the Republican opposition to Reagan’s veto of the sanctions legislation, Lowell and Ted Kennedy, and then Lowell is invited to go and see Nelson Mandela sworn in.

I sat next to Nelson Mandela at Bill Clinton’s inauguration. Lowell was up on the dais and I was sitting in the audience and this nice looking man came and sat next to me and there was a woman with him who turned out was his staffer. When I realized who it was, I turned to him and I introduced myself and I said, “I’m Lowell Weicker’s wife,” and he remembered. He said, “Yes, I know the role your husband played with Senator Kennedy.” And I said, “My husband collects autographs. Would you please sign my program from the Clinton inauguration?” So that is up on the wall. “To Lowell.”

Leffler: One last question. You know this man very well. Where do you think his courage came from, or comes from, to stand up to these issues without fear of personal consequence?

C. Weicker: I think part of it is genetic. I think it’s genes and I think it’s the way he was probably raised. He was raised in a very privileged family, and yet from what I understand, his father absolutely instilled in him, and his mother as well, that there are responsibilities, that if you are privileged, you also have responsibilities to society. I mean, his father volunteering when he was too old and too tall for the Army Air Force during World War II and serving out five years in England, or four years, and giving up the presidency of Squibb to do that. And his mother, whose father was a British general, obviously knew the role of responsibility and service to country, and his mother was a great fan of Franklin Roosevelt. Obviously Lowell was, as well, growing up through World War II, so I think that.

I think that he was raised by a family and learned growing up early that there’s nothing you cannot do. I think that was inbred, but yet you’re expected to do certain things, and it was always a matter of giving back. He wasn’t always moderate. He was pretty conservative when he came to Congress as a congressman. The thing that always horrifies me is that he introduced or he co-sponsored that resolution of Gerald Ford’s to impeach William O. Douglas.

Leffler: Oh, really?

C. Weicker: Yes, you should ask him about that. He did. And there he was, sort of a young congressman, and—

Leffler: Quickly overshadowed by Watergate.

C. Weicker: Well, quickly overshadowed, I think, by the realities of the people who came from outside of his immediate neighborhood, the people in Bridgeport and the people who really did need help, and also being from Connecticut, it’s a very diverse ethnic constituency with lots of blue collar, minority population, older cities that are failing. You begin to see, to take a good look at that. Not everybody works on Wall Street and lives in Greenwich.

So when I did marry him, he drove me to the town hall to register to vote and he said, “I’m not going to go in with you.” He said, “The only thing I will tell you, Claudia, is that I have a lot of problems with the Republican Party.” So I did for the first time ever register as a Republican, but as soon as he lost in 1988, I went back to being a Democrat. [laughter]

Leffler: I guess you didn’t have too much choice, did you?

C. Weicker: No. It was the only thing he’s ever really indicated that I should do. [laughter]

Leffler: Well, thank you so much for all this time. This has really been—

C. Weicker: Well, it’s good. It’s good for me, too.

Leffler: It’s wonderful to have your impressions on tape.

C. Weicker: I would like to take a look if you can get at any of the reports.

Leffler: I will try to do that, and maybe at some point we’ll sit down again, just more briefly.

C. Weicker: Can you access Lexis or the Library of Congress?

Leffler: Yes, we can.

C. Weicker: You can?

Leffler: Yes, I’m sure we can. Whether you can do this from home or whether I need to do it for you, I don’t really know, but—

C. Weicker: Because that’s probably the way for me to do it.

Leffler: Well, in any case, let me just say that perhaps we should find a time before this project is done just to round things out for you to talk some about your role as the first lady of Connecticut, if you’d like to. If not, that’s okay, but, anyway, if we do that, we’ll reserve it for another day. So for right now, I’m going to say thank you again, and that will officially end this portion of the interview.

C. Weicker: Great.
