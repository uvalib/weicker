Leffler: Today is January 11, 2011. This is Phyllis Leffler, and I’m sitting in the home of Governor Lowell Weicker for the fifth of our interviews in the oral history series related to the Lowell Weicker Papers, and today I would like to focus on the gubernatorial years. Even though the papers don’t really focus on that, I think it’s really important in terms of the project to try to round out your larger recollections.

Yesterday I was going through what we had done in the past, and actually in our first interview we had traced out your career and talked about the various stages, so I don’t think we have to talk too much about the election process, except that I do have a couple of questions that I’d like to put on the table before we actually talk about governing as Governor. The first one was about the creation of an Independent Party and how that worked for the campaign itself in the process of getting elected as Governor.

0:01:24

Weicker’s reasons for running for Governor as an Independent

L. Weicker: Well, having been persuaded that I should run for Governor by friends of mine, the only question that remained is how would I do it, and basically would I run as a Republican or would I run as an Independent? I had neither the time nor the money nor I believed the support within the Republican Party to follow down the road of a Republican convention and primary. Don’t forget it was the same Republican Party that basically had defeated me in the 1988 election, and the party hadn’t changed. Indeed, I would say it had become much more in the hands of the conservatives of Connecticut and more particularly, William Buckley and John Rowland.

I certainly felt I had enough friends within the party that I could get the necessary percentage in a convention that enabled me to primary. I was less sure of whether I could win the primary. In any event, even less sure that I had the finances to go through that whole process, and then assuming I won, get into a general election, so that was my analysis, and on the basis of that analysis, I said, “Look, let’s just go for it, go directly onto the ballot. I knew I could get enough signatures within the state to qualify to go on the ballot, and as facts proved out, we more than tripled, I think I’m right on that, the number of signatures that were required to get on the ballot. Claudia, what did he say?

C. Weicker: Ten times, I think.

L. Weicker: Ten times.

C. Weicker: I think that we needed ten thousand and you had over—

L. Weicker: It was, yes.

C. Weicker: —a hundred thousand. It’s all somewhere in the records, but it’s pretty close.

L. Weicker: And not only that, it proved to be a terrific part of the election itself because we introduced me to the state. It got people very excited at the grassroots level, and I knew that town committees—it made no difference whether they were Republican town committees or Democratic town committees, are worthless. They serve one function and one function only—to send people to conventions, but then after the conventions are over and they’ve had their butts kissed by whoever the aspiring politicians were, they’d go home and sit on their hands for the election, whereas if you could rouse the general public, they made a hell of an effort on your behalf.

C. Weicker: Can I add something?

L. Weicker: Yes.

Leffler: Please.

C. Weicker: You don’t mind? The day that Lowell announced that he was going to run as an Independent the telephone at home started ringing. We never had an unlisted telephone number, and it started ringing. We kept a yellow pad, like a legal pad, by the phone and filled it within a couple of days with names and telephone numbers of people who were calling our home to volunteer. It was absolutely extraordinary, and honestly, it’s the only time that we had to unlist our telephone number because it never stopped ringing. It was unbelievable the outpouring.

Leffler: So these were people who were committed to you because of your prior work and it didn’t really matter what party you were.

L. Weicker: Right. I think that’s correct.

Leffler: And that sort of proves the point of why the third party—

L. Weicker: Of why we went in that direction, but its value, as I say, extended way beyond just getting on the ballot. This formed the core of our election when they stayed with us to work for the election. Probably the closest I could recollect to this type of an effort was [Barack] Obama’s effort and how it resonated with the young people and minorities, and nobody could prove that better than right here in the back door of Charlottesville, my youngest son, Andrew [Weicker], was part of the Obama team that was assigned Fluvanna County—

Leffler: That’s a challenge.

L. Weicker: And Fluvanna County, yes, you hit it, a challenge, a big Republican repository of votes. Well, Andrew and some of his young friends from the University and within that group many blacks, many blacks outside the University, they were the ones that canvassed Fluvanna. Well, as it turned out, Obama lost I think by a couple of hundred votes in Fluvanna. Unheard of. Normally carried big margins by Republicans. It’s not so much the result, it’s the enthusiasm that is brought to the table, and when that enthusiasm is lacking, then you can’t accomplish it, because nothing proved it better than the subsequent election with Creigh Deeds or whatever, and that group didn’t turn out at all. That is a classic example of what happens if you can really rev up people to get involved in politics.

That was the decision. Why the name A Connecticut Party? Very simple: because that automatically puts you at the top of the ballot by using “A.”

Leffler: Oh. It was just a strategy.

L. Weicker: It was a strategy, right, to put us at the top of the ballot and not somewhere way down below.

Leffler: But then fast-forward a couple of years. You then made some efforts to create a national Independence Party.

L. Weicker: That was separate and apart, because you could not—I knew when I was Governor that I wouldn’t have the opportunity to engage in politics on behalf of A Connecticut Party, because if I did that, I’d lose any chance of getting majorities in the legislature, since the legislature was Republican or Democrat. If they saw me out there doing a thing on behalf of A Connecticut Party, that would decrease their likelihood of joining with me in legislative attempts, and that’s why I couldn’t do it. If I had done it, probably Eunice Groark could have been elected when she ran, but I didn’t and Eunice knew I didn’t, and it was the right thing to do in terms of governing the state.

The matter of the Independent Party nationally, that was something entirely different. That was along with I guess Jesse Ventura and others that were making efforts to have a run at the presidency. It maybe would have had legs had not Donald Trump entered the scene, and then all of a sudden everybody says, “Well, maybe we ought to think about him. He’s got a lot of money,” which is true, but certainly as far as the individual is concerned, there was nobody that I wanted to be associated with.

Having gotten on the ballot, having won as an Independent, made no further real attempt to energize a viable third party within Connecticut. Ironically, just last month in the general elections, the chairman of A Connecticut Party, a woman by the name of Diane Blick who was a good friend of mine from northwest Connecticut, was elected probate judge in her own right for the towns that she represented, so that was sort of a reminder to me as to what we used to be at one time. So that, I think, pretty well answers the question as to how we did it, why we did it—

Leffler: Can I just—

L. Weicker: —and then why I had to cease doing it once becoming Governor.

Leffler: Why did you choose Eunice Groark as your running mate?

L. Weicker: Good question. I didn’t know Eunice. Never met her. Two people were brought to me as being the most viable lieutenant governor candidates, and contrary to Rachel Pierce’s comment, she mentioned the name of the mayor of Hartford, I think Carrie Saxon Perry was her name. I never considered her. Two people that were considered were a woman legislator by the name of Edith Prague—

Leffler: Can you spell that for me?

L. Weicker: Sure. P-R-A-G-U-E—from southeastern Connecticut and a veteran legislator, and Eunice Groark, who was general counsel to the city government of Hartford. I felt that what we needed was not the old, if you will, but we needed the new, and having met her, I thought she was an extraordinarily bright young lady and could really bring some energy to the ticket and also some smarts to the ticket. She was a very, very intelligent person that thoroughly knew the issues that related to Hartford, and if you knew those, you knew every city in the state of Connecticut, because they’re all the same problems. So it was Eunice. Eunice had been suggested to me by Peter Gold, who was one of my campaign managers, and Tom also knew her, Tom D’Amore, so that was my choice.

Leffler: And that turned out, obviously, to be a good choice.

L. Weicker: A hell of a choice. As a matter of fact, I’ve always regretted the fact that the state of Connecticut went back to its partisan ways in the subsequent election. By every poll, Eunice was viewed as being the smartest, having the most integrity, most common sense, all of those types of values, and yet she always came in third in the poll being an Independent, not as a Republican or a Democrat, and when the results came in, that’s what the results said, the Republican and the Democrat winning the nomination but, by God, can you imagine where we would be today had Eunice been the Governor and we didn’t have to go through the corruption of the [John] Rowland years and the profligacy of the Democratic legislature during those Rowland years. Between the corruption and the profligacy, believe me, there wasn’t much of a state left by the time the recently departed Governor, [M. Jodi] Rell, took over.

Leffler: Okay. Let me just ask a very broad overarching question and let you take it where you would like to. What are you most proud of, of those four years when you were Governor? What do you think are the biggest accomplishments?

0:14:12

Weicker reflects on accomplishments as Governor

L. Weicker: Well, I think there would be three answers there, really, and it would be hard for me to grade those. I succeeded not at all in one, partly in another, and fairly comprehensively in the other. I would consider my greatest achievement in which I didn’t achieve anything was to try to implement the court ruling of [Milo] Sheff v. [William] O’Neill, bringing the quality of educational opportunity to all the children of Connecticut. I didn’t succeed because even though I had set the train on the track and it was starting to move down the track, as soon as I left office it was totally ditched and I couldn’t achieve it during my term of office. There were two big—

Leffler: Was this the idea of creating these regional districts then through—

L. Weicker: Well yes, as was later affirmed by my presence on the Century Fund Commission on Educational Opportunity, funded by the Century Fund, the segregation that we had in Connecticut was no different than it was at that time anywhere in the United States. It was economic segregation. It wasn’t racial. That doesn’t mean to say there aren’t vestiges of that, and that certainly relates to the economics. It’s economic, and it is today, also. It still is. And how do you get around that? Well, all I knew was that if anybody could do it, a small state like Connecticut could do it, and I wanted to get that on track to have the local communities, and I realized it was important that any solution should start at the local community level and then to the legislature and then to the Governor. Otherwise, the whole thing would be blown up at any given moment and we were in a very—we started off well.

In other words, where I said to the boards of education in the state, “Look, you tell us how to do it. You tell us. We’re not going to come down with a great pronouncement imposing something on you.” I knew the minute we did that we’d be in court. That would be the end of the effort. Rather have the local communities and the regions and the districts give us their ideas, and that was well down the track, including the kids themselves. I wanted to make it a grassroots effort. Well, as I said, I only got to the point where we got suggestions from the local communities, and then I was out of office and then nothing further to place on that score. So I didn’t succeed, but I have to say to you that I would still be right today. I mean, today is 2011.

We started this effort in 1990, roughly 1993 would be the last two years of my administration, so the time has gone by and nothing’s changed and we still have that economic segregation, so it didn’t succeed.

Secondly would be the health care provisions. We tried to get a program going for health care in the state of Connecticut, especially among young people. That continues today to some degree in some of the programs in the state, so at least it was a recognition of the problem that health care was an issue. Now we know it’s a huge issue, but nothing being done at the national level, even though Hillary Clinton made her effort. We figure we should least get started on it in Connecticut, and I’m proud of the fact I did that, because to me, it was then and is now a huge issue confronting both the state, obviously, and the country, and we’re still not through those woods. I think we’ll get there. I’m optimistic. But what’s going to happen with the present legislation is that there will be aspects of it that will be amended but it will stay on track and we’ll have a system in the United States rather than the ad hoc type of care that is now doled out in one form or the other.

Thirdly was the matter of disability, and most particularly, of mental disability, where I closed the training school at Mansfield, training school being the word for the warehousing of the mentally disabled and challenged. In closing that down and also implementing other ways, in other words, what became eventually the Americans With Disabilities Act, but we paid attention to that community where no attention had been paid in the past.

0:19:49

Establishing state income tax in Connecticut

L. Weicker: You probably wonder, Well, Governor, the thing that you’re most known for you don’t mention—the income tax. Well, to me, it’s not that I’m afraid to mention it. I’m very proud of the fact that we took a very regressive tax system and made it progressive. Of that, I’m proud. But that’s only something that should’ve been done. It was just a kick in the face to poorer people, and I say poorer, not poor, but the poorer. I know that it’s received a lot of negatives, but that said, I think most of the negatives came from the middle income earners in Connecticut, since they, in effect, were excluded from the capital gains and dividends tax which we eliminated, and they had no income tax, so literally all they had was a sales tax, and yet that was numerically the biggest constituency that was going tax-free in the state, and that’s where most of the anger was generated.

Leffler: But on the other hand, you put the state on a path so that you were generating surpluses rather than huge deficits.

L. Weicker: That’s right.

Leffler: Why wouldn’t you be proud of that? It seems to me it generated money to be able to do other good things within the state.

L. Weicker: I put it in the same category, Phyllis, as Watergate. I’m so much prouder of my work on behalf of the NIH [National Institutes of Health], on behalf of NOAA [National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration], on behalf of AIDS [acquired immune deficiency syndrome], on behalf of apartheid, all those things to me come ahead of Watergate, which was essentially a negative exercise.

Leffler: Yes.

L. Weicker: I considered reforming the tax system in the state essentially a negative exercise. I mean, my critics say, “All he’s going to do is to go ahead and create the income tax and they’re going to spend it.” Well, to that extent, my critics were right. They spent it to the point where right now it’s $4 billion in the hole, having spent all the income tax money and all the Indian gaming money. They just rifled through it, and the people that rifled through it for the most part were the biggest opponents of it, i.e., John Rowland and leaders in the legislature.

Leffler: What did they rifle through it for?

L. Weicker: Oh, Christ. Everything that the public thinks politicians rifle through monies for. Their own pet projects, just sort of buying their elections, giving money to constituents in whatever form.

Leffler: But surely, an income tax—I understand the larger point you’re making, but surely an income tax that generates a large amount of money, those monies can then be used for things like better health care and education.

L. Weicker: Sure, if you have an enlightened leadership. Yes, absolutely, it can.

Leffler: And you don’t feel those monies were ever used in that way.

L. Weicker: Oh, I think it’s been horrendous. I mean, look where we are, what we’re confronting now. When I went into office, when I was elected—you’ve got to grant me leeway on this, but I’m pretty sure. Actually, on Election Day I think we had about a $100 million deficit somewhere, or thereabouts. By the time I was sworn in, that amount was doubled, and then doubled again even before I got my feet on the ground, so let’s generously use a figure of a billion. The state today is $4 billion.

The incumbent Governor, Dannel Malloy, I’d like to know what he’s going to do to get out of the hole. He can’t impose an income tax because that’s been done. He can raise it, but he can’t impose it. He can’t go ahead and redo the Indian gaming deals. They’re done, so where’s he going to get it? Well, he’s going to have to get it by cutting spending and raising taxes, which is not going to be a very easy task. But—what was the thought on my mind there?

Leffler: This is all part of your explaining of why you don’t consider the income tax—

L. Weicker: No, I don’t consider it—

Leffler: So what you’re saying is that there wasn’t real fiscal responsibility that was exercised.

L. Weicker: No. There was none, there really wasn’t, and as I said, as I repeated, when I left— Oh, I know what I wanted to say. So for years after I left the governorship people would come up and say, “Oh, you’re the guy that went ahead and imposed the income tax.” Finally this year during the election I heard that a couple of times in places I had appeared. I had the Tea Party, that’s right. They went ahead and picketed me when I had a couple of radio interviews. I turned to the guys, I said, “Listen, you’ve had 19 years to repeal it. You haven’t repealed it. You spent it. So I don’t want to be carrying the blame anymore on this thing.” If it was no good, they could have gotten rid of it right away, especially having elected a Governor who was dedicated to no income tax, John Rowland, so why didn’t they do it? They had too much fun spending it, and now, of course, the tax had been raised by the previous Governor and I’m sure it’s going to have to be raised again by this Governor.

You can’t run a state on the sales tax unless you want to have the alternative that confronted me, which was had I balanced the budget on the sales tax when I took office, we would have to raise the sales tax in Connecticut to 13 percent, and that was just unheard of in those days, so that’s not the solution.

No. We’re where we ought to be, and I’m glad that I put that system into place. I’m sure there are a lot of people that are still pissed off. They ought to look at themselves and not at others. They have every right to say we don’t trust those in government to prudently handle the monies. I can’t argue against that because they didn’t prudently handle it, but they have no right to say that the system is wrong. The system is right. The people are the ones that are wrong.

0:26:27

Health care accomplishments as Governor

Leffler: Could you be a little more specific about the health care accomplishments? You said you’re very proud of those.

L. Weicker: Boy, that’s one thing. You know, I should, and I’m trying to think who can give you a better read on all aspects of that. Probably Sue Addiss, who was my public health commissioner. She’s at Yale University and lives in New Haven.

Leffler: Well, maybe I could get up there.

L. Weicker: Or call her. I’m trying to think of who else would be—Al Solnit, who was my mental health commissioner, would’ve been ideal, too, because our plan included not just physical disability but mental disability. He’s dead.

Leffler: So when you talk about health care, you’re really talking about expanding—

L. Weicker: Modest programs that took care of the most vulnerable—children. Now that program has been continued. I don’t know what they call it now. It was called SustiNet, I think, but that’s why I need somebody to carry the ball on this one for me because it was very modest, but we wanted to get a start on it. I think at the same time, if I’m not mistaken, I endorsed Senator [Hillary Rodham] Clinton coming forth with national health care.

I know Ted Kennedy and I put in the first bill on health care. First let me say this. If there are people that you want to get a hold of, and not only get a hold of, but you have not interviewed and you should interview them, there is a fellow by the name of Tom Dudchik. Tom Dudchik was on my staff as a United States Senator. He then went with me to Hartford, where he was a deputy in a couple of agencies and eventually deputy chief of staff. He literally gave up his college career for me and didn’t go to college until I left the governorship. He has managed to put together the most comprehensive Web site on Connecticut government and politics. There’s nothing like it in the state of Connecticut. There’s nothing like it in most states, as a matter of fact. [Weicker looks for Web site and gives Leffler Dudchik’s phone number] It’s called Capitol Report, and it just covers all aspects of Connecticut government and politics.

Leffler: And you think he could—

L. Weicker: He can do two things for you. Number one, he would be a font of information as to my years, both in the Senate and in the governorship, and he has kept close track of the whereabouts of ex-staffers. [discussion of connections to Sue Addiss omitted] Clearly he’s somebody you really should talk to because he does span both areas.

Leffler: Okay. So accomplishments—you’re listing education, health care, disability legislation, and we talked some about the income tax. What would you say are your biggest disappointments from those years as Governor?

L. Weicker: I think I’ve answered that question with somebody else. It would be the failure to achieve education reform. I think we should have been able to do that. I think the reason why we didn’t was that my normal allies on the Democratic side of the aisle just were plain tired from the income tax battle and they were not prepared to take on a controversial issue like economic desegregation. In other words, one was enough in any four years. I can understand it. I’m not blaming them in any way. I can understand that.

0:31:35

Gambling and gaming in Connecticut

Leffler: The records are very clear about your disagreement with the gambling policies and establishing gaming in Connecticut.

L. Weicker: Right. Right.

Leffler: You’re on the record in various interviews making the distinction between gaming and family values, for example. Could you just talk about that?

L. Weicker: Oh, sure, I’d be glad to go ahead and talk about it. As you know, before I took office, the Indian group, being the Mashantucket Pequots, had won a decision in federal court that allowed them to do the same thing that was done by the residents of the state of Connecticut. You say to me, “Well, Lowell, what’s that?” We had a law in Connecticut called Monte Carlo Night, and you could have table games in any institution, for a one night a year have regular gambling table games, so basically the federal court said that if that’s okay for the people of the state of Connecticut, then it’s also okay for the Indian tribe. What it doesn’t include, obviously, is the biggest moneymaker, slot machines. It just includes craps, blackjack, all those kinds of games. No video poker. No slot machines.

The Indians were very happy with that decision by the federal courts, and when I took office I was confronted with having to choose between—well, let me track back. After I took office, a man by the name of Charlie Duffy who was an attorney for the Indians came to my assistant chief of staff, Stan Twardy. Stan had been with me in Washington. He was a brilliant young lawyer. “Claudia, did Stan go to the U.S. Attorney’s office between Washington and Hartford or after Hartford?”

C. Weicker: [in other room] He was U.S. Attorney.

Leffler: He was U.S. Attorney, she said.

L. Weicker: No. When did he get appointed U.S.— ?

C. Weicker: [in other room] After he was attorney—

L. Weicker: I’m sorry.

C. Weicker: [in other room] What?

L. Weicker: Was Stan U.S. Attorney—

C. Weicker: [in room] —for the state of Connecticut.

L. Weicker: I know. When? Between Washington and Hartford or after Hartford?

C. Weicker: Oh, no. Between Washington and Hartford.

L. Weicker: Okay, so he was U.S. Attorney also. Duffy approached Stan and said, “Look, is there any way that we can compromise? We’re prepared to take this whole matter back into federal court looking for slot machines.” And Stan came and, “We want to know if there’s any way that we could effectuate a compromise without having to go to the lawsuit.” I told Stan, I said, “Geez, I don’t like the whole gambling idea, but I’ll think about it,” and I didn’t like the whole idea. I just didn’t. I was one of those that was disappointed in the federal court decision and did not want to have gambling either in the form of table games or more sophisticated forms such as slot machines. Then it was indicated to me that they would be glad if they could have slot machines to give a substantial sum off the top.

Leffler: Back to the state?

L. Weicker: Back to the state, and no strings attached. Well, that really gave me pause, because what if we went to court and we lost again? Then they were in place—without having to pay a nickel.

Leffler: Sure.

L. Weicker: And I think that what I had to do was to go ahead and resolve in my own mind my own feelings for the matter and my instincts as to what would be the best for the state of Connecticut. I’m going to get back to this in a minute to show you my continued opposition to the gambling. So we struck a deal, and giving them slot machines, and I think the take was somewhere around 25 percent. It might be 23, 24, or 25 percent, somewhere in there, which has proven to be a huge revenue boom to the state of Connecticut.

Now, along came the Mohegans, and they had been recognized as a tribe. At that stage of the game, the Mashantuckets could have proven to be a big obstacle because we, in effect, had given them exclusive right to gambling. The Mashantuckets figured it’s in our interest to share with our fellow Indian tribe and they did, and so they said it’s okay.

Leffler: Why was it in their interest to share?

L. Weicker: I suppose in their interest of—

Leffler: supporting Native American—

L. Weicker: supporting Native Americans or whatever. They didn’t put up any—

Leffler: fight.

L. Weicker: —opposition here at all. They said, “Look, it’s something we want to go ahead and do.” So they did, and obviously between the two of them it’s meant enormous revenues to the state. Along comes the man that I mentioned before on the Independent presidential movement, Donald Trump, and he in effect says, “Listen, hey, this is all a joke. These Indians are not Indians. They’re blacks.” There’s no question about the fact— Yes, Claudia.

C. Weicker: “They don’t look like Indians to me.” [quoting Donald Trump]

L. Weicker: Yes, well—

Leffler: Is that what he said?

C. Weicker: Yes.

Leffler: Really? I don’t remember that.

L. Weicker: Oh, sure. Because they were black. Well, most of them. Claudia—

C. Weicker: To [Richard] Skip Hayward.

L. Weicker: Because what had happened in the evolution of all this is both the blacks and the Indians were excluded from American society.

Leffler: Of course, and so there had been a lot of—

L. Weicker: And so there’d been a lot of intermarriage. “Well,” he said, “they don’t look like Indians to me,” so he said, “I want to open up a casino in Bridgeport, Connecticut.” And, boy, all the town fathers of Bridgeport thought this was a great idea, including a man by the name of Ernie Trefz who had been a good friend of mine and who owned just about all the McDonald’s, in Connecticut. He thought this would be super for Bridgeport. I said to Ernie and to the fathers of Bridgeport and the mayor and everybody else, “No. We’ve got to rebuild Bridgeport, but we’re not doing it on the back of gambling.” It just isn’t the way to go. It never has been the way to go where an economy depends only on gambling. There’s not much to show for it, really, thinking specifically of Atlantic City, and now the way that had gone. In any event, I denied that, that a state would not, because that would have to be a state issue.

Do you follow what I’m talking about? Not Indians, but the state would then have to officially get into the gambling business, and I said no. That’s what sort of reaffirmed my initial position on gambling, having accommodated to the position that was thrust upon me by the federal courts and also then by the fiscal needs of the state of Connecticut. So that’s the story on gambling, and it’s proven to be a very successful story. I think others have since, once again, tried to open the door to gambling in Bridgeport. I don’t think it’s had much success.

Weicker: A very nice little lady turned to Andrew, and how adults are, they get down in the face of the child and ask them a question. “Now tell me: What’s it like to live here?” Andrew looked at her and said—and this is in the middle of living room, there are hundreds of people around—“Living here isn’t as nice as it looks.” [laughter]

Leffler: And he was no doubt right.

C. Weicker: It’s true, and so out of the mouths of babes—so you have your ups and downs. There are good days and bad days living in the Governor’s residence.

L. Weicker: Like Sonny’s comment.

C. Weicker: Right, about the budget. It’s on tape.

L. Weicker: Did I tell you this one already?

Leffler: It’s in your book, actually.

L. Weicker: About this is “bullshit,” about the budget. What’s the plaque underneath the plaque there?

C. Weicker: This one is the Martin Luther King Award for Distinguished Service.

Leffler: When is that from?

C. Weicker: Lowell spoke on January 13, 1989. Presented to Lowell P. Weicker on the Occasion of the 60th Birthday and Fourth National Holiday in Honor of Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. Why was it 1989? I thought you spoke in 1988.

L. Weicker: I thought I spoke earlier at the Ebenezer Baptist Church. The only Republican I think who’s ever been invited to speak down there.

Leffler: Is that right, do you think?

C. Weicker: I am not so sure.

L. Weicker: I was then.

C. Weicker: Yes. Anyway, so yes, there were good days and bad.

Leffler: Sure sounds like you had your hands full. Sound like it was an amazing task between the things you were doing and the focus on the mansion and then the children. It seems like a huge—

1:27:48

Challenge of hosting official events at Governor’s residence

C. Weicker: The state had seen some financial crisis and there was no cook at the Governor’s residence. There was no cook.

Leffler: Right. I’ve read that in the book too, that there was no cook.

L. Weicker: She was the cook.

Leffler: Was that by choice or by necessity?

C. Weicker: Oh, my God, there was no way we were going to hire a cook in the middle of all the controversy over the income tax. It was by choice. I think the last—Ella Grasso had dismissed the cook when she was Governor, and the O’Neill’s [William O’Neill] who followed Grasso were a couple and they largely ate out or there were receptions that were catered at the residence.

L. Weicker: The Meskills [Thomas Meskill] never had a cook.

C. Weicker: The Meskills did have a cook, I think. No, they didn’t.

L. Weicker: No.

C. Weicker: Okay. I think [John] Dempsey was the last one to have a cook, and I gathered he had a very well-run household, so it was both by choice and by necessity that there’s no cook, and not so bad once the kitchen was redone, but in that old kitchen, I mean, literally, any time you wanted something from a freezer you had to go to the basement for ice or whatever it was and the refrigerator was a 1930s something—Crosby.

L. Weicker: Crosby.

Leffler: [laughs] That’s amazing.

C. Weicker: A double-doored little fridge. We gave a dinner party for some—the main job for Governor, to set the general policy for the state, is economic development, especially in bad times, and we gave a dinner party and had the heads of several major corporations.

Leffler: So you were the cook for those events, too? Oh my goodness.

C. Weicker: I was, and I made this dinner.

Leffler: That’s amazing to me. Not a caterer?

C. Weicker: Without a caterer. I think we had somebody come in and serve. There were about a half a dozen corporate heads from around various businesses around Connecticut, and I remember Gini Bantle, whose husband was Lou Bantle, the head of U.S. Tobacco, headquartered in Greenwich, wandered into the kitchen at one point and she went back and she said, “Lou, you have got to see where she’s cooking.” The next thing I knew Lou Bantle called and said, “How much will it cost to have a new kitchen for the Governor’s residence?” And that really started it all. That was a major contribution, but, yes, I cooked. I cooked for the family. I cooked for State Legislators during the income tax crisis. I cooked for business leaders. I cooked for cocktail receptions. That first Christmas I think we had three or four different—one for the press, one for the legislature, one for the general public.

Leffler: You didn’t do this all four years, did you?

C. Weicker: No. I didn’t do it all. After the first year and a half—I did a lot of it. I still did a lot of it, but after the first year and half, for the big, big receptions we had a caterer come in.

Leffler: That’s amazing. I should hope so. That is remarkable.

C. Weicker: But once the financial situation in the state began to improve—

1:31:42

Weicker’s position on availability of higher education

Leffler: I do have one question that I also wanted to put on the table, and it has to do with higher education, because clearly you were very focused on K-12 education. What would you say was the situation in terms of funding for higher education during those years?

L. Weicker: If I’m not mistaken, and I’d have to go ahead and review, that I wanted the state to actually get into the business of funding higher education, too.

C. Weicker: I think—

L. Weicker: I’ve always felt that.

Leffler: I’m wondering, given your close connection with John Casteen during those years he was president of the University of Connecticut.

L. Weicker: No, he’d left.

Leffler: He came to UVA in 1990.

L. Weicker: He checked out on me as president.

Leffler: That’s right, exactly.

C. Weicker: When Lowell ran.

Leffler: So he wouldn’t have overlapped with you at all during that time. But he would have while you were still in the Senate.

C. Weicker: It seems to me that Lowell—I think you would have to check on this, but it seems to me what I recall—clearly there wasn’t a great deal of funding to give to higher education at the time, but the one thing that you pushed for was more autonomy for the state university system.

L. Weicker: Just philosophically, and I can’t relate this to anything I did as Governor or Senator, I always believed quite frankly that higher education ought to be free also. I think education ought to be.

Leffler: Free.

L. Weicker: Public all the way through. You can still have your private universities, but there ought to be opportunity for any kid in America to go to college.

Leffler: My husband asked me to ask you whether the prominence of athletics for the University of Connecticut was anything you actively got started?

L. Weicker: No. N-O, no.

Leffler: [laughs]

L. Weicker: That was strictly John Rowland. We didn’t have great sports teams, and very frankly, I subscribed to the Yale theory, being a Yale man, that you go to school for academics and sports are secondary and, quite frankly, I don’t think the competition between Yale/Harvard and Yale/Princeton has diminished because we don’t have teams that go to the Rose Bowl.

Leffler: Indeed. I couldn’t agree more with that. But I think it probably was—

L. Weicker: And the best thing that could happen to the program down here is have UVA join the Ivy League.

Leffler: Here, here. I totally agree. Is there anything else you want to add in terms of what you think is important to say about these four years as Governor? Anything else we should just put on the tape for the record?

C. Weicker: Well, I think one thing that I would add, and again, I’ve alluded to this at the very beginning when we were talking about the great support that Lowell received from the public when he first announced that he was going to run for Governor. I think you have to look at the time as a time like this, as well.

People had a general sense that things were not going well in the state, that it was overspending, that there were going to be large deficits, although nobody knew quite how much, and that a change was really needed, but what I got from answering those telephone calls which he didn’t answer was they were supporting him because they really felt he could do this. They needed a strong individual to be Governor and Lowell had that reputation, and there were people who had not necessarily supported him in the Senate who were calling saying, “This is the time for somebody with guts.” They encouraged him to take this job on, so it was this real—and he likened it to the Obama situation, but I likened it to the fact that people really wanted someone who was very well disciplined and a known quantity, and he was known for being—[phone rings] People knew what they were getting. They just didn’t like the solution.

Leffler: Right. But there was a long career that built up to these years as Governor.

C. Weicker: And saying he was going to make those—yes, I mean—

Leffler: And he was going to make the tough decisions.

C. Weicker: I actually think that people knew what kind of Governor they wanted and they got the Governor that they wanted.

Leffler: But in retrospect, there was no alternative, was there?

C. Weicker: Nobody likes to pay taxes, but an awful lot of people who today even complain about the income tax actually got probably a tax break. Anyone on a fixed income certainly got a tax break. Anyone who, the seniors who were being taxed on capital gains— I don’t think people realized basically you were talking about 4 percent income tax.

Leffler: We see it today. People want services, but they don’t want to pay for them.

L. Weicker: You got it.

Leffler: So, I mean—

C. Weicker: That’s what it comes down to.

L. Weicker: You got it.

Leffler: She’s given us the right conclusion here? Is there anything—

L. Weicker: Well, as I said, I’m definitely going to talk to D’Amore and Balducci, and you’re definitely going to do that.

Leffler: I will do that.

L. Weicker: You definitely want to talk to Tom D’Amore, no question about that.

Leffler: No, I know.

L. Weicker: The other thing, Phyllis, if you go to Hartford, you certainly should drop in on the archives at the State Library. Don’t forget—they did the gubernatorial period. That was their job, and even now, we sent up a whole box full of material that somehow escaped and came down here.

Leffler: All those materials are processed and catalogued?

C. Weicker: Oh, yes, they have a very good index.

L. Weicker: It’s all done.

Leffler: Okay. Well, thank you very much. This was a terrific interview.

L. Weicker: So where do we go tomorrow? The state constitution or education specifically brought in as a state right.

Leffler: Really?

L. Weicker: Yes.

Leffler: That’s the only constitution?

C. Weicker: I better get that book out. [laughter]

L. Weicker: Well it’s right there. Yes.

Leffler: Where the state constitution literally says education is a right of the state.

L. Weicker: Yes.

Leffler: It’s just implied in all the other states?

C. Weicker: You’ll have to find it here, but the Constitution of the State of Connecticut is in here. Article 8 of education—“There shall always be free public elementary and secondary schools in the state. The General Assembly shall implement this principle by appropriate legislation. The state shall maintain a system of higher education, including the University of Connecticut, which shall be dedicated to excellence in higher education. The General Assembly shall determine size, number of terms, and method of appointment of the governing boards. The charter of Yale College as modified by agreement with the corporation thereof in pursuance of an act of the General Assembly is hereby confirmed. The fund called The School Fund shall remain a perpetual fund, the interest of which shall be enviably appropriated to the support and encouragement of the public schools throughout the state and for the equal benefit of all the people thereof. The value and amount of said fund shall be ascertained in such manner as the General Assembly may prescribe, published and recorded in the Controller’s Office, and no law shall ever be made authorizing such fund to be diverted to any other use than the encouragement and support of public schools among the several school societies as justice and equity shall require.”

Leffler: And you don’t think other state constitutions also lay out this responsibility?

L. Weicker: I’ve heard they don’t.

Leffler: Really?

L. Weicker: Check it out.

Leffler: Well, we’d have to check it out.

L. Weicker: We’ll check it out.

Leffler: Okay. Well, thank you, and I will see you tomorrow.

L. Weicker: We’ll see you tomorrow. Looking forward to it.
