[During this interview, an oxygen tank periodically dispenses in the background]

Leffler: This is Phyllis Leffler speaking. The date is March 17, 2011. I’m in Hartford, Connecticut, at the Doyle Group, and this morning I will be speaking with William J. Cibes, Jr., who was the chief budget adviser for Lowell P. Weicker when he was Governor. This is all related to the Lowell Weicker Oral History Project at the University of Virginia. Good morning. Thank you for being here.

W. Cibes: Good morning. My pleasure.

Leffler: I thought I would start out by asking you to quickly give us some information about your own background and experience before you came to work with the Governor.

0:00:48

Political background of Cibes before joining Weicker’s administration

W. Cibes: Okay. I was born in Kansas, attended the University of Kansas, and went to graduate school at Princeton. Then came to teach at Connecticut College in 1969 and moved through the ranks there to full professor. Along about 1972 or so I was convinced, actually by a member of the city council, that you couldn’t teach politics, which was my field, without actually being involved in politics and learning from the ground up. Since I was a farm boy from Kansas, she told me that you’ve got to get involved in the urban scene if you’re going to be doing that kind of work, so I became active in the local Democratic Party.

I contemplated running for the State representative in 1974, but in a deal concocted by my local ward chairman, was appointed to the local board of education instead of running for the State rep. When the State rep died in 1977 I ran and lost a special election but was elected in 1978 to a full term. I served in the House of Representatives in Connecticut for 12 years. Along the way I was deputy speaker, and when Rich Balducci became speaker in 1989, he appointed me as chair of the finance, revenue & bonding committee.

I had been active during this period of time while teaching at Connecticut College and also was involved in the State House, active in Governor Bill O’Neill’s campaign in 1986, and was involved with what he for a time persuaded everybody was going to be his re-election campaign in 1990. It became evident to me in the fall of 1989 that his heart was not in it and he probably would not run, so I began developing a campaign to run for Governor in the Democratic primary.

0:03:12

Cibes outspoken on support of Connecticut income tax

W. Cibes: On the day he announced that he would not run I announced that I would, which threw a monkey wrench in the plans of the Democratic Party, I think, but I did it primarily on the grounds of the need to rationalize the state’s tax structure, which had been cobbled together over many years in the absence of a personal income tax. With my academic background and so forth I was persuaded that there needed to be that leg of the stool of the revenue structure, so despite my attempts to make it clear that this was only part of an overall program, I was certainly tagged with the idea that this guy was going to just institute an income tax.

I did relatively well at the convention. I got enough to primary. I lost the primary, as you know, and was going back to teaching, and in late October or so I heard through the grapevine that if Lowell Weicker were elected Governor, he’d like me to be part of his administration, really third-hand, fourth-hand rumors. But right after the election, Tom D’Amore called and I met with the Governor and he asked what I’d like to do and I said, “I’d like to be secretary of OPM [Office of Policy and Management].” Well, he wasn’t quite sure about that because here was this income tax over there [laughs].

Leffler: Indeed.

W. Cibes: So I said, “Well, Governor, obviously, if I’m appointed I’d follow your direction.” You have to do that. He said, “But I think despite what you’ve said about the income tax, adopting an income tax would be like throwing gasoline on a fire.”

Leffler: I know he said that, yes, on August 13, 1990. I have that in my notes here.

W. Cibes: [laughs] I said, “Despite that, I think it’s got to be part of the mix.” He said, “Well, maybe this is something we’re going to get into later,” but basically he said, “You’ve got to do what I — my aim is to cut as much as I can from the budget.”

Leffler: But let’s hold up on that for just a moment.

W. Cibes: Okay.

Leffler: Tell me a little bit about your relationship with Tom D’Amore. I know from Lowell Weicker’s book that he’s published, called Maverick; he writes in that book that it was Tom D’Amore who recommended you to Weicker for this position, so I think that’s now part of the public record, since it’s in the book. [laughs]

W. Cibes: Well, that’s probably true. Yes.

Leffler: So how did you know and interact with Tom D’Amore?

W. Cibes: About at the same level I interacted with Lowell Weicker, which was to say not.

Leffler: Really? Interesting.

W. Cibes: I first met the Governor on the campaign trail marching in a parade in Tolland, and I think that may have been one of the few times I’d even talked with him on the campaign trail. I certainly had not been in contact with him before I was involved in the gubernatorial race. And I didn’t know Tom.

Leffler: That’s interesting, because as a member of the Connecticut Legislature for all those years, one might have expected some interaction—

W. Cibes: One might have thought that. [laughs]

Leffler: —with what was going on with the Senator from the state and his staff.

W. Cibes: Well, that’s true, but it wasn’t in my case. Republicans and Democrats don’t get intimately involved in one another’s affairs.

Leffler: Sure.

W. Cibes: I just didn’t know Tom at all. I’d known of his reputation, of course. He was chair of the Republican Party, but I think the first time that I can recall that I ever talked to Tom was after a gubernatorial debate sometime in the late spring/early summer of 1990. Connecticut being what it is, there was a series of debates, and at this particular one—I can’t even remember who sponsored it, but it was at the Farmington Marriott. The two Democratic candidates, Bruce Morris and myself and John Rowland and Governor Weicker and maybe one other, I can’t remember, but I know the four were there and all presented.

As usual at that time, I made clear what my position was, that the state needed a revenue balance, and I continue to think that I’d argued persuasively. One never knows about these things, but afterwards, Tom came up to me, introduced himself, and said, “You know, Weicker is looking for a lieutenant gubernatorial candidate. Would you like to drop out of the Governor’s race and run as his Lieutenant governor alongside him?” I did not do that.

Leffler: So that would’ve been on the Independent ticket?

W. Cibes: Yes, the Connecticut Party. And I told him, no, I was committed to running and I thought that the positions I held needed to be completely aired, so I told him I didn’t want to do that. I guess later I heard that the Governor had been impressed at least with what I’d said, and I’m sure Tom would not have made that approach without the Governor’s—

Leffler: Of course. Right.

W. Cibes: —approval, but that was about the only time that I talked with Tom.

Leffler: That’s very interesting.

W. Cibes: Later on, after I’d lost the primary, the fourth-hand rumor I’d heard that Weicker was interested in me joining the administration came through a mutual friend of John Doyle’s who had said that John had mentioned something about this and that’s how I was contacted there.

On the day after the election, I’d written in response to a request from The Hartford Courant an editorial piece about the election and made some comments about the fact that I thought everybody needed to pull together because the state was in a serious position. It was published on Sunday after the election. Tom called me from his car on the way back from the Cape after it had been published. He’d read it and said, “The Governor would like to meet with you,” so we arranged that. That happened at Eunice [Groark]’s house, I think, a couple of days after that.

Leffler: That’s Eunice Groark?

W. Cibes: Eunice Groark, right.

Leffler: My records say that the election took place on November 6 and on November 19 you are announced as Secretary of the Office of Public Management.

W. Cibes: Office of Policy & Management.

Leffler: Policy and Management, thank you, which oversees the budget, so we’re talking about 13 days from election until that announcement, and obviously you were contacted before then—

W. Cibes: Yes.

Leffler: —to talk about that, and that almost seems like that was one of his first—

W. Cibes: It was.

Leffler: Would that have been one of the first announcements?

W. Cibes: It usually is in the gubernatorial administration.

Leffler: Okay, all right.

W. Cibes: It certainly was this last time with Governor [Dannel] Malloy.

Leffler: So it strikes me that that’s gutsy on two levels: one, it’s gutsy of the Governor to bring in somebody who had already announced himself as being in favor of the income tax, even though I know that right from the start he said we’re not going to start off this way, but also on your part, because you’ve been associated as a Democrat, and by doing this, you’re supporting at least a long-term Republican who’s just become an Independent. Did that concern you at all?

W. Cibes: It did not. It was gutsy on the part of the Governor. It was not gutsy on my part. I was in a position then and I guess sort of remain in the position now that I don’t come from wealth. I didn’t have a lot of money, and I needed to be sure that whatever happened and if I joined the administration I would be able to fall back to Connecticut College where I was a tenured professor. Once I was assured of that, I made arrangements with the college after the offer was made, and there was really no problem for me. Basically it was not gutsy to take a job that I might be fired from because I had the fallback of a tenured position at Connecticut College, and so in that respect, it was not gutsy. Also I guess I didn’t see it as a gutsy move to join an independent administration because I’d pretty much been rejected by the Democrats.

I was convinced that the state could not continue with the revenue structure that it had, and I’d been adhering to that position for ten years. I admired the fact that the Governor and his campaign, while he’d made some cracks about throwing gasoline on a fire with an income tax—

Leffler: And then six days later said that the income tax was not on the table.

W. Cibes: But he did not say it was off the table.

Leffler: Off the table. Right. [laughs]

W. Cibes: I think all of us at that time were looking for those little nuances, and it was important to me that he had not taken that pledge. Politics being what it is, you look for those little nuances, and I was encouraged by the fact that he had not taken the income tax off the table and not made the pledge that he would never sign an income tax. And given the fact that the Democratic candidate, Bruce Morrison, had made it clear that he was not in support of an income tax, Governor Weicker was essentially the sole hope of ever moving forward in a direction that made sense for the state.

Leffler: Sure. All right. So perhaps we could now get into some of those deliberations and processes that actually led to the income tax. I’ve followed the record myself and I know that there was a period of about six months in terms of exploring this until ultimately the income tax got passed, but in those six months, there were a lot of storms along the way. Could you, from your perspective, describe that period of time?

W. Cibes: I think the critical time was a period of about six weeks which was before Governor Weicker even mentioned it in his budget speech because that was the period of time that he really came to view the income tax as an essential part of a balanced revenue structure. As I’d spoken a little bit earlier, he’d said when he’d interviewed me, I guess he didn’t come out and ask me right then, but he wanted to know what I thought. He made it clear that he wanted, first, to look at reductions in state spending. If that absolutely failed to close the gap and there was a special circumstance, I guess, then is that—I think all of us on the inside of government knew that there was a huge deficit in terms of the 1991 budget and a bigger projected for 1992 that would be adopted in the spring of ’91, the fiscal year being as it is, but a lot of people didn’t believe that.

Governor O’Neill in his announcements made efforts to minimize the deficit in the fall of 1990 as the election was approaching, but it was going to be a big deficit and it in fact got bigger. I think right before the election some people said it’s only a couple hundred million. I’d been saying in the campaign it was a billion. That was the number it turned out to be at the time, but by the time spring rolled around after the Governor’s budget was announced, it turned out that the overall shortfall in ’91 and the projected shortfall for ’92 was $3 billion, so it just got worse during this period of time. But to go back, he’d made it clear: “First of all, you look at budget reductions, and if that turns out not to be enough, then you can look at income, but you can’t look at the income tax. I don’t want you looking at the income tax until we’ve exhausted everything.”

Leffler: That’s well described in his book, too, right?

W. Cibes: [laughs] Well, it’s accurate. I think some people still don’t believe that’s the case, but it was. I’d been fortunate to be allowed into OPM [Office of Policy and Management]. I’d been on good relations with Governor O’Neill since I’d been involved in two of his campaigns at a fairly high level, and so the outgoing secretary made the office available to me. I was able to work with the budget analysts starting in early December actually.

Leffler: December of ’91?

W. Cibes: ’90.

Leffler: December of ’90?

W. Cibes: ’90.

Leffler: Of course. Right.

W. Cibes: Long before the Governor was inaugurated.

Leffler: Right, right, of course.

W. Cibes: And I was able to build a great team of people, some of whom are still active in state government today, and they were incisive minds, great people, and so we also added a critical part, and that was a guy by the name of Charles Ward who had been on Weicker’s campaign team.

Leffler: W-A-R-D?

W. Cibes: Yes. Charles Ward, “Chip,” as he’s known. And the Governor stuck him in the revenue section just to make sure that everybody was honest over there. A brilliant, brilliant guy. Probably the smartest person I know. Chip was involved in working through the numbers and making sure that nobody—in fact, nobody looked at the revenue until Weicker said it was okay. [phone call]

Leffler: Just carry on.

0:19:40

State sales tax and its role in the budget

W. Cibes: Chip was heavily involved in looking at the spending cuts as well. Once the Governor was ensconced in the capitol after he was inaugurated, we would traipse over to the office to make presentations about potential cuts, and the Governor would come over to OPM and look through the details. After a couple of weeks, by early January, he was convinced that we couldn’t close the gap through budget cuts alone. So he authorized us to look at revenue, and he particularly wanted us to look at sales taxes, to end some sales tax exemptions and potentially increase the sales tax rate.

Leffler: Those would be exemptions on things like gasoline?

W. Cibes: There’s a whole raft that are still in existence today and may have actually expanded, but there’s a whole raft of things ranging from vending machines to car washes to haircuts to clothing—

Leffler: Okay.

W. Cibes: —to food and so forth. And you go through that list of exemptions and the Governor would say, “Yes, we can cut that. We can eliminate that exemption. Or no, we have to keep that.” It turned out that what looked to be a great gold mine, from his perspective, turned out not to produce a lot of revenue, so then we looked at increasing the rate of the sales tax. In all of this, we were aided by some studies that had been done under my watch on the finance committee, a rep by the name of Miles Rapoport, a very liberal representative from West Hartford, had persuaded the assembly in 1989 to establish a revenue study commission. Miles is now the chair of Demos in New York. Persuaded the legislature to establish a revenue study commission, which was basically the finance committee, but put some money—

Governor O’Neill let us go ahead and do that, and we hired three national consultants to study the sales tax, the corporate tax, and the income tax and build computer models and do complete analyses of these kinds of things, so we were aided in the analysis of the sales tax by one of these studies that had been produced. The final reports came out in the summer and winter of 1990 during the election campaign and shortly after that, and so we knew how much revenue would be generated by a sales tax increase and so forth, so that remained on the table through probably the third or fourth week of January, something like that, but it turned out that in order to close the gap we’d probably need a sales tax rate of 12 percent.

Leffler: Oh, that’s—[laughs]

W. Cibes: That was the Governor’s reaction. [laughs] Almost the same facial expression. Chip verified that that number was right. The Governor trusted Chip. He didn’t know yet whether he could trust Cibes or not, but he could certainly trust Chip. So that was a little daunting for the Governor. He said, “Well, now you can look at an income tax to see if there’s some way you can put this together,” and again, aided by the computer models that had already been built. Chip and the revenue section at OPM looked through this and we constructed a budget that would eliminate the selective income tax on interest income and dividends and capital gains, which I think was at 14 percent. It would eliminate that. It would reduce the sales tax and would institute an income tax at, I think, a 6 percent rate, which would close it.

The Governor was really torn. He didn’t want to do that at all. I don’t know whether he was getting advice from other people. I know he consulted some people down in Greenwich, probably a lot of people from New York City who were active in finances and so forth. I made a pilgrimage down to his Greenwich home to explain what the situation was to some people. I can’t remember now who was there. Of course the Governor was there. I guess it had pretty much been developed, the whole package, but he wanted some economists to look at that, so he called in a panel of economists to ask them generally would it make sense to have an income tax, a personal income tax, or would it make sense to increase the sales tax rate. Well, the economists wimped out, and I’ve since learned this about academics generally. I probably knew it because of my background in academic life, but all these economists—

Leffler: There are pros and cons. Is that what—

W. Cibes: No, it was actually even worse than that.

Leffler: It was worse than that? Okay.

W. Cibes: These economists, who in private life in their classrooms would tell you, to a man, to a woman, that Connecticut needed a personal income tax and needed to balance this structure, walked into his office, and I was there, and said we can live with the sales tax increase. Yes. Again, incredulous—

Leffler: And you think they did that because they thought that was what he wanted to hear?

W. Cibes: Yes. Absolutely.

Leffler: They were reading their audience.

W. Cibes: Yes. [laughs] Yes. They had heard during the campaign, he’d said no income tax, so why should they think things had changed in the last six weeks or two months or something? One of them did not, and I’m trying to remember who it was, but at least had some guts to say it didn’t make sense, and I tried to make the point that there would be a dampening effect on the economy by increasing the sales tax to that level, but the Governor was really torn and remained torn.

A couple of days later he called me into his office and said, “Bill, I can’t do the income tax. I’m going to do the sales tax,” and I said, “Let me try one more time to persuade you. Let’s marshal all the facts. Let’s have a sit-down. You can look through this,” and he said, “Okay.” A couple of days later we met in the fifth floor conference room of the capitol and we went through it. He said, “Look, I can’t do a 12 percent sales tax.” Chip assured him that it would be that high and he trusted Chip, as I said, and rightfully so.
So he said, “Okay, we’ll do—” Actually, he asked us to draw up three plans. One was a conventional tax package, one was the income tax package, and one was a plan that was just so abominable that nobody in the world could accept it.

Leffler: So, let me see. They were called aardvark—

W. Cibes: Yes.

Leffler: and camel—

W. Cibes: Yes.

Leffler: The income tax one, and then one in between which was—Oh, I can get—

W. Cibes: I can’t remember what “W” was. Camel was for Cibes.

Leffler: Oh I see. That’s interesting.

W. Cibes: Camel was the mascot at Connecticut College. Aardvark was the horrible one. That was cobbled together.

Leffler: There was another one, some kind of a beast. I’ve forgot what that one is.

W. Cibes: Wildebeest for Weicker. So “W” for Weicker.

Leffler: Oh. Okay. What was the “A” for? We don’t know.

W. Cibes: Just a bad—[laughs]

Leffler: Okay. Good. [laughs]

W. Cibes: It was the worst kind of construction we could think of, so we outlined them all, and he decided eventually to go with the income tax plan, but he didn’t want anybody to know where he was going. He wanted to keep this absolutely secret. He even went to the extent that because the budget has to be printed ahead of time and it’s always printed with absolutely secrecy, it’s printed by an outside contractor. He was so distrustful that this word would leak that he instructed that our OPM staff had to sleep in the office of the printer to make sure that nobody escaped with any copy or any idea of what was going forward.

So we went through that and our budget chief went over to do that and his trusted aide, and it didn’t leak, but Avice [Meehan] had the idea that maybe a little misdirection would not hurt. She knew that a reporter, whose name I will not mention because he’s still active in Connecticut, regularly went through the garbage cans at the capitol to see what people had thrown out, so she tossed in one of the garbage cans “aardvark” and he picked it up.

Leffler: From a garbage—that’s interesting, because in Weicker’s book I think he tells that story and I think he says a copy was left at the top of the stairs or something.

W. Cibes: It was in a garbage can. [laughs]

Leffler: So that’s the correction to that. That’s interesting.

W. Cibes: And I guess Avice did it without his knowledge, from what I understand subsequently, but anyway it happened, and the reporter ultimately published it and it looked as if their sales tax was going up, so it was in fact a complete surprise when the Governor stepped up to the podium in the hall of the house and said we’re going with an income tax.

Leffler: And he describes that in the book as not having been authorized by him, but probably had some salutary impact after all because the notion of a sales tax that would have to be that high had people very nervous and very worried.

W. Cibes: Right.

Leffler: So you said those six weeks when all that’s going on and the decision is being made as to which direction to go in, obviously given your role, you see that as the most critical time period, and it was in terms of setting a direction.

W. Cibes: Right, but obviously going beyond that—

Leffler: But then it had to get passed.

W. Cibes: Then it had to get passed, and I would say that whole period of time, probably longer than six months, from February to the actual passage and through December, when there was an attempt to repeal that—

Leffler: He gives his first budget message on February 13, 1991. The general assembly passes the budget on August 22, but I know that within that period there were at least three different times that the Governor vetoed—

W. Cibes: Right.

0:32:00

Budget passed by Connecticut Legislature

Leffler: —the budget that the legislature passed, and there was a partial government shutdown during which time he’s making speeches both to the public and to the Congress, so that must’ve been a very intense time period.

W. Cibes: Oh, it was, certainly, for him. I suppose for all of us. Everybody who had some part in it was selling it at the same time as well. Everybody had their own individual responsibilities. I was not to set foot at the capitol, but I was sent out to make some of the speeches. But the critical part of that, and as I said, it did last through December because of the attempt to repeal, was dominated by the Governor’s personality and his courage.

I often said during that time and subsequently that he’s got a backbone of steel, and that was evident, because once he decided that this was the way he was going, he would not even consider alternatives. It helped that the alternatives were so bad that it was pretty easy to defend, but it’s that kind of courage I think that inspires loyalty of people who work for him. It certainly did in my case, even though I hadn’t worked with him before. He just was completely dedicated and continued to adhere to the decision he’d made. He didn’t waffle around with those things, and it could have been tempting the second or third time with the budget to go in any direction, but he chose not to. One of the things I didn’t see on this gubernatorial timeline was the party on the lawn.

Leffler: Why don’t you tell us about that.

0:34:37

Demonstration and harassment of officials after budget passed

W. Cibes: After the budget was passed in August, it looked very much as if—people were upset, obviously—but it looked like there might be enough popular uprising to overthrow that, aided and abetted, of course, by a couple Republican conservatives who called for a major demonstration on the lawn of the capitol some Saturday in September I guess it was. Some Governors, I suspect, would have holed up in the capitol or never appeared any place close to Hartford. Not Weicker. Weicker said, “I’m going out there and talking to them.” Fortunately, some cool heads on the part of the state police prevailed and required that he have an escort as he walked among these 40,000 people, and then they hustled him out after things were thrown at him and he was spat upon and so forth, but that was vintage Weicker.

If I can digress here for a second—

Leffler: Please. Yes.

W. Cibes: One of the things about the Senator—I’d not paid a lot of attention to his senatorial career. I was focused on Connecticut, but later on in the late 1990s at CSU [Connecticut State University] we had an annual dinner for what we called the Global Majority Retreat for folks who were normally called minorities, but actually around the globe are the majority—

Leffler: Yes.

W. Cibes: —and we regularly were able to attract some really fantastic speakers and people who were known in the civil rights community particularly. One of the years we had, I believe the name was Randall Robinson, I’m not sure –

Leffler: Oh, I know who that is.

W. Cibes: —with an anti-apartheid group in Washington.

Leffler: Right. He was the head of some Africa organization.

W. Cibes: Yes. You know more details than I can remember at this point, but we were sitting before dinner and he was talking about his knowledge of Weicker. He said at one point they were trying to drum up opposition to apartheid and get the United States government to take a position and he said he got a call out of the blue, “Randall, this is Lowell. I want to get arrested.” [laughter] So they arranged a demonstration and Weicker apparently became the first United States Senator ever arrested.

Leffler: Did he not chain himself to a fence or something like that?

W. Cibes: Probably. I don’t know the details, but it’s certainly—

Leffler: Right. Yes. He was the first Senator? Could be.

W. Cibes: That’s what I heard, because the Constitution says during the course of the session the members of the House and Senate can’t be arrested for one thing or another. Well, Weicker was arrested apparently, so to me, that just confirmed what I knew about the Governor as the Governor with an unbending will.

Leffler: Of course there was also the Watergate experience—

W. Cibes: Yes. That’s very true.

Leffler: —much earlier on when he was a very junior Senator.

W. Cibes: Yes.

Leffler: That took an enormous amount of courage.

W. Cibes: Absolutely. Just his unbending will, a conviction that once he’s made a decision that he was right and he was going to stick by it. That generates a lot of loyalty.

Leffler: I know about that party on the lawn. It was your euphemism that caught me off guard for a moment. And I also know that there were people who really felt personally vulnerable, in danger in some ways, or that they had suffered various kinds of abuse as a result of supporting the income tax, one of them being [Lawrence J.] Bettencourt, who chose not to run again and felt that his family had been subject to various kinds of abuse, air being let out of tires and threatening phone calls and things like that. Did you ever feel threatened as a result of your role?

W. Cibes: My family was harassed. We’d get calls at two o’clock in the morning from bars where people had ginned themselves up with a few beers to make some calls. My number was in the phone book, so at two o’clock in the morning, when the bar was ready to close, they’d call up and rant for a while, and my wife would get phone calls. I think not as threatened as Larry. Larry was the Senator from my district, but I probably was not harassed as much as Larry was, because Larry was a Republican and had been elected to follow whatever Republicans do. I represented New London, had been outed ten years before as an income tax advocate, and my district had overwhelmingly elected me for five terms. Well, five terms after it became clear I was an income tax advocate, and so by and large I was very supported in my district and in town.

Leffler: But this is no longer a local issue. This is now a state issue.

W. Cibes: I probably have. I think a vision which admirably supported the best in people, the ideals that didn’t try to beat people when they were down but up to uplift them and the courage to then move forward with that vision, a backbone of steel.

Leffler: John Doyle did say yesterday, and I’m sure he’s on record saying it, that he could be very tough to work for as well. Did you feel that way at times?

W. Cibes: I did not. He must’ve been very gentle with me because I certainly felt that I had access to him. I felt that he treated me fairly, more than fairly. I can’t recall any time that he really tore me apart, and I’ve heard that he did tear other people apart.

Leffler: Indeed. Well, sure, but you had a very special role, I think, in terms of the responsibilities, but everyone did.

W. Cibes: Everybody does. As I reflect back on it I think one of the things that I really consciously made an effort to do was to recognize that I was not elected Governor, and I suspect maybe some people who had run for Governor might think that in a position like I ended up in that I could substitute my judgment for that of the Governor. I really deliberately tried not to do that, to support the Governor, just recognizing that he had been elected.

Leffler: I understand. This has been very, very informative. I think you so much for being here.

W. Cibes: You’re quite welcome. I hope it was helpful.

Leffler: It was extremely helpful and very nice to have this part of his career as part of our record in the project, so thank you.

[End of interview]
