0:00:00

Leffler: This is Phyllis Leffler. Today is April 13, 2010. I’m conducting an interview with Geoffrey Baker at 1725 20th Street Northwest as part of the Lowell Weicker Oral History Project. So welcome, and thank you for doing this.

G. Baker: Thank you.

Leffler: I would like to start out by your just telling me your full name, and then if you could just speak a little bit about your own background before you joined the staff of Lowell Weicker.

G. Baker: Okay. My name is Geoffrey Burnett Baker. I was born in New York City; I grew up in Greenwich, Connecticut. I went to Greenwich Country Day School and away to prep school at Deerfield and then out to college in Stanford. My connection with Lowell Weicker began as he was the first selectman of Greenwich, which was in effect mayor. So while I did not know him personally, he was somebody I had seen. I had followed his career locally. And then of course he went to the House of Representatives. I can speak to how I joined his staff, if that would be helpful.

Leffler: Please. That would be great.

G. Baker: Well, as I said, I actually brought a few papers to refer to.

Leffler: I wish we were videotaping.

0:01:30

Baker’s experience as a summer intern in Weicker’s office

G. Baker: Well, actually, I must confess to all and for posterity that this is to help me refresh my memory as we go. I have a letter dated December 3, 1969, that was addressed to me at Stanford, where I was in college. I’ll read it, just a few lines, to give you a flavor. This is from Lowell P. Weicker Jr., member of Congress from the 4th District, which was my district in Connecticut.

“Many thanks for your letter of November 23 in which you expressed an interest in a summer position as an intern in Congress for 1970.” And it says he has not made any definitive commitments, but “I’d like to hear more and get your resume. Come in for an appointment when you’re in Greenwich, at home.”

I have some other correspondence related to this, but essentially I was in college and I had not been drafted. I had friends who were going to Vietnam, but I felt I should be doing something useful, so I thought I’d go to Washington. Well, that’s a great idea, and I didn’t know anything about it. There wasn’t a program at the time, although Stanford now has Stanford in Washington, and I’m sure many colleges do. I’m sure Virginia and others do. And so I just thought of the only politician I that knew, which was Lowell Weicker, my first selectman, now Congressman. I wrote to him and said I’d like to come to Washington for a summer. And this is just an indication that I actually did do that. He thought about it and eventually accepted me as a summer intern. As it turned out I came in the summer of 1970, and then I had another summer in 1971.

Leffler: You would have been brand new in Congress.

G. Baker: Yes, and then he was running for Senate.

Leffler: Very short order.

G. Baker: Yes. I came back and was hired after that. I’m very appreciative of summer internships because that’s how I started in Washington. I’ve lived in Washington and my two sons have grown up here. I encouraged them to become summer interns, and they were interns both on the Hill and at the White House over the years. So I credit Lowell Weicker for bringing me to Washington. I also thought it was a very valuable experience. It was everything I hoped it was. And it led to my first position, which was as a legislative aide, along with Bob Dotchin in my class. And of course from that point, Watergate, and a very exciting early experience on the Hill, which I thought would be very brief because I was planning on going to law school.

Leffler: So if I can just clarify this. You came in for two different summers when you were still at Stanford. So this would have been your junior and senior years at Stanford?

G. Baker: Yes. It was senior year, let’s see now, ’70. No, it was junior and senior.

Leffler: And after you completed the second internship you stayed on as a legislative aide?

G. Baker: Well, as it turned out, I did a year of graduate work at Stanford and then I came back and went to work. That was the plan.

Leffler: And your graduate work was in what?

G. Baker: Actually it was at the school of education. But I decided—

Leffler: Not to pursue that.

G. Baker: —not to pursue it. I always wonder about that. I came back to Washington and eventually went to law school. So that was the plan.

Leffler: Right.

G. Baker: For the record, although this cannot be seen, this shows that I really did save some things. This is my student intern card—

Leffler: That is fantastic. That’s wonderful.

G. Baker: —from that period.

Leffler: It must’ve been very meaningful if you saved it.

G. Baker: Yes, and again this cannot be seen, but I used to be very young, and that’s a picture of me with Lowell Weicker in the Greenwich Time newspaper. So. And here is a—

Leffler: That’s very nice. And he’s looking a lot younger too. [laughter] As we all do.

G. Baker: Well, I assume this is all in the archive. And then, just to give you a flavor, this is a picture of a summer internship group. This is Bob Dotchin. And there’s me as a young man.

Leffler: These are all summer interns. So he would’ve taken 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7.

G. Baker: Yes, and there may have been others, but that was one photograph. Actually, in going through my papers a couple of summer ago, I came across a number of photographs that I gave to Lowell and Claudia [Weicker] for the archives. Not that they’re critical, but they showed some pictures of our group when we were all that age.

0:06:28

Baker joins Weicker’s staff as legislative aide

Leffler: So you come on as a legislative aide. This would have been 1972?

G. Baker: Yes. It would’ve been the summer of ’72.

Leffler: So right at the start of Watergate? Or—

G. Baker: Yes. I have to be careful, because I don’t know the exact timeline, but Senator Weicker was appointed to the Watergate Committee eventually. But that time period was a period of the election and many of the events. And then the follow-up in ’73, ’74, had to do with the Watergate Committee and the eventual resignation of the President. Bob Dotchin I mention because he and I came out of college at the same time and we were colleagues and followed a career on the Hill very closely, always affiliated with Lowell Weicker in one way or another.

Leffler: Well, tell me about that. How long did you stay with the Senator?

G. Baker: I left at the end of 1982, so it was essentially—

Leffler: Ten years.

G. Baker: —ten years of employment on the Hill. And during the middle of that, from ’75 to ’77, I was on the staff of the Senate Commerce Committee. That, I know, is one of the questions that you have, and I can clarify how that all came about.

Leffler: Right. But then you said you went on and stayed on the Hill.

G. Baker: Oh, no. Actually, Bob Dotchin stayed on longer than I did. But for me, and I think at the time all the young aides, thought of ourselves as going to the Hill for a couple of years, going to law school, going on to something else. I just recall that with the excitement of the Watergate Committee, Weicker’s close involvement in that, it sort of put all thought of following a career path out of mind. You couldn’t imagine anything more interesting than being on the Hill, involved in an office that was closely involved in the Watergate Committee, at least I couldn’t. So I put off going to law school for several years. I’m glad I did. I had a great experience.

Leffler: And you went back to law school after—

G. Baker: I actually went to Georgetown Law, where I met my wife. But I was working at the same time, so I went to night school. I got my law degree while I was working at the Commerce Committee, and then back at Lowell Weicker’s office.

Leffler: I certainly think that what you’ve just said about the excitement of working in Congress in those years, and particularly on some of these issues that Congressman and then Senator Weicker was involved in, has been corroborated by some of the other interviews that I’ve already had. I haven’t had many, but I already have this sense that that’s a consistent, people feeling very invested and very excited about the issues that they’re working on.

G. Baker: Right.

Leffler: So clearly, since we don’t quite have all of this right, perhaps you can just tell me what were the issues you started working on initially, and maybe you can just explain what you did with those issues. Do you want to follow this chronologically?

G. Baker: Well, I want to be careful. I want to follow the issues, and I can follow your issues in any way that you want. Essentially what happened was that from 1972 to June of 1974 I was a legislative aide, which meant I worked on any number of issues, whatever I was assigned internally within the office. Those did include space, oceans, and the environment, but I think the one set of issues that may not have been highlighted in the materials that you have was that consistently in my career there we were concerned with surface transportation issues, railroad issues, particularly starting with the failure of the New Haven Railroad and moving into the creation of Amtrak, the efforts to sustain low-density freight lines in New England, and eventually the creation of Conrail, which took over all the freight lines in the northeast. That was consistently an issue that Congressman and Senator Weicker obviously had great concern about, and I worked closely with him on those issues and the staff.

0:11:27

Senate Commerce Committee and focus on railroad issues

G. Baker: I went to the Senate Commerce Committee. You have some information that Mary MacAuliffe, who was Senator Weicker’s staffer, was assigned to the Commerce Committee, and I was to move over to Commerce in 1975. I have correspondence here that explains all this, but Senator [James B.] Pearson, who was the minority, that is, the ranking minority member, which meant he was the lead Republican Senator, had negotiated a deal where I could come over if he could have my services. Mary stayed as a Weicker staffer involved in many of the Commerce issues. But I was essentially assigned to work for Senator Pearson on the minority side. Now, that did not mean I wasn’t working closely with Senator Weicker.

Leffler: That’s fascinating.

G. Baker: It’s just that Senator Pearson claimed my services so that he could essentially call me a professional staff member of the Committee. I worked closely with Senator Weicker, but all the Senators on rail issues in particular. I also did certain utility energy issues that were under the purview of the Commerce Committee and also ocean issues and so forth. But Mary and I, while we were both associated with the Commerce Committee, were under different classifications.

Leffler: Who paid your salary? Do you know?

G. Baker: Who paid my salary? The Committee paid my salary.

Leffler: So you were no longer technically—

G. Baker: During that period I was no longer technically in the employ of Senator Weicker. However, I will say for the record that as far as Senator Weicker is concerned, I’m sure that during that period he put me to work on the Senate Commerce Committee. I was working on issues that were important to him and to Connecticut and the region. But my focus was whatever Senator Pearson asked me to do. It just turned out that there was a confluence of interests in terms of rail issues. So I spent most of my time on railroad issues.

The timeline is that in June 1974—I have a note here that June 1974 to March 1975 I was Senator Weicker’s legislative assistant, which meant I basically managed the legislative operations and staff. Then I went for two years plus to the Senate Commerce Committee. And from 1977 through 1982 I came back to Senator Weicker’s office as a legislative assistant. Part of the reason that all this happened is that Bob Dotchin and I came on at the same time and we were legislative aides and moving up, taking on responsibility at the same time. He moved in the direction of the Small Business Committee and so forth, so his focus was more on small business. He was very much Senator Weicker’s man in that whole arena. We still continued to work as a team, and during that time that I was on the Commerce Committee, although I technically wasn’t on Senator Weicker’s staff, I still considered myself a member of the Weicker team.

Leffler: Loyal to the team. Right.

0:15:40

Animal welfare amendments and interstate commerce

G. Baker: Yes. All of the issues that that I worked on, you’ve identified a number of them. I remember it’s kind of interesting that I worked on the—and here’s a copy just of one bill and report, the Animal Welfare Amendments of 1975, and I’m sure you have that. But I happened to save that because I think it was the first bill that I actually had the largest hand in writing and reporting and so forth.

Leffler: Really?

G. Baker: And it was interesting that this wasn’t the biggest issue obviously in the world, but it was an issue that he cared about and we did something about it. Fast forward to the current day. My wife and I have Rhodesian Ridgebacks—we only have one now, we’re down to one—but if there’s one issue in the world that she cares about, it’s humane treatment of animals.

Leffler: And you came by this very early.

G. Baker: And this was before I met her. Everyone has issues that they sort of started off with, and this is one of the issues that was memorable for me.

Leffler: Before we go on, why do you think the Senator cared so much about this? What did that come out of?

G. Baker: I believe if I reread the report I could give you a better sense of how we approached the issue. Normally, in the legislative arena, many of the issues came up because either a constituent, or a group of constituents, or a constituent corporation had been in touch with our office and alerted us to an issue. I believe that in this case—although you have to ask Senator Weicker about his love of dogs, but he’s always had dogs. I believe if I think back upon it, it was, at the time, articles about the mistreatment of animals, particularly in air transportation from Connecticut, that caused us to take a look at this. I remember going over to Reagan Airport, it was then National Airport, to look at the cargo facilities and talk to the staff over there about how they handled animals so we could get a good sense of what we needed to do to regulate the air.

Leffler: My notes here, I don’t know if this is correct or not, say that your ability to regulate animal treatment at the federal level stemmed from a broad interpretation of federal authority over interstate commerce?

G. Baker: Well, I think when you think about particularly air transportation that that was pretty obvious.

Leffler: Right.

G. Baker: Reading that now that’s going to be—you know we’re going to have an interesting case on these issues.

Leffler: Health care, particularly. Right.

G. Baker: Before the Supreme Court within two years. I wasn’t thinking about that. No. Whenever he got hold of an issue he was prepared to tackle it. I realize that this issue was raised up in some of the questions you ask, but it wasn’t so much creating big government, it was finding a way to deal with issues in a rational and responsible way, I think. This was something that there wasn’t a great deal of opposition to. The problem with any regulation, even in these years, was that there’s some cost to regulation. It would require airlines or other transportation modes to spend more time and money to take care of the animals in this case. No one really likes to do that unless they can be convinced it’s a reasonable solution.

Leffler: Sure.

G. Baker: So whether it was protection of coral reefs, or of scientific research in the space program, I don’t think any of the issues that he tackled were radical issues. And the solutions that he proposed were not radical solutions, but many of them, if you go down the list, have not been given a lot of attention. If I recall, and I’m not an expert, coming through the late 1960s in college, issues like environmental protection and preservation were only just getting attention.

Leffler: That’s absolutely right.

0:20:27

Weicker’s involvement in oceans and space research

G. Baker: And so legislative and regulatory solutions to these problems were just being considered, and they obviously weren’t all dealt with during that period. But I think when you think of the oceans, and I was not the expert on the oceans, Bob Wicklund and others were really experts. I recall my responsibilities, more to make sure we got something done. We had our legislative team working on these issues properly and getting something done. So managing the process more than anything else, but with the oceans there wasn’t anyone else who was really championing the oceans. But once Senator Weicker got underwater and started scuba diving and meeting the people who were doing the research and trying to preserve the reefs and large parts of the ocean, he began to appreciate the issue as one that should receive national attention.

Leffler: Absolutely. I have interviewed Bob Wicklund and also have read through an enormous amount of documentation on this, and it really seems like it’s something he became very passionate about.

G. Baker: Yes. Well, I think it’s just an example, if you’re asking me how do these things happen. Bob Wicklund was the expert and Tim Keeney and others that he met and worked with on the staff and off the staff. He usually had some direct interest in what was going on and he did understand. He wasn’t looking for the issue, the issue came to him and he developed it. I think that’s to his credit. I don’t get any credit for the oceans because all I did was help. Others inspired him, others informed him and educated him. But that’s one area. The space program was another that he was very involved in, the Space Committee.

I happened to run across these documents. I went down to Cape Canaveral on a number of occasions with the Senator and to see space shots. This was some of the material that we got from one of the space flights when we were down there. I happened to just keep that folder and somehow put it in a box. But that was an exciting time too. I mean the promise of space travel and exploration seemed limitless at that time, particularly after [John F.] Kennedy set us in motion.

It tied in in many ways as you look across Lowell Weicker’s career at scientific research, medical research, environmental research. There are many issues where he felt that we could do more and it was worth the investment. So I think a lot of the issues that you’ve identified, and others—he did feel that an investment in research would yield tremendous dividends for the country, and not just for individuals, but for the broader country and internationally.

Leffler: Now you’re raising the space program. This is not something I have seen him write about in his book, Maverick. Maybe I’ve missed it.

G. Baker: I don’t recall.

Leffler: Are you aware that he was particularly invested in that issue?

G. Baker: Well, he was on the Committee. Other than that I don’t think I have anything with me that relates to it specifically.

Leffler: I think you’re quite right that it’s an example of putting resources into research for causes that hadn’t gotten much attention, and I would’ve expected him to support it, but—

G. Baker: Well, I just recall it as something that was very exciting at the time. It may not have been his top priority, but he was a strong supporter and he understood it and he made the effort. It may have been that we did more work at the staff level on this issue and he didn’t consider it as important. But he was involved and he was on the Space Committee.

Leffler: Well, I think I should correct myself on this. I think he actually writes in his chapter about exploration of a variety of worlds. He invested himself, I think, most in the world of the oceans, but he understood that the world of space was equally important. So—

G. Baker: I want to be careful that I’m giving you just my clear recollections, but I’m also trying to connect some of the dots here because you’ve asked me to think about what this has meant to me and how to understand Lowell Weicker’s career that I knew on the Hill. And as I know his life and interests, he pushed hard in areas that not only needed attention but needed investment. In a later time he got more involved with issues of the disabled and medical research, which has become if not his top, one of his top issues even now. I can look back in my experience, and that was early in the career that the notion of exploring the oceans was something that when he was first introduced to it, he didn’t know very much about it, I don’t believe, as none of us did. But he really dug into it and he realized there was a lot there, and it wasn’t just life forms, it was energy and minerals and all sorts of things. We needed to explore the oceans and he—

Leffler: With aquaculture and—

G. Baker: Oh yes, and as you know, he spent time living down below.

Leffler: I think Bob Wicklund told me he was one of only two members of Congress who ever did that.

G. Baker: Well, I think that reflects his willingness when he got engaged in something to commit himself. He’s willing to do anything. And as you look at his legislative career, as I recall it, and my involvement particularly on issues that involved floor debates, filibusters, and those sorts of things, there were criticisms of Lowell Weicker over the years that he would grandstand issues. But there’s an example where yes, he in effect promoted himself. There were pictures of him underwater and so forth. But he did it for a very real purpose, and that is to bring attention to the issue, which to his credit, he was successful in doing. I can’t think of anyone else at that time or since who’s invested as much. Now, the space program was not as important to him, but you could tie this together and say that his support for a program like that just as his support for medical research is trying to extend frontiers of knowledge. And beyond what he could even understand or we could understand, but we knew there was a lot out there.

0:28:02

Commuter and freight transportation issues

G. Baker: And I think that’s what for a number of us who worked with him, and however difficult life was, it was inspiring to be involved in a lot of these issues. Not all of them. I’m sure for most people the idea of railroad legislation is as boring as it gets, but for the northeast, for Connecticut commuter rail, freight rail, still very relevant in those times. We know we haven’t been as successful with rail in American as we should’ve been, but now we’re rethinking it. Now we’re getting some high-speed corridors.

Leffler: Absolutely.

G. Baker: Now we’re even getting trolley lines in Washington.

Leffler: And some of this is also going on during the oil crisis. Right?

G. Baker: Oh, yes. Sure.

Leffler: The oil reserve and the need to think about alternative means of transportation, and I think the issues with rail—you should tell me if you see it differently, but the issues with rail transportation are tied in with larger issues, also environmental protection at the same time. So they’re all kind of coming together at the same time, aren’t they?

G. Baker: Right. Yes. Well, this is off the subject, but you raise a good question, because you can argue strongly that freight rail transportation environmentally makes a great deal of sense because of getting trucks off the road and all that. And I think that railroads make a good case for themselves. I’m just thinking that in fact the reason that we are burning as much coal as we are is because the railroads are bringing in coal from Wyoming, which is where it’s been found, and it’s easy to get out and move around. So there are all sorts of issues. In New England and in the northeast we were just trying to keep business alive and transportation along that northeast corridor. You know, offer some alternative to I-95.

Leffler: I know I don’t remember the exact dates of this, but I do remember reading that Weicker proposed that certain monies from federal highway bills be allowed to be used for other forms of public transportation, specifically rail. Do you remember that? Were you involved with any of that?

G. Baker: I’m sure I was involved. I don’t know specifically what it is, I can’t point to a specific time or speech, but the context of our working on these issues—at that time there were a number of moderate Republicans he worked closely with as well as Democrats, and he worked well across the aisle, in fact. But in the New York Metropolitan region, if you think about Senator [Jacob] Javits and others who were very prominent at that time, commuter rail tied in very well to mass transit. Commuter rail for Connecticut was as important as money for subways in New York City for Senator Javits.

And so there was a confluence of interests in terms of many of the urban or ex-urban Senators at that time. Not that they were building a great number of highways, but there wasn’t a lot of money available to save the railroads and to save the subway systems—urban transit, but that’s been a struggle forever. We had a struggle over many years once Amtrak was created to make sure that Amtrak was funded. Unfortunately, Amtrak never made any money, and so therefore it needed constant funding, and it’s still been an issue. It’s not quite as bad as the post office, but
it’s a struggle.

Leffler: As an aside, we’ve just created a new short haul. I don’t know if that’s the right term, but this short rail trip between Lynchburg, Virginia, and Washington D.C., going through Charlottesville, adding one more train a day to the route. There used to be just one train a day, there are now two. A member of the city council of Charlottesville and a former member were very influential in getting this to happen, and it has apparently been incredibly successful. It started out being something that was going to be heavily subsidized. It’s actually made money. So I think there’s a lot of interest now in having alternate forms of transportation between that southern corridor and the Washington, D.C., area.

G. Baker: Well, unfortunately the northeast corridor is an old and inefficient corridor. It needs to be reworked, and it’s going to be very expensive. But they can build some other corridors elsewhere. I am certain that if Senator Weicker were involved in any way legislatively now, this would be a top issue. Transportation would still be a top issue for him.

Leffler: So in your work on transportation issues, this is when you are a legislative aide.

G. Baker: Initially.

Leffler: Initially, and then do you remember anything about the process itself of working on these kinds of issues that you think is valuable to talk about?

G. Baker: Well, in terms of the actual work, I think Senator Weicker was very good about giving staff a directive, “We need to do something about this,” but then letting staff go to work. And so we were given a lot of responsibility and expected to show initiative and get the job done. I’m not saying that I did, but we all did. We all worked together. So the process was one where the staff was given a lot of responsibility, and I respect that. That’s true on all issues. And that’s not always true, but that’s what I recall about those times.

When I came to his office I had grown up in Greenwich, but only as a boy. I had traveled up to Massachusetts for summers and so I knew how to go up 95. But I didn’t know Connecticut that well. One of the first things that I did so I would understand where people were when they called in—because many constituents called in on all these issues—is I ordered up a big wall map of Connecticut and I had it up right next to my telephone—it’s about three feet by five feet. People thought it was kind of silly, but I didn’t know Connecticut.

So you ask how these things get done. Somebody calls in a small business up in the northeast corner of Connecticut that I didn’t really know and says, “We’re losing a short-haul line or low-density line, and what can you do about it?” Well, obviously, to develop a legislative response you first have to look into the situation and how the various state agencies and federal agencies are handling the matter and why they’re doing it. And in many cases we were responding with broader legislation to initial calls from constituents who were losing their freight access, for example.

Business in Connecticut was declining in many of the traditional industries in the Waterbury area and throughout the state. Losing jobs to the South where non-union jobs were and so forth. So this was a constant struggle of trying to help business stay in New England, stay in Connecticut.

I’m not sure I’m answering the question, but the process was essentially responding to constituents and constituent interests, and doing it in a way that would build coalitions with other states, other Senators. So if we had a problem in Connecticut, we’d call on the Senators throughout New England, and the New York Senators get together at a staff level and say, “Okay, we obviously need to protect our region. What can we do?” And we’d talk to federal agencies and members of Congress also, and try to put together a consensus plan, a piece of legislation that we knew we could get through our committees in the Senate. Pass the Senate as well as go through the House. Very often those were solutions that ended up in bigger bills. They were just a piece, but we put them in. But that’s the nature of, as you know, the legislative process.

Leffler: Sure. Were there any issues that you worked on that you remember as being particularly thorny or difficult in terms of building the kinds of coalitions you needed to build in order to make progress?

G. Baker: Well, Senator Weicker was considered a fiscal conservative going into his career—a fiscal conservative and a social moderate. But as his record shows, he was engaged in many social issues. The toughest issues were issues where I actually did spend a lot of time with him on the floor of the Senate in either filibusters or extended debate on issues like school busing and abortion, and so forth. Those were difficult issues because at that time it was not as partisan, as unpleasant an environment, particularly in the Senate, as it is now. Senators and their staffs worked well across the aisle. Senator Weicker had a number of—

Leffler: It’s almost hard to believe that—

G. Baker: It’s true. Senator Weicker had good friends who were very conservative, both in his party and on the other side. Of course the Democrats then were not a liberal party so much as a southern party. And they still have many of the committee chairmen who were southern Democrats and they were very conservative across the board. I mean, they worked together as Democrats—

Leffler: Sure.

G. Baker: It was a very different party, and the Republican Party was more New England oriented or was more a moderate sort of party. Now they’ve all changed quite a bit. But moderate Republicans got along well with each other. Moderate Republicans also got along well with the northeastern and urban Democrats. They just agreed on a lot of issues. But socially they would see each other. They might have a very acrimonious debate one afternoon or evening, but on many occasions Senator Weicker would go out with his more conservative friends and opponents on many issues and have a drink afterwards.

I think that you can’t always say that the debates that you heard or you read about on the floor were entirely civil, but there was a certain level of civility in social relationships both at the staff level and the principal level in the Senate at that time. I constantly say that to my friends today, particularly those that are younger friends who may be working up on the Hill, because that was a very different time.

G. Baker: And here in 1975 a letter from Gerald Ford thanking me for my work on the Metric Conversion Act of 1975, which came to the Commerce Committee, and somehow I had been assigned to work on that. Anyway, so—

Leffler: So did working on these issues very intensively give you a level of expertise, which you then took with you after you left?

G. Baker: I wish I could say that, because I don’t think I’ve managed my career properly. Most of my friends with whom I worked up there, and now know, went on to either work in law firms or in other government agencies, and when I left the Hill, I went essentially to work for myself and with the others doing venture capital and private equity investing. I’ve been doing that for the last 30 years.

Leffler: What did you learn about leadership from working with Lowell Weicker and experiencing the work of others on the Hill?

G. Baker: Well, I think he was a strong leader and he inspired leadership. He could be prickly and difficult, but we all can. So he wasn’t perfect. I learned a lot from him about inspiring people to get things done, which is something I’ve always respected. I’m still involved in organizations and I work with people, and I’m still in a position where I’m trying to get things done. Relying on other people, so I think that’s essential.

Leffler: And how do you inspire people to get things done?

G. Baker: I try to convince them that it’s important to get it done. But I think that one of the things that I take, and I’m sure we all took from working as we did as young people in those settings, is that you really have to have teamwork to get anything done. You have to have consensus to get votes on the floor, and your Senators have to work together, but to get anything to the floor you need teamwork at a staff level. And that means you have to be willing to work across the aisle and you have to convince your opponents either not to oppose you as strongly or you have to neutralize them in some way.

It’s not all about winning hearts and minds. It’s very often strategic. But if you just take as an example trying to get one piece of legislation through from conception to passage, and funding, as you obviously know, is a very complicated process and it involves a lot of people. Whenever you’re involved with a lot of people you have the potential to be derailed at any turn. Most of what I work with involves investors, entrepreneurs, bankers, and so forth. I work with a lot of people even though I’m not part of a large organization, and what I learned from my experience on the Hill has been extremely valuable in terms of getting things done.

Leffler: Lowell Weicker certainly comes across as a pretty passionate person. Whatever he takes on, he’s very passionate, and I wonder if some of his leadership skills have to be able to transfer that passion down the line and make people really feel that what he believes in is worth believing in. And therefor they would work for—

G. Baker: Well, I think that’s a very good way of thinking of it. Whether or not that works for all of us, I don’t know. Yes, you’re projecting passion, but it has to be received in a way that you respect the principle behind the passion and everything else. So yes. If you can make a good strong case passionately, as he often did, I think, you have a good chance of garnering the kind of support you need and inspiring those around you to further the cause. I can say that to the extent I’ve been able to follow that model, I’ve been successful.

I give Senator Weicker a great deal of credit for allowing me to learn a lot about myself and about life at an early age in a difficult setting, and it’s meant a lot to me. Lifelong. Even though unlike many of the people that you talk to I didn’t have an experience that went beyond the first two terms in the Senate, and I haven’t been involved with him directly since then, only as a friend. My wife and I see him and Claudia on occasion. We like them both very much. But for me everything goes back to those early, early years.

Leffler: What do you think he’ll be most remembered for? Or should be most remembered for?

G. Baker: What’s interesting is that, as I say, I have a number of friends now that are my age. I’m a senior citizen who had experience on the Hill, and they know the difference between then and now. Whenever people ask me what was your experience on the Hill, and who did you work for and so forth and I say Lowell Weicker, he is extremely well regarded by people who have some experience at that time. I’m not sure how many young people know of him, but anyone who was in Washington or around Washington or had anything to do with Washington in the ’70s, for example, thinks very well of him.

Conservative Republicans today don’t consider him a Republican, and perhaps liberal Democrats don’t consider him liberal enough. So he’s still somewhere in the middle, but the reputation he has with the folks of my generation who have any experience with Washington is very high. I get credit for that, which is undeserved, but just being associated with him is rewarding.

Weicker’s accomplishments

Leffler: Well, just to clarify this a little bit. He has written or said in interviews that he thinks that environmental issues and the work that he did on oceanic research and conservation are among the most substantive accomplishments. That is his own self-reflection. I also know that [Joseph] Lieberman ran against him on environmental issues as one of the arguments that he brought. He said he could do a better job, but nonetheless, Senator Weicker on self-reflection feels that this is a very important part of his legacy. Others would say, like Bill Shure with whom I spoke yesterday, and so it’s on the record, that he surely thinks Watergate will be remembered as his most important legacy. Not necessarily substantively, but at least in terms of the public perception.

Maureen Byrnes, who worked so intensively on the AIDS [acquired immune deficiency syndrome] issues, and on issues relating to education and mental retardation, etc., feels very passionately and continues to work on those issues. So there are all these different little areas that he’s remembered for. It seems to me as his legislative assistant who saw so many of the issues run through his office, do you think you would have a good ability to step back and reflect on whether you think any of these are more central than others or should become a more central part of his legacy? Or would you just want to say all of them?

G. Baker: Well, to some extent, all of them for different reasons. I spent most of my time of the period I was up there on transportation issues. I think he had a great deal of impact. Now, as a lasting impact, will people remember him for that? Not necessarily. I’m satisfied that he did have a great deal of impact, but that’s not what I would say he’ll be remembered for.

We haven’t talked about Watergate that much, except that it was very exciting to be around him and the committee at that time. We felt we were the center of a lot of attention, but he really dug into that, and it was for Senator Weicker, in some ways, a formative experience in terms of establishing his character. Because from that time forward he wasn’t afraid to dig into things, particularly with issues of ethics and so forth, and good government. There was no question that he established his reputation with Watergate, and I think that that’s sort of the beginning. Oceans is an example—I mean he felt passionately about that and he was engaged in it. He got wet. So you know when you do that and you believe in it, it’s central to his life, and yes, I think he had a great deal of impact. Did he transform the world? I don’t know, but he certainly gave it the kind of attention that has kept it alive.

And generally on environmental issues I think he had a strong record, but I think you’d have to focus on oceans. If you ask me, it was more after my time, but his interest in medical research generally, that’s probably where he’s had the greatest impact. And he was on the Appropriations Committee and he really gave attention to the disabled issues. So if I had to give you one answer, it would be medical research.

Leffler: In part because he controlled the purse strings.

G. Baker: Yes. But he was in a position where he could make a difference, and he made a difference. He took on issues that hadn’t gotten a lot of attention, made sure there was funding, and frankly, at the time he was doing it, it was important for AIDS, and for many of the other health issues, and for the disabled in particular. I really believe in what he was doing, and I’m involved in some medical technology research now that is meaningful to me, but I think everything that he did set the stage for a lot of what’s going on now in that area. So I’d give him full marks for that. If anyone wants to argue that he’ll be remembered for Watergate, I would just say yes. But that allowed him to do many of the other things that he did do because he had greater prominence and credibility for those issues.

Leffler: That sounds like a very important thing to say. I think that sounds right to me. That it gave him the prominence, the place from which he could then go on and do other things. Thank you very much for doing this.

G. Baker: I hope it’s useful. I wish I had a sharper memory of everything, but it’s interesting to think back, and I just hope it’s useful to the project.

Leffler: It’s certainly useful getting as many perspectives on the tape as possible to get an understanding, as much as the process. You’ve certainly filled in some pieces that we didn’t have and that researchers will happy to have, without going through the rows and rows and rows of boxes to try to re-establish some things.

G. Baker: I think you can go back and research some of these other areas too through the Congressional Record and through just general news sources to get a sense of things.

Leffler: Thank you very much for your time.

G. Baker: You’re very welcome.
