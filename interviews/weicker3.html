0:00:00

Leffler: This is Phyllis Leffler. The date is May 10, 2010. I’m at the home of Lowell Weicker for the third of our interviews in the Lowell Weicker Oral History series, and today we’ve agreed to just continue our discussion of Watergate and finish that up as best we can and then move on to some of the other important issues that you were concerned with while you were in the Congress.

0:00:40

Weicker’s interactions with Haldeman and Ehrlichman at Watergate hearings

Leffler: So on Watergate, we had just finished talking about [Harry Robbins “H.R.”] Haldeman’s testimony and the reaction of Haldeman to you, the very strong signals he was sending. I think you addressed in our last discussion your own reactions to that and your understanding of how livid and angry he was with you. Do you remember any other moments like that with particular individuals that seemed to you to be very significant passages in the hearings themselves?

L. Weicker: No. I think that both my dialogues with Haldeman and [John] Ehrlichman were rather spirited. I think as spirited was the dialogue with Ehrlichman where he admitted he’d hired somebody to spy on the personal habits of United States Senators, and thus also inferring that that was a practice that went on all the time in politics, and I remember getting pretty irate over the fact. I said, “Look, I’ve run X number of campaigns and I’ve had X number of campaigns run against me. I don’t recall either spying on my opponents or them spying on me.” I think Ehrlichman’s flip remark, he said, “Well, you must be an angel,” and I remember my response to him was, “I’m no angel.” No question in my own mind, I had plenty of faults along all the lines that he projected in the course of a lifetime, as do most people.

I might add, one of the amusing things that came of that was a group of ladies in North Carolina went ahead and needlepointed a picture which sort of shows me as an angel and underneath it they said, “Yes, you is.” It had clippings from the transcript also as part of this montage. I think that is probably in the archives. If it’s not—

Leffler: Actually, I’ve seen Kent. He has a picture of it posted on the wall.

L. Weicker: That’s what came of that dialogue, so that was very spirited. Haldeman was of a different nature as he tried to go ahead and portray [Richard] Nixon’s political opponents. And by that, I don’t mean the opponents themselves, but rather the supporters of his opponents, as nut cakes and violent. He did that by the directives, both as to the violence he knew was coming with Nixon’s appearance somewhere in the South. Also Billy Graham, because he felt that really would be the icing on the cake as to how it would make these people appear, and he encouraged that. Well, I can tell you right now, for someone to incite violence in order to gain points for their candidate, I certainly had never heard of that in my life, and yet here it was being practiced in memoranda, in black and white, with instructions as to that happening.

I felt that was probably one of the lowest moments of the Nixon administration because it wasn’t just some kid on the campaign trail. You’re talking about the Chief of Staff to the President of the United States, you’re talking about the President, and you’re talking about a highly revered man like Billy Graham. These people were encouraging violence against him. I think it was bad enough they encouraged violence against the President, but to do that against Mr. Graham I thought was outrageous, and that’s why I was as tough as I was on Haldeman.

Haldeman was really I’d say an enigma in many ways. Number one, he lived in my backdoor and I never heard of him. He lived in Cos Cob, Connecticut, and in all my political life in Greenwich, which Cos Cob is part of, I had never heard of Bob Haldeman. In any event, as you know, he became Chief of Staff. He was the gatekeeper. Ehrlichman, I guess I can only gather he came from the West Coast, and maybe Haldeman originally came from there, but Haldeman actually lived, as I say, right next door to where we were and I never heard of him, and, quite frankly, I never heard of him in Washington until he became known as the Chief of Staff to Nixon.

I understand the job of Chief of Staff is a very tough one. You are the gatekeeper, but I look upon that as being the gatekeeper in terms of governmental policies and people. I don’t look upon it as being as a gatekeeper for the political antics of the President, and he certainly was that, as was Erlichman. They worked as a team.

So that’s why early on, as you well know, in the proceedings where I got upbraided by both Sam Ervin and Howard Baker, I suggested that even though I had no proof at that time, because this was very early in the proceedings, enough bad things had happened that I thought the Chief of Staff ought to resign. That was turned on me by Baker and Ervin, who thought I was too big for my britches—I probably was—for intimating that Haldeman knew of illegal activities.

I had no evidence of that early on. I did later on, but not early on. I merely said that a Chief of Staff certainly owes it to his boss to keep to a dull roar or keep to a minimum the number of unfortunate acts that are taking place within the White House. On that basis alone, I think if Haldeman had been working for me, I certainly would’ve canned him or he should’ve had the grace to relieve himself. That was the basis of it. Not anything illegal. By the time you get to these documents that we’re talking about here and also other events that transpired during Watergate, it was very clear. He was very much in on all the stuff that was either nasty, illegal, gross, or all three.

0:09:25

President Nixon and the IRS

Leffler: As part of the Watergate investigation—maybe it was not part of Watergate, but I think it was—you decide to explore President Nixon’s tax records from ’68 to ’72. Can you explain why that would be related to—

L. Weicker: Well, that actually came, I think, after the Senate had closed its hearings. It was on information received from John Dean and also subsequently by Pete Kinsey, who was on my staff, and without getting into all the details, what it had to do with basically was his donation of his papers—

Leffler: Right. To the National Archives.

L. Weicker: —to the Archives. And the taking of a tax deduction [laughter]. What it really boiled down to was Richard Nixon paying, I think, anywhere between $500 and $800 worth of federal taxes year after year. Well, for a man that used the IRS [Internal Revenue Service] to go ahead and embarrass his opponents, I don’t think there was anything more embarrassing than the revelation that these donations, which were illegal and for which he had claimed a deduction, ended up, for all intents and purposes, with him paying no taxes at all.

I mean, look. We all had problems with the IRS. I’ve had them. I’ve paid late, but boy, I had to pay a whopping fine and I paid a whopping amount of taxes. I wouldn’t fault him if he had been late on his taxes, or he could pay penalties on his taxes. That happens to everybody, but to pay no taxes—and you’re the President—on the basis of an illegal gift, wow, I think that’s wild. I really do. As I recall, it was either the Providence Journal or the St. Louis Post-Dispatch that got the story on that. I can’t remember which. One of those two newspapers I think we worked with in getting them the story.

Leffler: But you say in your book, Maverick, that you feel that that was essentially the nail in his political coffin.

L. Weicker: He already had a public that was pretty fed up. Maybe the public really wasn’t aware of the Constitution of the United States and the powers of the various players in the Constitution, but boy, I’ll tell you, one thing the public knows about is income taxes, and if they couldn’t somehow translate the betrayal of his oath of office to the Constitution, they sure as hell could understand him not paying taxes.

0:12:24

Weicker’s handling of campaign contributions

Leffler: I know you were cleared of all these charges, but there were people in the Nixon administration who also made claims against you, right? For false reports of improper campaigning.

L. Weicker: Sure. Absolutely. They claimed—you’ve got to understand the federal law as it was written at the time, and the federal law in campaign donations has been changed so many times it doesn’t even bear any resemblance to what it was, but basically you could not take cash, and by “you,” I mean the candidate himself or herself, from anybody. Nixon had his right-hand man, Jack Gleason, offer a campaign contribution to me, which was a contribution made both to the campaign and to me personally in terms of cash. The only problem was I never got the cash.

I was busy campaigning upstate, and so my chairman, Phil Drake, was the one that received the cash. Phil went ahead and duly put it into the campaign bank account, and because it was Connecticut, it was reported. In other words, Connecticut’s laws were stronger than the federal law, so not only did I not get it, but what was gotten was reported.

Now, I knew all this, and so when they started to throw this bullshit around, and they did from day one. When I went and had my secret meeting with John Dean, he mentioned this. A reporter for the Washington Star tried to make a story out of it in the middle of the hearings. Other reporters asked the question, but I knew we had done nothing wrong.

I figure I’m not going to put up with this anymore. “Why don’t you just throw it in the hands of the special prosecutor, put it in the hands of the law enforcement. Let them investigate whether anything was wrong.” I think it was actually after the hearings had concluded in a time after that that they did issue a report that, lo and behold, nothing wrong was done, but I figured that was the only way to handle it. I could sit there and protest until I was blue in the face. Probably nobody would believe me, any more than they’d believe any other politician. Let law enforcement handle it. Law enforcement handled it, and the conclusion was exactly as I had said it was.

Leffler: Do you feel like there was a direct connection between that and your role in Watergate? Did you feel like this contribution was purposefully manipulated in any way?

L. Weicker: No, no, because the contribution was given before I was even elected to the Senate.

Leffler: Okay. It went back to 1970.

L. Weicker: It was back to 1970, right.

Leffler: But the revealing of this, the claiming of it, came out much later.

L. Weicker: Sure. It came out, as I say, during the Watergate. And I might add, of course, to me the great story of all that isn’t so much me, because the contribution was given, the contribution was reported, the contribution was declared legal, so the only part of that story is the attempted blackmail. Call it anything you want to, but the real story is when Jack Gleason called me and told me that he was delivering these envelopes addressed to various United States Senators in which envelopes was the detail of the contributions made to their campaigns or to them personally by him, Jack Gleason. He ran what is known as the townhouse operation. He operated out of a townhouse, either in D.C. or Alexandria. I can’t remember which. In any event, the whole purpose of that was to tie the candidate or the Senator or the House member to the administration.

Anyway, Jack called and he said, “Look.” Because I’d called Jack earlier when I learned about this and I said, “Jack, if I’m not mistaken, I never saw you with the contribution.” He said, “Well, you didn’t, because I never gave you one.” So we established some sort of a personal rapport by that conversation. He called me and he said, “I am now delivering these envelopes addressed to various United States Senators and Representatives to George [H.W.] Bush, who was head of the RNC [Republican National Committee], and then I’m going to let him do what he wants to with it.” Which I suppose is perfectly proper protocol for Jack rather than go Senator by Senator.

I got a call from Bush. I didn’t trust him anyway, so I made damn sure—I didn’t have a speakerphone and I certainly didn’t have recording devices. But he called me and he said he’d received these documents and then he asked the question, “What should I do? Should I burn them?”

My response was, “For God’s sakes, George.” I could call him George because I knew him when he was in the House. He had one term in the House before he got beaten for the Senate. I said, “For God’s sakes, the Republican Party is not involved in this mess. The Committee to Re-elect the President is, and that’s not the RNC. You do that and it comes out, and all of a sudden you have managed to put the whole Republican Party into this pit called Watergate.” So he thanked me and hung up.

Now, I knew, I could just tell from the resonances, or whatever you want call it. I knew I was being taped. There was no question about that, absolutely no question about that in my mind at all. They would’ve loved to have me say on tape, “No, burn them.”

Leffler: So you think that was a setup?

L. Weicker: Oh sure I do. Oh yes. I can’t prove that, but do I think it was? Absolutely. Why would he turn to me and ask me for my advice as to what he should do with these 50-some odd envelopes with money in them? I can understand him calling me and saying, “I’ve got an envelope here for you, what do you want me to do with it?” No, no. It was the generic, the whole thing. I know I was being set up, and my answer that I gave was the answer I’d give, setup or not setup. It was true. It was really true.

I think one of the motivations, remember, that I have stated many times in the past is I felt not at all anti-Republican or anti-Nixon. I felt that the best help I could give the party was to do the best job so that people wouldn’t look upon it as Democrats doing their thing and Republicans doing theirs, that indeed whatever result was arrived at was arrived at by virtue of hard work on all sides, and I felt very keenly that my doing a good job was in a way protecting the Republican Party, since I was a Republican, so the same reasoning that I applied to myself got me in an uproar when Bush suggested as the Republican National Chairman you ought to burn these documents. We’d already been through burnings with Pat Gray and all the rest of it. I didn’t need any more of that stuff, and certainly not at my party headquarters.

0:21:58

The risk involved in accusing the President of the United States

Leffler: It sounds like one has to be awfully aware, constantly, about the efforts to undercut—especially, I suspect, once you’ve been found to—

L. Weicker: Once I got into the middle of this thing, I literally had to walk on eggshells. Again, I could check with Bill Shure, but I’m sure his name was [William] Dobrivir. It’s a strange name, but I’ve remembered it. He made some tax accusation or something on Nixon during the Watergate, which has proven false. He was made mincemeat of by Nixon’s people in the White House. That’s why I knew that anything that I said or did had to have merit. Otherwise, they’d make mincemeat of me.

The White House is an enormously powerful organization with all of its various arms of public relations and news and all that, and the President himself. My God, I mean you cross that and believe me, you’re going to get blown out of the park. So yes, whatever I said, whatever I wrote, whatever I did, believe me, was done very, very carefully to assure that I wasn’t going to enjoy the same fate as Mr. Dobrivir.

Leffler: Lots of landmines, it sounds like.

L. Weicker: Oh, yes, tremendous. I might, just out of curiosity—I’m going do something here. I know Bill Shure would be able to pick this one up for me.

Leffler: Are you going to check out this guy’s name?

L. Weicker: Yes, I just want to see what—

[Interruption for phone call]

Leffler: I don’t remember—

L. Weicker: He’s not in my book. No.

Leffler: I don’t remember coming across that name.

L. Weicker: No. All right. Bill Shure will know. Because boy, they really blew him right through the roof. I vetted everything and I made sure my staff vetted everything, because we knew early on that the Republicans on the Committee weren’t pleased with me, and the Democrats, and that included especially Sam Dash, didn’t want a Republican to get any credit for any work they were doing.

Now, fortunately for me, I had a very good relationship with the Democratic researchers or investigators, as did my staff. Terry Lenzner, who I’ve maintained a friendship with, Scott Armstrong, and there was one more, but I’ve maintained a very good relationship with them, so we got along perfectly well.

Leffler: So that’s what you mean when you say you vetted everything, that you needed to sort of work it through the Committee and the staffers?

L. Weicker: Yes, to make sure that I wasn’t going to get caught off base.

Leffler: So, last time we talked, we did talk about the implications of Watergate for your own career, and how early on this put you in a very, very public place and essentially in the spotlight. Do you have anything further that you want to say about the implications of your early involvement in Watergate for the record?

L. Weicker: Well, I was pretty miserable at the outset.

Leffler: In what way?

L. Weicker: Well, young freshman Senators did not attack Presidents of the United States, and certainly having just been elected from Connecticut, Republicans didn’t expect that kind of performance by me. Indeed, oh my God, I named her National Committeewoman. Martin Ransohoff was her son. He’s a Hollywood producer. Babette Ransohoff lived in Stamford. I had named her the National Committeewoman. She never forgave me for my performance on Watergate, and we were close friends. We were really close friends. She was a good gal, and yet she could never reconcile herself to the fact that Nixon was what he was, and I was responsible for his downfall. And that of all the people that I knew in Watergate, I would say was the one person I lost and never, never got back. It was too bad, because I truly liked her. She was a nice person.

Leffler: This has to have other implications.

L. Weicker: But to go from that point—at the beginning, believe me, more often than not, people flipped me the bird in the state of Connecticut. They didn’t believe me. They believed Nixon. And you can’t blame them. I don’t blame them. Now, admittedly, as it was all over and drawing to its conclusion, I was the state hero, in some people’s minds, a national hero, but that wasn’t the case when it started off. So that was the two-edged sword. Anything where you take that much of a gamble, you’re either going to be a dead man or you’re going to be a hero.

And because I did move slowly and went step by step, I never doubted the ground that I was on. I would not have hesitated. If I’d come across witnesses or incidents that were exculpatory in the case of Nixon, I’d have said so. I would not have hesitated, because I wasn’t after Richard Nixon. I was certainly after the kind of behavior that I thought he was involved with, and I’ve said many times, as you know, my conclusions, my separate views, were all written before the tapes came out. Before. And when the tapes came out, I don’t think we were wrong in anything, and I don’t think there was much of anything that I hadn’t already said. There’s where I was exonerated by an impartial jury in terms of what those tapes revealed, but it only gives you an idea as to how careful we had to be as we moved through this morass.

0:33:57

Weicker reflects on how taking unpopular stands affected his career

Leffler: But you bucked your own party in essence and certainly took on the President, who was a member of your own party. Do you see that as having ramifications throughout the rest of your career?

L. Weicker: Well, I think it does. It certainly didn’t at the time. Republicans had to sort of regroup after the Nixon debacle. Certainly I backed [Gerald] Jerry Ford, and I liked Jerry Ford. My credentials with the Republicans were pretty good. I think the problem with my career was I’d say that first of all, there was Richard Nixon, and then I had to deal with Ronald Reagan.

There’s nothing dishonest about Ronald Reagan except for the fact that he has an agenda that is way off from what I thought the agenda should be. All you have to do is see Reagan’s comments about me that have been released with his diaries to understand that I wasn’t exactly the most popular kid on the block in the White House. The same holds true of the conservative Republicans throughout Connecticut, especially due to the fact that the state chairman, a man by the name of Frederick Biebel, was angling for a job in Washington and was trying to link the Republican Party of the state of Connecticut with the National Republican Party, which I felt would be a total disaster because Connecticut was such a dyed-in-the-wool Democratic blue-collar state. So the hate for Lowell Weicker certainly went not just to the experience of Richard Nixon but also with Ronald Reagan and everything he tried to do, and my bucking him.

Add to that some of the social measures that I opposed—prayers in school, flag burning, court stripping, AIDS—all of these kinds of issues, and I had certainly a pretty hefty band of opponents out there waiting for me. In 1988 it all came to a head when William F. Buckley was able to sort of corral that whole group and suggest that they vote for Joe Lieberman, which they did, as witnessed the vote totals in the Gold Coast of Connecticut.

So I would say that the Watergate and Nixon proved to be a huge boon. It certainly is responsible for the landslide election I had in 1976, and I think it also carried into 1982. But after ’82, I think that Republicans said, “Hey, what the hell is this guy?”

Leffler: A whole new set of issues after ’82?

L. Weicker: Yes, new issues and sort of the bad taste from before.

Leffler: Let’s move on. Are you ready to do that? Is this a good time? Unless there are any final comments you want to make about Watergate?

0:37:48

Weicker’s view of what America learned from Watergate

L. Weicker: I read the book, the comments in there. Well, yes, one comment I think that I do want to make. I wish I could say that Watergate taught the country a lesson, but it hasn’t. It was splashy news with lots of celebrities involved, but the basic issues which were at the core of the scandal, i.e., the Constitution of the United States, the power of the President, the rights that we all have as Americans, that’s all been forgotten.

My God, all you have to do is take a look at the way the young George [W.] Bush acted as President of the United States. He makes Richard Nixon look like an altar boy. I would’ve been on the floor and nailed him a thousand times were I still in the Senate, but nobody’s going to do that, least of all the Democrats who should’ve, so the Watergate—it will be looked upon, I suppose, as a watershed when it comes to a President of the United States being held accountable.

In the past, really nobody knew much about the President, and nobody was of a mind to hold a President accountable. To that extent, you live in a new age. People don’t mind holding a President accountable, and a President has to tread lightly if he’s going to go ahead and disobey the law.

But I was hoping more would come of it than that. I was hoping that we really would clean up our campaign financing. I was hoping that there would be laws in place that would make this kind of activity more difficult, but I don’t see any of that truly having happened, and I think you could have another Watergate tomorrow. Well, you did, in effect. You had Reagan’s fiasco and then all the stuff with Bush that has taken place.

The American people have got to go ahead and maintain an active interest in their nation’s affairs, and it’s not enough to do it just when they get pissed off about some particular piece of legislation. They’ve got to be on the watch all the time. Maybe that’s too much to ask of everybody in a democracy, but I don’t think it’s too much to ask at least more than 50 percent to stay on duty, and that’s not even happening, so the Watergate to me was—I was vindicated as to that set of facts at that time, but do I think it’s had any lasting effect? No. And I think that’s too bad.

Leffler: That’s discouraging and disappointing.

L. Weicker: Yes, it is.

Leffler: And maybe that is really the opportunity to move into an area where you have invested a huge amount of energy and time and commitment. Another issue that’s very much in the news today is just the nature of the oceans and waterways in the country, because here we are looking at this massive oil spill and its effect on fisheries and the ocean and the ocean floors. It’s something you worked really tirelessly on to try to put into place a whole series of laws to try to improve—

L. Weicker: I have more hope here, ironically, than I do on our knowledge of the Constitution. Just to go back to Watergate for a moment. I cannot remember who I was interrogating. I think he came from the military, somebody that was associated with some branch of the military and I asked him, “When you train your officers and your soldiers, do they have to take a course in the Constitution of the United States?” And I think he answered in the affirmative, but I want to tell you, Phyllis, I think a course in our Constitution should be mandatory at every level of education, K-12, college, obviously graduate schools. It just ought to be pounded home, and if that happened I’d feel vindicated on Watergate because people would be very alert to their rights.

I mean the only damn rights you hear anybody advocate for now are gun rights. Jesus. I mean, thank you very much. I own my own shotguns, I go hunting, so I’m not some liberal nut, but do I think as a matter of common sense that there shouldn’t be any handguns in the United States. Damn right. Or any automatic weapons. You’re damn right. Long guns are an entirely different issue from concealed weapons and automatic weapons, and yet all I hear is people running around saying—I mean, can you imagine what they were advocating for in Washington, D.C.? Running around Washington, everybody should have a gun with them? This was this Tea Party stuff that just happened. My God, I mean—

Leffler: But I’m not sure reading the Constitution would help on that matter.

L. Weicker: Well, I think, but that’s what they say—“We’ve got the right under the Constitution.” I’d like them to understand what other rights and responsibilities are in that Constitution, never mind just their bloody guns. I know it’s very simple and seems benign, but it would be devastating.

If you took a poll on should there be prayers in school, I have no doubt 90 percent of people would say, “Yes, sure.” Well, doesn’t anybody understand that the separation of church and state has created a haven for every religion known to man, and every religion thrives in the United States of America because of that separation? It’s these things that people ought to understand. They do if you sit down one-on-one, they understand, but they have to understand these things if the country is to survive and not just selectively pick out some little portion of the Constitution which I think has been totally misread and interpreted. But that’s my opinion. That isn’t a fact. Just to pick out a piece that they’re happy with—but it’s the whole document that works in this country, the whole document.

0:45:27

Weicker’s interest in oceans

L. Weicker: So in any event, to get to the oceans. Yes, after Watergate, I really wanted to get involved in something positive, and as chance would have it, I received an invitation from a foundation, Perry’s operation—what was the name? It wasn’t the Farber Ranch; that was the Johnson family. Bob will know—to go down and see an underwater habitat in the Bahamas. Up to that point, I’d never scuba dived. I had done a little bit of snorkeling. I knew nothing really about marine science or the oceans, but it certainly was intriguing, I thought, and I really felt like I wanted to get my mind on something else.

I went down and I saw this thing, and the Senators and congressmen were told to stand on the bow of the boat and the water was clear and you could look down and you could see people and you could see this underwater habitat. I turned to the guy that was in charge of the project, a fellow named Bob Wicklund, and I said, “Mr. Wicklund, I’d like to see that operation firsthand.” He said, “Do you scuba dive?” I said, “No, but can I be taught?” He said, “Yes, but it’s going to take a day, and even that’s only a quickie course. You won’t be fully qualified.” I said, “Well, whatever it is, let’s get to work.”

So I spent that day and the better part, I guess, of the next day in the swimming pool learning how to use my air tanks and take my tanks off and just the breathing apparatus in my mouth, all the basic stuff they still do for scuba diving. At the end of the 36 hours or whatever it was, out we went to the site. I went down and I saw the habitat and the people working in it. I saw the marine life in the area, and from that moment I was just hooked on the fact that this is the aspect of the planet that needs a lot more vision and a lot more care.

Leffler: Would you say that was really your first engagement with that?

L. Weicker: Absolutely.

Leffler: Because Congress is quite involved in passing legislation from 1970 on. Congress passes in ’72 the Marine Protection, Research and Sanctuaries Act. It’s also referred to as the Oceans Dumping Act. They passed the Marine Mammal Protection Act, the Coastal Zone Management Act.

L. Weicker: A lot of that I was involved with.

Leffler: The Endangered Species Act.

L. Weicker: I was involved with that, but it was after Watergate.

Leffler: Because this is all happening while Watergate is going on, 1972, 1973. You go down to the hydrolab in 1975, so I’m just asking, really, in 1970, 1972, 1973, are these issues that concern you then? Not a whole lot?

L. Weicker: Not a whole lot. I was aware of what was going on, but I can’t say that I was really involved.

Leffler: So, it’s really your personal experience that triggers this understanding.

L. Weicker: Oh, there’s no question about that. Incidentally, I saw Bob Ballard on television last night or the night before in the Black Sea doing his work. [Weicker calls Bob Wicklund.] He’s invited me to join him on that.

R. Wicklund: [via telephone] N.C.W.

L. Weicker: There might have been some stands individually, but I know the things that overwhelmed me, in other words, as to their—

Leffler: And do you remember the—

L. Weicker: You’ve already done one thing nobody could do. I’m rereading my book. I’m going over the notes, in other words, that you’ve left me. I’m doing everything I can to bring myself up to speed, and I’ll be seeing a lot of my staff.

[Discussion of dates for future meetings]

Leffler: So what would you like to address on the 14th when we get together? What topic would you most like to talk about? We can talk about disabilities work. We can talk about larger issues of mental health and AIDS legislation. We can wrap all those together if you’d like to, but I’d like to figure out what we should focus on next time.

L. Weicker: Well, we may want to talk about the NIH.

Leffler: And AIDS?

L. Weicker: AIDS obviously falls in there.

Leffler: Okay. And what else falls in there from your perspective? I’d like to just go over some—

L. Weicker: And disabilities.

Leffler: Let’s focus on AIDS and disabilities, and I think you have some materials just focused on that itself. So I’ll go over that and try to be up to speed. I know you’re going to be busy, but at least—thank you very much.

L. Weicker: Thank you for this. I’m looking forward to it.

[End of interview]
