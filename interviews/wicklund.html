0:00:00

Leffler: My name is Phyllis Leffler and we are in the Harrison Small Library. Today is April 6, 2010, and I am here as the chief interviewer on the Lowell Weicker Oral History Project. I’m with Bob Wicklund today. So for the tape recorder can you tell us your official name?

R. Wicklund: It’s Robert I. Wicklund.

Leffler: Just as a little bit of background information, could you indicate something about what you did prior to joining the Lowell administration?

0:00:40

Wicklund’s background in oceans research

R. Wicklund: Sure. Way, way back in the early ’60s, right at 60, I was kind of knocking around. I didn’t have any formal education, so I was working for the Teamsters Union and got really tired of that and found out about a Marine lab opening up in my hometown. I went and I offered to sweep floors or whatever. And they hired me to do some menial tasks for a month.

Leffler: What was that hometown?

R. Wicklund: Highlands, New Jersey.

Leffler: Right.

R. Wicklund: Sandy Hook was where this marine lab was. At that time it was the Department of Interior and later became a NOAA [National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration] lab, when NOAA was formed in 1970. I worked there for eight years. I was taken under the wing of the director and learned how to become a scientist. Before I left there I wrote over 20 papers and so on.

I took over the job of co-founder and the job of directing and operating the hydrolab in Freeport, Bahamas. The hydrolab is an underwater laboratory that people can go, scientists particularly, can go down for a week at a time. And so anyway I ran that for five years. We put people down on the bottom of the ocean for a week at a time, and they would live down there and go through a big decompression at the end for 17 hours. We possibly put over 340 people through that process, which is the most in the world, ever, and still holds. That’s where I met Lowell.

Leffler: But let’s back up for a minute. You said you had no formal education for this except on-the-job training?

R. Wicklund: Right.

Leffler: That’s remarkable to me that you learned as much as you did without formal degrees in this. I’m jumping forward, but I even remember from some of what I read you said that [Fidel] Castro called you “El Scientifico,” or something like that.

R. Wicklund: Yes.

Leffler: So clearly you developed a huge amount of expertise by working there.

0:02:47

Weicker’s introduction to oceans research

R. Wicklund: Yes, well, when I started at the hydrolab, I was director of that and I ran all the science programs and so on. I did some of my own research, but mainly in support of scientific research. And that’s basically where I, as I said before, met Lowell.

Leffler: Right.

R. Wicklund: He came down, and I don’t know if you want me to go into the—

Leffler: Please tell that story. He actually tells it in his book, but I think it would be great to have.

R. Wicklund: Yes. Lowell was one of a number of congressmen that came down for special projects we had in Freeport, Bahamas, that involved this laboratory submarine. We had the Secretary of Commerce come down, Pete Peterson. We had three Members of the House of Representatives, and Lowell Weicker was the only Member of the Senate. Those that could dive we were ready to take out to show them the laboratory, and those that couldn’t we arranged to have some lessons. Lowell was one that had no diving experience.

I had this young kid working for me, and he came to me this one day and said, “Hey, Bob there’s this big guy up by the pool and he’s insisting that he’s going to go out on the boat diving with us this afternoon and he hasn’t been through the diving course.” So I said, “Well just go back and tell him that he can’t go [laughing] and that’s all there is to it.” The kid said to me, “Well, I tried that, but he wants to talk to the head guy, and that’s you.”

So anyway, I went up to him and he was sitting at the side of the pool with his feet in the water. I walked over to him and I said, “Sir, I’m Bob Wicklund. I understand that you want to go out diving with us, but you can’t do it,” and he turned around and looked at me. And it was at that point that I realized that I was standing and he was sitting, and I was looking straight in his eyes. And I go, “Whoa.”

He was looking right through me. I could tell that he was sizing me up, and you know, “Who’s this guy telling me—” So anyway, I said, “You know, you really don’t have the experience.” And so he said, “Well, I want to go. I don’t want to be sitting here, wasting my time on the beach. I came here to learn something.” So he says, “Let’s go out.” I said, “We can’t do it, but let’s do something different. You and I can go out, just the two of us, later, but we won’t go down all the way. We’ll just go down 15 feet. The water’s very clear. You’ll be able to see what’s going on down below.” So he agreed to it.

We went out, and he went in the water. He was very comfortable, and we swam around for a while and made no motion whatsoever. Finally we came up after 15 minutes or so, we came up to the surface and he starts screaming. I mean, like, “Son of a bitch, that’s the greatest thing I’ve ever seen!” He really startled me because I thought it was something wrong. You know, this is his first time diving. And he said I was thinking, Oh man, we’ve got a problem here. So anyway, we got back in the boat and he was real excited, you know. I was really impressed by how excited he was about the whole thing. And this basically was—and he’ll even say it himself—sort of the Genesis of his interest in a big way. He was always interested in the oceans, but this kind of put him over the edge, I think.

Leffler: Well, he says that in his book.

R. Wicklund: Yes, I guess he did.

Leffler: I was looking at it again this morning, but he basically talks about the importance of the oceans and his understanding of that. He really ties that to this experience. He said he was in his forties before he came to understand the significance of the oceans and the magnificence of them. He really ties it to this first dive experience.

R. Wicklund: I know. It was pretty amazing, and I learned a lot from him how important firsthand experiences are.

Leffler: He also says in his book about you, he says, “I later learned that Wicklund was one of the country’s top divers. When he came by, I sized him up quickly. An outdoors type, probably someone who didn’t have a great deal of respect for politicians. You learned to sense that.” So is that true, that at that point you wouldn’t have had respect?

R. Wicklund: Well, I would modify that and say it wasn’t that I didn’t have any respect for politicians. I didn’t have any understanding.

Leffler: Of course.

R. Wicklund: I was pretty much a babe in the woods. I didn’t know one politician from another. You ask me about legislation, I wouldn’t know a thing about it. And so to me, he was just another person. It wasn’t a lack of respect, but—

0:07:48

Wicklund joins Weicker’s staff as oceans adviser

Leffler: And was it about a year later that he asked you if you would join his staff?

R. Wicklund: Yes. The follow-up to that dive was that he and Bill Alexander, who was the Representative from Arkansas, and the deputy director from NOAA wanted to do a three-day dive in this underwater lab. It’s called a saturation dive. And we did, four of us. I was down with him as sort of a safety diver. And we spent three days on the bottom of the ocean. Those were the first Members of Congress, the two of them, to ever, and the only ones—Weicker’s made four now. He made three more after that later in St. Croix, and he’s the only Member of the Senate that’s ever been living underwater to this day.

Leffler: That’s remarkable. So he came down for that saturation dive, and then what happened?

R. Wicklund: Well then after that we had a contract with NOAA to do the work of science. And NOAA decides—

Leffler: You said we had a contract. Who is the “we”?

R. Wicklund: Oh, the project, the system itself, the hydrolab, and the project was the Perry Foundation. John Perry was an ex-newspaper man. He owned 35 newspapers in Florida and he sold most of those and he started a submarine company. In the meantime he built this lab that was off the coast of Florida for a little while. We tested it out. Finally built it and moved it over to the—so it was the Perry Foundation run by John Perry, and I worked for him.

Leffler: Okay. And then so how did you—

R. Wicklund: So then, NOAA was going to fund this other program from Germany called the Helgaland up in Massachusetts. It was a big project and they only had a little bit of money, so it was either us or that project, and they figured they were supporting us for four years, so they were going to support this other project.

I wrote a letter to Lowell and said, basically, “Can you save us?” He actually called me up. It was unbelievable. He called me up and said, “Listen, I don’t think I’m going to help you,” but he said, “I really decided that I’m going to get into the oceans in a big way, and I want you to come and work for me.” And it was like, aw. You know, either I thought in my mind or I believe I asked him, “Do I have to wear shoes?” [laughter] Because I hadn’t been wearing shoes very much for 45 years, you know. I guess I thought I’d try it for a year, and I moved to Washington and worked for him for seven-and-a-half years.

Leffler: So how old were you when you actually started that position?

R. Wicklund: With him?

Leffler: Yes.

R. Wicklund: About 40.

Leffler: You were about 40. You worked in Washington for seven years, and I know you’ve written a little bit about the challenge of making that switch. Could you talk a little bit about that in terms of what you observed initially about the process of doing legislative work or the process of working with others?

R. Wicklund: Well, as you can imagine, coming from the beaches of the Bahamas and the ocean to Capitol Hill was more than a shock. [laughs] I was really lost. Totally lost. I didn’t have any understanding of process, I had no understanding of how things worked, and he didn’t know what to do with me, actually. So he put me over in the Government Affairs Committee because there was an opening there. He didn’t know what to do with me at first. He knew I was going to be working on ocean issues, but we didn’t get that settled up right away. I found the whole process amazing. The amount of energy was incredible. Mostly young people, lawyers, economists, and so on. I felt very much out of place being from where I came from.

Leffler: Did you ever consider not staying?

R. Wicklund: Oh, any number of times. As, you know, time would go on, especially in the beginning when I didn’t feel I was accomplishing very much. I’m not a city person, so I actually moved 70 miles out of Washington just to be out in the country.

Leffler: Oh, you commuted every day?

R. Wicklund: Yes, every day.

Leffler: 70 miles.

R. Wicklund: 70 miles. It was crazy.

Leffler: How? By train, by car?

R. Wicklund: Car.

Leffler: By car. My goodness.

R. Wicklund: Because when you work for Congress, you never know what time you’re going to get home.

Leffler: Of course.

R. Wicklund: Trains were a possibility, but usually it didn’t work very well. But as time went on I started to see particularly how much could be accomplished. I didn’t really realize in the beginning that even though you work for the Senator, you represent the Senator, you have a lot of leeway. He has to trust you to provide him with information, with direction, on legislation and so on. But you have to understand completely what his philosophy is. And during that time, that’s what I was mainly learning, which was very important.

Leffler: So could you talk a little bit about the evolution of that process, just to get the dates on the table? You started in 1976.

R. Wicklund: Yes.

Leffler: And you went until 1983. Is that right?

R. Wicklund: ’82.

Leffler: Okay. I guess what I’d really like you to talk about is what the process was like of working with Lowell Weicker. Clearly, the two of you had to develop a rapport and a relationship. Did that change over time?

Wicklund: Well, it changed immediately because before I came to work for him I was sort of a friend. We became friends, and it was on that level, and as soon as I moved in the office and I was in the presence of everybody else and Lowell, I realized that he was a different animal. He loomed huge at that point, and I saw that right way, that we had a different relationship, still on the friendly basis, however. I worked for him and I better make sure that I did the right thing. Friend or no friend. You had to be very careful and make sure that what you’re doing is on his behalf and on behalf of the country and everything else, but you have to be very cautious of that.

Leffler: Right, and so how did your own role change over time?

R. Wicklund: I’m sorry?

Leffler: How did your own role change over time?

R. Wicklund: As time went on, Lowell’s seniority was increasing, so his level within the Senate was becoming more stable—well, not stable, but he was able to accomplish more. One day he came to me—this is where a big, big change had come in. He came to me—well, back it up a little bit. He was on the Commerce Committee, which is an authorizing committee that had jurisdiction over oceans, but just the authorizing level. He had the opportunity to get on Appropriations, and I had no understanding of how important being on Appropriations was. That’s where all the money is.

I had a private meeting with him and I said, “Senator, please don’t get off the Commerce Committee. You’ve got to stay on. It’s really important.” And he was saying, “You just don’t know what you’re talking about.” So anyway, he did what he was going to do, and he got on the Appropriations Committee. Suddenly I realized that he had his hands on the funding for all these different agencies. Particularly, he had gotten onto the—at that time it was called the Commerce, Justice, State, and Judiciary Subcommittee of Appropriations, which had jurisdiction over NOAA, over the Marine Mammal Commission. As far as oceans are concerned that was like gold. When I got it through my thick head that this is where it’s at, that made a big change, and then I became his chief staffer specifically for ocean issues.

When it came to each year doing appropriations for NOAA, I had a lot to say. I talked to NOAA and relayed a lot of information back to Lowell. It was all his decisions. I worked on all this stuff. I made a lot of recommendations and so on, but in the end it was Lowell Weicker’s decision on what was to be done. It was at that point, when he was still only ranking—no, I’m sorry. He was on the Subcommittee and then he became ranking because the Democrats—

Leffler: —change in the administration.

R. Wicklund: Then later on when [Ronald] Reagan was elected, Lowell became chairman of that Subcommittee. All-powerful, and I was right in the middle of it. And for a short period of time there, it was pretty incredible some of the things we could do. We basically saved the Sea Grant program, and that was like a $57 million a year program.

Leffler: Could you explain what that program is?

R. Wicklund: Yes. Sea Grant is a NOAA-run or funded program that has offices in 30 different coastal states. Each office gets a certain amount of money from NOAA, $57 million at a time, spread out over the 30. They give grants out for ocean research mostly to universities and so on. Basically it’s one of the big programs that universities are involved with as far as doing ocean research under NOAA funding.

Leffler: So you were the chief staff person on matters relating to oceans. But if I’m reading the documentation correctly, it seems that there are multiple other issues and organizations that sort of come together. So there’s the work of the Environmental Protection Agency—protection of oceans has to do not just with protections of sea life but also the spillover into issues related to oil and ultimately Commerce, etc. So could you talk a little bit about that in terms of the interactive processes and how that worked in Lowell Weicker’s office.

0:19:50

Offshore drilling and the fishing industry

R. Wicklund: Yes. One of the big issues at the time when I was working there in 1978 was offshore drilling, as you mentioned, and the OCS, Offshore Continental Shelf Act of 1978. Lowell, I believe, was on the Energy Committee at the time. This was an authorizing committee, so it was a big bill and set a lot of rules for later on and how we deal with offshore drilling. He was a big member of that committee that got that bill through, and specifically he had a number of amendments. One was the fisherman’s contingency fund, which still exists. Every year NOAA provides money for that to provide damages to fishermen that ruined their equipment.

That was a time when fishing was becoming bigger. We were transferring all the foreign fishing over to domestic fishing and our domestic fishing fleet became very, very big and very efficient. We were at a crossroads at that time for whether we protect the fisherman or protect the fish because it was getting to that point where fishing was becoming so efficient that we were starting to clean them out. The foreigners were doing it first, and then we started doing it.

Leffler: Yes.

R. Wicklund: But Lowell was a big advocate of the fisherman. The Fisherman Contingency Fund was one way of helping them out. They were ruining gear on stuff that was dumped overboard. A lot of the equipment that the drillers use, they just dump it overboard, and the fishermen catch their nets on them. That was one thing. The other— he’s got the only legislation that up to this point provides for diving safety, and that’s in that bill as well. Those are two things that kind of stand out in my mind, but there were a lot of other things in there, fees and all of that, that I can’t quite remember.

Leffler: Sure. But in terms of this, you might say, overlapping process of creating legislation, where one set of issues sort of competes with another, were there specific people that you remember who were particularly important that worked in comparable areas or competing areas that made your work over time either easier or more difficult?

R. Wicklund: Well, maybe. I think I sent you a little piece on the deep seabed mining issue. That might be one we can talk about a little bit because that involved different people. The Deep Seabed Mining bill was being run through the Commerce Committee, which was run by the Republicans at that time. I think that was during the Reagan administration. Anyway, Lowell wanted to have some protection, meaning that if there was going to be deep seabed mining, where they drag along the bottom in the Pacific to pick up these manganese nodules, that there be some area set aside called stable reference areas that would not be touched. It would be left alone so that we could use it for research and as a protected area, because even though it was in deep, deep ocean, it’s still, ecologically, a very important place in the world.

He agreed that that was something we should do, so that’s something I worked on, and we had a lot of support from a number of people. The chairman of the Committee didn’t care one way or the other about it, but the Republican staff were really up in arms over it. And, you know, we were Republicans too. They used to come to me and threaten and say, “We’re going to cut this thing out because the mining industry is totally against it. I guess the lobbyists were up there in force.

I talked to Weicker any number of times, and you know, the one great thing about Lowell Weicker was that if he trusted you, as they talk about it in horseback riding, he’d give you your head. In other words, you can go ahead as long as you know what you’re doing. And so I had a lot of leeway on that one. The staffers came to me one day and said they were going to cut it out, and I said, “If you do, then Lowell’s going to stop the bill completely.” And then they said, “You’re bluffing. He would never do that.” And I said, “Yes he will.” He said, “Well, we’re going to call.” I said, “Go ahead.” Lowell wasn’t even in town at the time.

Leffler: And you had no idea, probably—

R. Wicklund: Yes. I was like, “Oh man, should I have done this before talking to him?” I knew that I was in the right there, but I was a little nervous about the whole thing. But I took a chance on it anyway. It was only a job, you know, and so they backed off.

Leffler: Yes. I noticed in I think both what you gave me in writing and what Weicker says in his book that Secretary James Watt was the real thorn in your side.

R. Wicklund: Oh, yes. He was terrible. Oh, he was terrible.

Leffler: Well, he was Secretary of the Interior, so why was he such a thorn?

R. Wicklund: Because he was a real advocate of offshore drilling. He didn’t care about research. We had a bill, the Georges Bank Protection Act, that we were pushing. Lowell wrote it, we were trying to get it through, and Watt just saw that as a way of stopping drilling. Basically what we wanted to do was—that protection act was to do research on Georges Bank, which was a big fishing area, to see what kind of a balance we could get from that research. He wouldn’t go for that at all, James Watt, and he just made all kinds of trouble for us.

We got into a special meeting that Weicker invited me to come to because I had all the background information. The funny thing about it is Watt got really belligerent and nasty to me personally. Lowell just sat back and watched and I could see his smirk on his face. He thought it was pretty funny, and I so I figured, Well, he’s not stopping me. I had this argument with the Secretary of Interior and it didn’t do any good one way or the other.

Leffler: He wasn’t going to change his mind.

R. Wicklund: No.

Leffler: Wouldn’t offer any protection for the environment, basically.

R. Wicklund: Exactly.

0:27:08

NOAA’s complex mission

Leffler: Well, on that matter, I actually wanted to ask a very specific question about NOAA. I’m wondering if you see any inherent conflict in NOAA’s mission. If I’m correct in how I read the documentation about NOAA’s mission, it’s both environmental protection and research as well as stimulating economic development. I know that there was some effort to get—in 1983 there was a bill proposed by Weicker to make NOAA independent of Commerce. I’m wondering if that was an issue in terms of your own work in any way, in terms of its inherent—

R. Wicklund: Absolutely. Yes, NOAA was a strange agency in a lot of ways because, as you said, it did research, it was responsible for protection, but on the other hand it was an advocate of the fishing industry, for instance, and some of these other things. They conflicted all the time, and environmentalists are never very happy with the way NOAA—the National Marine Fishing Service, which was a part of NOAA—they are constantly being bombarded by the environmentalists because the National Marine Fishing Service supposedly protects our fish stocks, but by giving in, because of politics, they’re allowing these fish stocks to be over fished. Right now our fisheries are in terrible, terrible shape, and that exists today. That whole issue is just as real today.

Leffler: Really, very real—

R. Wicklund: I talked to a Congressional staffer just about two months ago, and they’re going to introduce a bill they’re getting—because they’re so disgusted with NOAA—to try to get them out of Commerce. Whether it’s going to be independent, or maybe under the Department of Interior would be better. But independent is probably the best. It’s been an issue for a long time.

Leffler: In his book, Weicker claims to, in his words, change the focus of NOAA while he was chair of the Small Business Committee. And so I’m wondering, was this largely his initiative? Or this was clearly after you were hired, so was that something you brought to him?

R. Wicklund: Well, when it comes to that, he had his own thoughts about the way NOAA was operating. And of course I assisted him in getting information to him or making advice on certain areas you can go. But one of the things he saw was that NOAA was slowly—even though there was a real push to get NOAA to be more of an ocean agency because the ocean and atmospheric—most of the big money, especially back then, was for the Weather Service, and he wanted to get that more balanced.

Leffler: To go towards oceans?

R. Wicklund: And so that was what he was talking about. It was focusing more on ocean issues, and he did a lot by putting more money through Appropriations into ocean programs, into whaling and so on.

Leffler: Yes, ok. And for you having previously worked within NOAA, although not necessarily for them, but within NOAA, were you able to maintain your personal and professional relationships with the agency after you went to Washington, and how did that affect your role in the legislature?

R. Wicklund: Oh in a big way, and even more so when I got into Washington. I established a lot of friendships. We would go out drinking together with NOAA and people. And a lot of the legislation that we did and a lot of the things that I learned about was through these people that we had these backdoor meetings with that actually worked in fairly high levels of NOAA. But they were not allowed to be with me unless they had somebody to accompany them from legislative affairs in NOAA. But we were friends. Like I said, we would go out and party together and we worked together a lot. So a lot of the things that we did, we had the help of NOAA.

Leffler: So that was pretty smart of the Senator to have brought you on, given the fact that you had all these contacts and relationships.

R. Wicklund: Yes, well, my contacts were fairly good before I came, but it was after. Because I had a feeling for NOAA and the people there. In the end a lot of people that I dealt with were sort of the middle-level bureaucrats. There were guys that ran the program and just normal people. They wanted to see things work right, politics aside.

Leffler: Right. In your seven years there, was there a particular time that you would say was much more focused on the issues that you were brought in to deal with, or were the Senator’s interests consistent over time?

R. Wicklund: Well, his interests were consistent over time, but I’ll have to say that it was during that time when we were involved with the appropriations process, from—and I can’t remember exactly when he got on it—but it was sort of the last half of my time there at least that was the most productive, particularly when he became chairman. Now of course back then, which was totally different than it is now, we were able to work across the aisle very, very easily. Senator [Ernest “Fritz”] Hollings from South Carolina was the chairman of the Subcommittee that had jurisdiction over oceans, and Lowell was ranking. His staff and myself and our staff—Tim Keeney was another one that worked on these issues from Lowell’s staff, and Claudia [Weicker], she was a big staffer on that Subcommittee. So Claudia, Tim Keeney, and myself, we all worked on these things.

Leffler: Were there other staffers that we ought to, that you remember or—

R. Wicklund: Yes, Al Stayman came as a Sea Grant intern. He came right from college. Sea Grant gave his stipend to work a year with Congress. Al came over and worked with me for a year, and later took my job when I left. Al, right now, works for the Energy and Natural Resources Committee for the Democrats. He’s there and he’s still very active in all of this. And he was someone. Tim Keeney.

Leffler: Did Geoff Baker work with you on these issues?

R. Wicklund: Geoff was the chief of staff.

Leffler: Yes.

R. Wicklund: We always worked with him, but he had so many other issues that he dealt with, you know.

Leffler: Right.

R. Wicklund: Kim Elliott as well.

Leffler: Right. I saw her name on some of the documentation.

R. Wicklund: Yes, these were people who were right-hand people for the Senator. I was pretty lucky. I was a little different. He wanted me to stay on ocean issues, and some other environmental things as well. Other staffs, sometimes they had a dozen different issues that they had to deal with. So from my standpoint it was pretty fortunate.

Leffler: And were there other congressional offices that worked closely with you on these issues?

R. Wicklund: Oh, well, definitely Senator Hollings’ office.

Leffler: Right.

R. Wicklund: And then over on the House side at that time, it doesn’t exist anymore, but the Merchant Marine Fisheries Committee. I worked with a lot of those people over there.

Leffler: Why does that not exist anymore, do you know?

R. Wicklund: I don’t know. Congress got rid of it. That was blow, I think. On the Senate side, the Commerce Committee, we worked with them a lot, and the different Appropriations committees.

Leffler: Right. How did your work change on the basis of who was in the presidency?

R. Wicklund: Well, certainly when Ronald Reagan took over there were a lot of changes. And of course, the big change for us was the fact that we were now in the majority, and Weicker became the chairman of that Subcommittee. The first order of business was that Lowell Weicker actually selected the head of NOAA for the next four years.

Leffler: It might have been after your time, but I noticed in the correspondence that, is it 1985 when there’s a claim that there’s a big leadership problem in NOAA? Do you know about that?

R. Wicklund: Well, ’85? I’m trying to think of who was in there. Yes.

Leffler: Let me see if I can—yes. So it’s actually from you, this memorandum. Maybe I can just show it to you, to look at. It comes out of special collections.

R. Wicklund: Yes, the process was what motivated them. It wasn’t a passion.

Leffler: So was it the process of winning that motivated them?

R. Wicklund: Yes, the process of winning.

Leffler: So it wasn’t a particular issue that they—

R. Wicklund: No. I talked to a lot of these guys. Particularly the type of mind of a lawyer, for instance, an attorney, is get the job done and win it. But it doesn’t matter whether—they can go from saving the monk seal to offshore drilling all in the same breath.

Leffler: I wonder if a lot of that has to do with the way in which people come into those jobs. I mean, you came into your job having a very specific expertise, and were tapped for it. Not something you sought, you didn’t move up really through the ranks. You brought your expertise in with you and that was your focus. And even you say that at the beginning Weicker didn’t know what to do with you.

R. Wicklund: Right, yes.

Leffler: But I think especially young people who join Congress, they’re doing it for the rush of being involved with people of power and many other reasons too, but perhaps not with a particular focal issue that really interests them.

R. Wicklund: Right. I’ve known a couple of young guys that right out of law school they came to work for Congress because they felt that it would be good for their careers. Well, it turns out that in both cases they wanted to go back to private practice and their year or two in Congress was of no help to them whatsoever. They just had to go back and learn all over. They thought that would be a big step and they’d go back with more experience, but they found out that going back, they’d have to start the bottom level.

Leffler: Yes, but they also had a lot of contacts when they went back.

R. Wicklund: Well. Yes, that’s right, exactly.

Leffler: So you know it may not have helped in terms of where they joined the firm—

R. Wicklund: Right, but later on—

Leffler: Yes, sure. And all those lobbyists that they met who had money to bring, I’m sure it helps in that sense.

R. Wicklund: Yes. Exactly.

Leffler: Well, I don’t have anything else very specific to ask you at this point. As I do other interviews in this project there may be some follow-up that I’ll want to do, and I hope you’ll be open to that.

R. Wicklund: Yes, oh yes. I’d love to. One thing I have to say is, based on my background, the fact that I didn’t have any formal education and I did fairly well in marine science with this lab—but then when I met Lowell, and he hired me and I got that experience in D.C., that experience has been the underpinning of my career ever since.

Leffler: I can imagine.

R. Wicklund: I started this project down on a small island in the Bahamas. This was after I left Weicker’s office. I started it down there and I got a big, big program. Marine science, we had ships and submarines and everything else, and the reason that it worked is because I knew how to find funding.

Leffler: Of course. Yes.

R. Wicklund: I know what to do.

Leffler: Well, it sounds like you have had really a fascinating set of life experiences that are remarkably different and all incredibly meaningful. I mean, how many people can say that in the course of their lives?

R. Wicklund: Yes. Well, it’s been fun.

Leffler: Yes, I’ll bet. So anyway, I really want to thank you very much for doing this.

R. Wicklund: Oh, you’re welcome. I hope it’s helpful.

Leffler: I think getting the different perspectives of different people who worked with Lowell Weicker on a variety of issues ultimately will add enormously to this project. And, as I said, it may be necessary for us to figure out some time later on when we might be able to meet again. I’ll be happy to make the trip to you, if that’s easier for you. But as we hear from others, there may be things that I’d like to pick up that others are saying about other issues.

R. Wicklund: Sure. Yes, call me anytime or email me anytime. I’m usually in my office all the time. Pretty much.

Leffler: Right, okay. Thank you very much.

R. Wicklund: Okay. You’re welcome.

[End of interview]
